Ngā Mahi - Lesson Activities (75 minutes)

1. Society Component Stations - Essential Elements Investigation (25 mins)

Setup: Students rotate through 4 stations exploring the fundamental components every society needs. Each station focuses on a different essential element, with hands-on investigations and real-world examples.

Station 1: People & Social Organization

Investigation Focus:
  • How do people organize into groups (families, communities, nations)?
  • What roles do different people play in society?
  • How do age, skills, and relationships affect social position?
Activities:
  • Map your own social networks (family, school, friends, community)
  • Compare how different cultures organize social roles
  • Analyze population data: Who makes up our community?
Key Question: What would happen if a society had no way to organize people or assign roles?

Station 2: Rules & Governance Systems

Investigation Focus:
  • Why do all societies have rules and laws?
  • Who gets to make decisions that affect everyone?
  • How are rules enforced and conflicts resolved?
Activities:
  • Compare school rules, family rules, and national laws
  • Design rules for a new community from scratch
  • Investigate traditional Māori justice systems vs modern courts
Key Question: How do societies balance individual freedom with collective order?

Station 3: Resources & Economic Systems

Investigation Focus:
  • What does every society need to survive and thrive?
  • How are resources gathered, produced, and distributed?
  • What happens when resources are scarce or abundant?
Activities:
  • Map the resource flows in our school community
  • Compare different economic systems (sharing, trading, markets)
  • Design a resource distribution system for 100 people on an island
Key Question: How do societies decide who gets what, and is it fair?

Station 4: Culture, Values & Identity

Investigation Focus:
  • What beliefs and values hold societies together?
  • How do traditions, language, and customs shape identity?
  • What happens when different cultures meet?
Activities:
  • Explore cultural values in our class (individualism vs collectivism)
  • Compare celebration traditions across different cultures
  • Investigate how Te Ao Māori values influence NZ society
Key Question: What happens to a society that loses its cultural identity?

2. Society Systems Map - Connecting the Components (20 mins)

Collaborative Mapping: Students create visual maps showing how the four society components connect and influence each other.

Systems Mapping Template Instructions:

Step 1: Place Components
  • Draw four circles: People, Rules, Resources, Culture
  • Add specific examples in each circle from your station work
  • Use different colors for each component type
Step 2: Draw Connections
  • Draw arrows showing how components influence each other
  • Label connections with specific examples
  • Use thick arrows for strong influences, thin for weak

Systems Thinking Insight:

When you change one component of a society, it affects ALL the other components. This is why social change is complex!

3. Island Society Challenge - Applying Systems Knowledge (25 mins)

Scenario: 100 people from diverse backgrounds are stranded on a large island with abundant resources. They need to create a functioning society to survive and thrive long-term.

Your Challenge:

Working in groups of 4-5, design the basic structure for this new society. You must address all four components and show how they connect.

People Questions:
  • How will you organize into groups?
  • What roles do people need to fill?
  • How will you handle conflicts?
Rules Questions:
  • Who makes important decisions?
  • What rules are absolutely essential?
  • How will you enforce agreements?
Resource Questions:
  • How will work be organized?
  • How will goods be distributed?
  • What if someone can't contribute?
Culture Questions:
  • What values will guide decisions?
  • How will you celebrate diversity?
  • What traditions will you create?

Presentation Format:

Each group creates a visual poster showing their society design and presents their key decisions to the class. Focus on how your four components work together as a system.

4. Gallery Walk & Systems Reflection (5 mins)

Comparative Analysis: Students examine all group solutions and identify patterns in how societies address universal needs.

Reflection Questions:

  • What solutions appeared in multiple group designs? Why might these be universal?
  • Where did groups make completely different choices? What does this tell us about values?
  • Which society components seemed most challenging to design? Why?
  • How did groups handle the tension between individual needs and group needs?

Te Ao Māori Connection:

Compare your island society designs with traditional Māori social organization. How did iwi and hapū balance individual needs with collective wellbeing? What can we learn from indigenous approaches to community organization?

Cross-Curricular Applications

History

Analyze how ancient civilizations organized their societies and compare with modern approaches

Geography

Investigate how environment and resources shape society development

Mathematics

Use statistics to analyze population data and resource distribution patterns

English

Research and present on different cultural approaches to social organization

Aromatawai - Assessment

Formative Assessment

  • Station Investigations: Quality of observations and questions asked
  • Systems Mapping: Accuracy of connections identified between components
  • Island Society Design: Demonstrates understanding of all four components
  • Reflection Responses: Shows systems thinking development

Success Criteria

  • Beginning: Identifies society components but struggles to connect them
  • Developing: Shows how some components connect and influence each other
  • Achieving: Demonstrates clear understanding of society as interconnected system
  • Excelling: Applies systems thinking to predict consequences of changes

Whakamutunga - Lesson Reflection

Today we discovered that every society, no matter how different it appears on the surface, must address the same fundamental challenges. Understanding society as a system helps us see why changes in one area (like new technology or economic shifts) affect everything else. This systems thinking will help us analyze government systems, understand social issues, and eventually design our own ideal society.

"He waka eke noa - We are all in this together"

Every society is like a waka where everyone's contributions matter for the collective journey.
