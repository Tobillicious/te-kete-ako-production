Model "Connecting to Whaiara" Paragraph:

"The protest movements of the 1970s powerfully demonstrated the value of Whaiara (Rising Up). When MÄori activists saw injustice - land loss, language decline, systemic discrimination - they chose to rise up rather than accept the status quo. Groups like NgÄ Tamatoa used marches, petitions, and occupations to demand change. While not all protests achieved immediate results, they were effective in raising public awareness and eventually leading to the Waitangi Tribunal and the MÄori Language Act. Protest can be effective when it's organized, persistent, and focused on clear goals. However, it requires courage and often involves personal sacrifice, as many activists faced arrest and social criticism. The effectiveness of protest depends on whether those in power are willing to listen and change."

Time Activity Description & Teacher Guidance
0-5 min DO NOW
Whakawhanaungatanga		 Greet students. DO NOW: "When you see something unfair, do you: (a) stay silent, (b) speak up, or (c) take action? Explain your choice." Students write 2-3 sentences.
5-15 min Explicit Teaching
Direct Instruction		 Introduce WALT and Success Criteria. Use "Do Now" to introduce the concept of activism. Explain that by the 1970s, urban MÄori (from Lesson 1.2) faced ongoing discrimination, poverty, and loss of cultural identity. Show images from protests. Introduce NgÄ Tamatoa: founded at Auckland University in 1970, led by young MÄori demanding change. Explain Dr. Walker was both participant and historian of this movement - his column "Years of Anger" documented this era.
15-40 min Guided Practice
Student Activity		 Distribute "NgÄ Tamatoa: The Young Warriors" handout. Students read about the group's formation, goals, and actions. In small groups (3-4), students complete a graphic organizer with three sections: 1) Grievances (What were they angry about?), 2) Strategies (How did they protest?), 3) Goals (What did they want to achieve?). Groups share findings with class. Teacher facilitates discussion on effectiveness of different strategies.
40-60 min Independent Practice
Application		 Connecting to Whaiara: Students write a paragraph answering: "How did the protest movements of the 1970s demonstrate the value of Whaiara (Rising Up)? Do you think protest is an effective way to create change?" Show WAGOLL paragraph before they begin. Encourage students to use specific examples from the lesson and to consider both successes and limitations of the protests.
60-75 min Plenary
Wrap-up		 Review WALT and Success Criteria. Class discussion: "Dr. Walker wrote that the 1970s were 'Years of Anger.' Was this anger justified? Was it productive?" Exit ticket: "What is one question you have about the MÄori protest movements that you want to explore further?"

ğŸ§‘â€ğŸ« Kaiako Notes & Reflections

Culturally Responsive Pedagogy:

  • Critical: This lesson deals with protest, anger, and conflict. Some students may have whÄnau who were activists; others may have whÄnau with different perspectives. Create a respectful space for diverse viewpoints
  • Framing: Present activism as a response to genuine injustice, not as "troublemaking." Emphasize the courage and commitment of protesters
  • Student Voice: If students have family stories about this era, invite them to share (but don't pressure anyone)
  • Balance: Acknowledge that protest was controversial and not universally supported, even within MÄori communities. Teach students to analyze multiple perspectives
  • Connection to Present: Help students see the legacy of 1970s activism in contemporary MÄori movements (e.g., te reo MÄori revitalization, Treaty settlements)

Connection to Unit Theme:

This lesson is pivotal: it shows how Dr. Walker moved from observing history to participating in it. His "Years of Anger" column gave voice to the protest generation and challenged the dominant narrative that MÄori should be grateful and passive.

Post-Lesson Reflection:

  • Did students understand why MÄori were angry in the 1970s?
  • Were they able to analyze protest strategies critically (not just accept or reject them wholesale)?
  • How did students respond to the concept of "justified anger"?
  • Did the exit tickets reveal genuine curiosity about the protest movements?
  • Were any students uncomfortable with the content? How can I support them?

Teacher Preparation Note:

Consider inviting a guest speaker who participated in 1970s activism (or showing a video interview) to make this history personal and real for students. Ensure cultural protocols are followed for any guest visitors.

Historical Context:

The 1970s protests led directly to the establishment of the Waitangi Tribunal (Lesson 1.4) and broader recognition of MÄori rights. Help students see this lesson as part of a larger story of struggle and change.
