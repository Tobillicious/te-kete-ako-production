Debate Skills with Māori Oratory Traditions

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Understand key elements of traditional whaikōrero (Māori oratory)
  • Compare and contrast whaikōrero with contemporary debate structures
  • Apply rhetorical techniques from both traditions in formal debates
  • Develop confidence in public speaking and argument construction
  • Demonstrate cultural awareness when engaging with Māori protocols

NZ Curriculum Links: English L5-8: Speaking, Writing, Presenting; Social Sciences L5-8: Identity, Culture and Organisation

Cultural Context & Safety Notes

Whaikōrero is a sacred Māori tradition of formal speech-making, typically performed by men on marae. While we adapt some elements for classroom use, we must respect its cultural significance:

  • Traditionally, whaikōrero is performed by men, but in educational contexts, all students may participate
  • Always acknowledge the iwi/tribe when referencing specific whaikōrero examples
  • Begin with a karakia (prayer) if appropriate for your school context
  • Be aware some students may have whānau connections to expert orators - handle with sensitivity
  • Encourage students to share only what they're comfortable with regarding personal connections

Lesson Content

1. Understanding Whaikōrero Structure

Traditional whaikōrero follows a specific structure:

  1. Tauparapara - Opening incantation to establish speaker's credibility
  2. Whakapapa - Connections to people and place
  3. Kupu whakatepe - Main message or argument
  4. Whaikupu - Supporting evidence or stories
  5. Poroporoaki - Closing remarks

Activity: Watch this video example of whaikōrero and identify these elements.

2. Contemporary Debate Structure

Compare with standard debate format:

  1. Introduction - Hook and thesis
  2. Arguments - Main points with evidence
  3. Rebuttal - Addressing opposing views
  4. Conclusion - Summary and call to action

Discussion: What similarities and differences do you notice? How might each approach be effective in different contexts?

3. Hybrid Debate Activity

Topic: "Our school should incorporate more Māori cultural practices in daily routines."

Instructions:

  1. Divide into groups of 4-5
  2. Prepare arguments using a blend of both structures:
    • Begin with a personal connection (similar to whakapapa)
    • Include traditional proverbs (whakataukī) where relevant
    • Use evidence and logical arguments
    • Close with an impactful statement
  3. Each group presents their arguments (3-4 minutes each)
  4. Audience provides feedback using the assessment criteria

Assessment Suggestions

Criteria Excellent (4-5) Proficient (3) Developing (1-2)
Cultural Elements Skillfully integrates Māori oratory techniques authentically Includes some Māori oratory elements appropriately Minimal or inappropriate use of cultural elements
Argument Structure Clear, logical progression with strong evidence Logical structure with some supporting evidence Arguments unclear or unsupported
Delivery Engaging, confident delivery with appropriate body language Clear delivery with some confidence Difficult to hear/understand, lacks confidence
Cultural Respect Demonstrates deep understanding and respect Shows appropriate respect Cultural elements handled insensitively

Alternative Assessment: Written reflection comparing the two oratory traditions and their effectiveness.

Extension Activities

For Advanced Learners:

  1. Research specific iwi traditions of whaikōrero and present findings
  2. Create a debate entirely in te reo Māori incorporating traditional structures
  3. Compare Māori oratory with other indigenous oral traditions (e.g., Pasifika)
  4. Organize a school debate competition using the hybrid format
  5. Interview a local kaumatua about their experiences with whaikōrero

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

Support Strategies:

  • ESOL students: Provide sentence starters, allow use of first language for planning
  • Anxious speakers: Offer options to present to small groups or record speeches
  • Physical disabilities: Ensure accessible speaking spaces, allow seated presentations
  • Neurodiverse students: Provide clear structure templates, allow fidget tools
  • Cultural considerations: Be flexible with participation requirements based on cultural comfort levels

Teacher Implementation Guide

Before the Lesson:

  1. Consult with your school's Māori cultural advisor if available
  2. Prepare video examples and any necessary equipment
  3. Print structure guides and assessment rubrics
  4. Arrange seating for group work and presentations

During the Lesson:

  1. Introduction (15 mins): Explain objectives, show video example, discuss cultural protocols
  2. Analysis (20 mins): Compare structures in small groups, share findings
  3. Preparation (25 mins): Groups research and prepare hybrid debates
  4. Presentations (30 mins): Each group presents, peers provide feedback
  5. Reflection (10 mins): Whole class discussion on what worked well

After the Lesson:

  1. Provide individual feedback using rubric
  2. Display exemplary work or recordings (with permission)
  3. Follow up with interested students about extension opportunities
  4. Reflect on what cultural elements resonated most with students
