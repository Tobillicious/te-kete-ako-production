Poetry Analysis Through Māori Literary Traditions

He kōrero tēnei mō ngā momo whakataukī me ngā waiata o te ao Māori

This lesson explores traditional and contemporary Māori poetry forms, connecting them to universal poetic techniques while honoring their cultural origins.

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze traditional Māori poetic forms (whakataukī, waiata) and contemporary Māori poetry
  • Identify poetic devices in both English and te reo Māori texts
  • Compare and contrast Māori poetic traditions with other poetic traditions
  • Create original poetry inspired by Māori literary techniques
  • Understand the cultural significance of poetry in te ao Māori

Cultural Context & Safety Notes

Cultural Protocols: When sharing Māori poetry, especially traditional forms, acknowledge the iwi/hapū origins where known. Treat all texts with respect as taonga.

Language Considerations: Provide English translations alongside te reo Māori texts. Encourage but don't force pronunciation attempts.

Sensitive Content: Some contemporary Māori poetry may address colonization impacts. Be prepared to discuss these themes appropriately.

Lesson Content

1. Exploring Whakataukī (Māori Proverbs)

Whakataukī are traditional Māori poetic sayings that convey wisdom, cultural values, and observations about nature and human behavior.

Example Whakataukī:

"Ehara tāku toa i te toa takitahi, engari he toa takitini"

My strength is not as an individual but as a collective

Analysis Questions:

  1. What cultural value does this whakataukī express?
  2. Identify any poetic devices (metaphor, parallelism, etc.)
  3. How does the structure contribute to its meaning?
  4. Can you think of an English proverb with a similar message?

2. Waiata Analysis

Waiata are traditional Māori songs that serve various purposes - lullabies (oriori), love songs (waiata aroha), and lamentations (waiata tangi).

Excerpt from "E Pā Tō Hau" (Traditional Waiata)

E pā tō hau
He wini raro
He homai aroha

Oh the wind
Bringing the southerly
Bringing love

Analysis Tasks:

  1. Identify the natural imagery used
  2. Explain how the wind serves as a metaphor
  3. Compare the structure to English lyric poetry
  4. Research the historical context of this waiata

3. Contemporary Māori Poetry

Modern Māori poets blend traditional forms with contemporary themes and styles.

Excerpt from "Ka Ao, Ka Ao, Ka Awatea" by Hone Tuwhare

It is the new day
it is the new day seen
greening
at the edge of the sky

Comparative Analysis:

  1. How does Tuwhare use repetition differently from traditional waiata?
  2. Identify modern poetic devices in this excerpt
  3. What connections can you make to traditional Māori concepts of dawn (ao marama)?
  4. How does the poet blend Māori and English poetic traditions?

Assessment Suggestions

Formative Assessment:

  • Class participation in analysis discussions
  • Journal reflections comparing Māori and other poetic traditions
  • Small group presentations on assigned poems

Extension Activities

For Advanced Learners:

  1. Research the work of a specific Māori poet (e.g., Hone Tuwhare, Hinemoana Baker) and create a critical review
  2. Compare Māori poetic traditions with another indigenous poetic tradition (e.g., Native American, Aboriginal Australian)
  3. Create a bilingual poem incorporating both te reo Māori and English, with commentary on translation challenges
  4. Investigate how contemporary Māori poets address issues like colonization, identity, and environmental concerns

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

Support for Emerging Learners:

  • Provide pre-annotated poems with key features highlighted
  • Use graphic organizers for comparing poems
  • Offer sentence starters for analysis responses
  • Create mixed-ability discussion groups

Engagement Strategies:

  • Incorporate audio recordings of poems being performed
  • Use visual representations of poems (art responses)
  • Create digital poetry anthologies using simple platforms
  • Invite local Māori writers for classroom visits (virtual or in-person)

Implementation Guide

Lesson Sequence:

  1. Introduction (20 min): Begin with a karakia (optional) and overview of Māori poetic traditions. Show examples visually and aurally.
  2. Whakataukī Analysis (30 min): Small groups analyze different proverbs using guided questions.
  3. Waiata Exploration (40 min): Listen to recordings, analyze structure and imagery, discuss cultural context.
  4. Contemporary Poetry (40 min): Compare modern works to traditional forms, identify continuities and innovations.
  5. Creative Response (30 min+): Students create original poems inspired by Māori forms.
  6. Reflection (20 min): Journal entries or discussions about what they've learned.

Resources Needed:

  • Printed copies of poems with translations
  • Audio recordings of waiata
  • Biographical information about Māori poets
  • Graphic organizers for analysis
  • Art supplies for visual responses (optional)
