📋 Your Challenge

You've been assigned a real-life scenario. Using ACTUAL NZ prices from 2025, create a monthly budget. Your goal: Figure out if you can afford basic necessities and have anything left over. This simulation reveals the harsh economic realities many New Zealanders face.

🎭 SCENARIOS - Choose One (or assigned by teacher)

📍 SCENARIO A: Single Adult in Auckland

  • 25 years old, working full-time retail (40 hours/week)
  • Earning minimum wage ($23.15/hour)
  • Renting a room in flat share, Auckland central
  • No dependents, no car
  • Monthly income (after tax): $3,210

📍 SCENARIO B: Single Parent in Hamilton

  • 32 years old, working part-time hospitality (25 hours/week)
  • Earning minimum wage ($23.15/hour)
  • 1 child (age 8), renting 2-bedroom apartment
  • Receives Working for Families Tax Credit (+$250/month)
  • Monthly income (after tax): $2,255

📍 SCENARIO C: Young Couple in Rotorua

  • Both 28, one works full-time supermarket, one studying part-time
  • One earns minimum wage ($23.15/hr × 40h), one works 15h/week ($23.15/hr)
  • Renting 1-bedroom unit, Rotorua
  • One car between them
  • Combined monthly income (after tax): $4,085

💭 REFLECTION QUESTIONS

1. Can you afford basic necessities in your scenario? What had to be cut?

2. Compare minimum wage vs living wage income. How much difference would living wage make?

3. What unexpected expenses (medical, car breakdown, school costs) could create a crisis?

4. How does this simulation change your understanding of poverty and economic inequality in Aotearoa?

5. What systemic changes would help people in this situation?

👨‍🏫 Teacher Notes

Purpose: This simulation makes economic inequality tangible. Students experience the math of poverty - how minimum wage workers face impossible choices between necessities.

Key Teaching Points:

  • 2025 NZ minimum wage: $23.15/hour vs living wage: $27.80/hour (calculated by Living Wage Movement)
  • Living wage accounts for ACTUAL cost of living with dignity
  • Māori and Pacific peoples disproportionately affected by low wages
  • Discussion: Is it a "personal choice" or a systemic issue?

Extension Activities:

  • Compare all 3 scenarios as class - which is "easiest"?
  • Research actual rent prices on Trade Me for realism
  • Calculate: How many hours at minimum wage to afford average Auckland rent?
  • Graph wealth inequality using Stats NZ data
  • Debate: Should NZ raise minimum wage to living wage?

Culturally Responsive Note: Many students' whānau may be living these scenarios. Approach with sensitivity - this is about systemic analysis, not judgment of individuals.

NZ Curriculum Links:

  • Social Sciences: Economic concepts, Resource allocation, Social justice
  • Mathematics: Financial literacy, Budgeting, Percentages, Real-world application
  • Key Competencies: Thinking (problem-solving), Managing self (financial planning), Participating and contributing (understanding social issues)
