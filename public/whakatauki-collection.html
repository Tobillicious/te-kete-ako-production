💬 Whakataukī

Māori Proverbs: Wisdom, Values, and Teaching

"He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tangata, he tangata, he tangata."
Translation: What is the most important thing in the world? It is people, it is people, it is people.
Cultural Context:
This whakataukī emphasizes the paramount importance of human relationships and community. In education, it reminds us that our focus should always be on the wellbeing and development of our students (tangata) above all else - not test scores, not bureaucracy, but people.

🎓 Teaching Applications:

  • Use when introducing relationship-based learning approaches
  • Remind students that collaboration values people over competition
  • Frame cultural safety discussions around prioritizing people
  • Connect to pastoral care and wellbeing programs
"Kāore te kumara e kōrero mō tōna ake reka"
Translation: The kumara does not speak of its own sweetness.
Cultural Context:
This proverb teaches humility - let your actions and quality speak for themselves. True excellence doesn't need boasting. In education, it encourages students to demonstrate their learning through their work rather than self-promotion.

🎓 Teaching Applications:

  • Teach humility and letting work speak for itself
  • Encourage peer recognition over self-promotion
  • Frame assessment as demonstration, not performance
  • Connect to growth mindset and continuous improvement
"Whāia te iti kahurangi, ki te tuohu koe, me he maunga teitei"
Translation: Seek the treasure you value most dearly; if you bow your head, let it be to a lofty mountain.
Cultural Context:
This whakataukī encourages pursuing excellence and only accepting the highest standards. In education, it inspires students to aim high, pursue meaningful goals, and maintain their integrity in the face of challenges.

🎓 Teaching Applications:

  • Set high expectations for student work
  • Encourage pursuit of excellence over mediocrity
  • Frame goal-setting around personal values
  • Support students in maintaining standards
"Ko te amorangi ki mua, ko te hāpai ō ki muri"
Translation: The leader at the front, the worker behind.
Cultural Context:
Leadership requires support and collaboration. This whakataukī reminds us that visible leadership is only possible because of the essential work happening behind the scenes. In classrooms, it validates all forms of contribution to group success.

🎓 Teaching Applications:

  • Value all roles in group work, not just leadership
  • Recognize support roles as essential
  • Teach collaborative leadership models
  • Frame success as collective achievement

⚠️ Important Cultural Guidance

Proper Use of Whakataukī:
• Understand full context before using
• Seek guidance from kaumātua or cultural advisors
• Use appropriately in relevant contexts
• Pronounce correctly (provide audio where possible)
• Respect the depth of meaning - not just decoration
• Acknowledge source and iwi where known
