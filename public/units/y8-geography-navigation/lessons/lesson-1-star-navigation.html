Lesson 1: Te Kā Rererangi – Stars as Compass

Whakataukī

"Tōia mai te waka; ka eke ki te ua, ka eke ki te whenua!"

Pull the canoe to the shore; onto the rain-drenched land, onto the sun-baked land!

This whakataukī celebrates the navigational expertise that brought our ancestors across vast oceans.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify key star constellations used in Polynesian navigation (Matariki, Te Māhutonga, Tautoru).
  • Explain how star paths change with seasons and latitude.
  • Apply star compass principles to determine direction at night.

NZ Curriculum: Social Sciences L4 – understand how people interact with places | Science L4 – Earth & Space systems

1. Hook (10 min)

Image prompt: Show traditional waka hourua (double-hulled canoe) under night sky.

Question: "How did our tūpuna navigate thousands of kilometres across ocean with no GPS, compass, or maps?"

Think-pair-share → harvest ideas.

2. Concept Build (20 min)

Star Compass (Te Kā Whetū)

  • Introduce Matariki (Pleiades) – rises in winter, signals new year; used to mark north-east direction.
  • Introduce Te Māhutonga (Southern Cross) – points south; Pointers show true south pole.
  • Introduce Tautoru (Orion's Belt) – rises due east, sets due west.

Activity: Students use Star Compass Worksheet to plot constellations and practice finding cardinal directions.

Extension: Use planetarium software (Stellarium) or interactive star chart to see seasonal changes.

3. Guided Practice (15 min)

Scenario: "You're sailing from Rarotonga to Aotearoa. It's June. Which stars will guide you south?"

Groups work through navigation problem using star charts, then present reasoning.

Teacher prompts: What time of night? What if it's cloudy? How do you know when to adjust course?

Assessment & Success Criteria

Formative

Exit ticket: Sketch Southern Cross & label where south is; explain one reason Matariki is culturally significant.

Rubric Focus

  • Accuracy: Correctly identify constellations and directions.
  • Cultural Understanding: Explain significance beyond mechanics.
  • Application: Solve navigation scenarios with reasoning.

Teacher PD Note

Emphasise that traditional navigation is not "ancient history" – it is living knowledge. Modern navigators like Matahi Brightwell and the crew of Te Aurere continue these practices. Invite students to research contemporary navigators as extension.

Cultural safety: Acknowledge that some iwi hold specific navigation knowledge as taonga; focus on publicly shared knowledge and encourage respectful curiosity.
