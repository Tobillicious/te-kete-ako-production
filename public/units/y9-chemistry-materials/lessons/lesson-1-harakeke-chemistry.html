Lesson 1: Te Harakeke – Fibre Chemistry & Processing

Whakataukī

"Mā te rongo, ka mōhio; mā te mōhio, ka mārama; mā te mārama, ka mātau; mā te mātau, ka ora."

Through perception comes awareness; through awareness comes understanding; through understanding comes knowledge; through knowledge comes wellbeing.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the chemical structure of cellulose and its role in plant fibres.
  • Explain the retting process (microbial decomposition of pectin and lignin).
  • Compare traditional harakeke processing with modern industrial methods.
  • Apply tikanga (protocol) to sustainable harvesting.

🔬 Practical Investigation: Harakeke Fibre Properties

Materials:

  • Harakeke leaf samples (harvested sustainably with karakia)
  • Water, vinegar (weak acid), baking soda solution (weak base)
  • Beakers, pH paper, hot plate (optional)
  • Microscope & slides

Procedure:

  1. Observation: Examine fresh harakeke under microscope; sketch fibre bundles.
  2. pH test: Soak fibre strips in water (control), vinegar, baking soda for 24 hours. Compare strength after soaking (pull test).
  3. Retting simulation: Place leaf in water jar; observe weekly for 4 weeks. Note colour, smell, texture changes as pectin breaks down.
  4. Data: Record pH, time, observations. Graph strength vs. pH.

Safety: Gloves for handling soaked materials; wash hands; dispose respectfully.

Chemistry Concepts

  • Cellulose: Long-chain polymer (C₆H₁₀O₅)ₙ giving tensile strength.
  • Lignin & Pectin: Binding agents broken down in retting; bacteria/fungi produce enzymes.
  • pH effects: Acids weaken cellulose; bases may help remove lignin but can damage fibre if too strong.

Mātauranga Integration

  • Traditional harvesters understood optimal timing (lunar cycles, seasonal moisture) for best fibre quality.
  • Karakia acknowledges mauri (life force) and requests permission from Tāne Mahuta.
  • Sustainable cutting ensures plant regeneration – aligned with modern conservation chemistry.

Assessment & Reflection

Lab Report Criteria:

  • Clear method & observations (scientific rigour)
  • Chemical explanations using correct terminology
  • Connection to traditional practice & tikanga
  • Consideration of sustainability

Use rubric to ensure equal weight on chemistry accuracy AND cultural understanding.

🧑‍🏫 Teacher PD Note

Harakeke is a taonga species with deep significance. Before harvesting, consult your school's cultural advisor and seek permission from local iwi if on their rohe. Model the harvesting tikanga yourself first (do not cut rito/centre shoot; take from outer leaves only; recite karakia). Frame the chemistry as explaining what Māori practitioners already knew empirically – avoid implying Western science "discovered" these properties.
