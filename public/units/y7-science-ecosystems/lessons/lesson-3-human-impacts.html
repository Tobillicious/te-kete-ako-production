    Lesson 3: Human Impacts & Kaitiaki Action

    📚Ngā Whāinga Ako | Learning Objectives

    NZ Curriculum: Phase 3-4 (Years 7-8) | Adaptable for: Years 7-7

    🎯 Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Intentions

    📚 Students Will Learn

      How human activities impact local and NZ ecosystems (positive and negative)
    • The difference between introduced and native species in Aotearoa
    • How kaitiakitanga practices help protect and restore ecosystems
    • Strategies for reducing human environmental impacts

    ✅ Students Will Demonstrate

      By identifying 3+ human impacts on local ecosystems with evidence
    • By explaining why introduced species threaten native NZ ecosystems
    • By proposing kaitiakitanga actions to protect a local ecosystem
    • By creating an action plan for reducing their environmental impact

    🌿 Ngā Tikanga Ako | Māori Pedagogical Approaches

      Ako: Reciprocal teaching and learning - students and teachers learn together
    • Tuakana-teina: Peer teaching with more experienced students supporting others
    • Wānanga: Deep collaborative discussion and knowledge sharing
    • Mahi tahi: Working together towards shared understanding

    💡 Teacher: Consider how these approaches can be authentically integrated into your lesson delivery.

    💡 Differentiation: Look for related resources at different NZ Curriculum phases to provide appropriate scaffolding for diverse ākonga. Consider creating learning pathways that connect resources at increasing complexity.

    🌿Cultural Context | Horopaki Ahurea

    🏛️ Connection to Mangakōtukutuku College Values

    Whaimana: Integrity - Standing firm in truth and principle

    whaimana by encouraging students to stand firm in environmental responsibility—honestly facing human impacts on ecosystems, taking principled action to reduce harm, and advocating for kaitiakitanga practices that protect our natural world for future generations.

    • Approach Māori content with respect and openness
    • Recognize students' diverse cultural backgrounds and experiences
    • Create space for Māori students to share their perspectives
    • Consult with whānau and local iwi for culturally sensitive topics

    Inquiry Focus

    Investigate a local issue (e.g., water quality, invasive species, habitat loss). Gather evidence from field notes and community voices.

    Data to Decision

    1. Summarise evidence (tables/graphs, photos, observations).
    2. Identify likely causes and affected species (use food web from L2).
    3. Draft an action plan guided by kaitiakitanga (who, what, when, tikanga).

    Action Proposal (Summative)

    • One-page proposal + short presentation to class/whānau.
    • Criteria: scientific validity, feasibility, cultural integrity, positive impact.

    Extend learning: try the Ecorestore simulation (Coming soon) to test interventions.
