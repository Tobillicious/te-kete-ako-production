    Lesson 1: Kaitiakitanga & Local Ecosystems

    📚Ngā Whāinga Ako | Learning Objectives

    NZ Curriculum: Phase 3-4 (Years 7-8) | Adaptable for: Years 7-7

    🎯 Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Intentions

    📚 Students Will Learn

      The concept of kaitiakitanga (guardianship) and its role in environmental care
    • How to identify living and non-living components of local ecosystems
    • How traditional Māori knowledge helps us understand ecosystems
    • Methods for observing and recording interactions between organisms

    ✅ Students Will Demonstrate

      By explaining what kaitiakitanga means with a community example
    • By identifying at least 5 living and 3 non-living things in a local ecosystem
    • By describing how organisms depend on each other in the ecosystem
    • By connecting Māori traditional knowledge to modern ecology understanding

    💡 Differentiation: Look for related resources at different NZ Curriculum phases to provide appropriate scaffolding for diverse ākonga. Consider creating learning pathways that connect resources at increasing complexity.

    WALT (We Are Learning To) | Ngā Whāinga:

      Understand the concept of kaitiakitanga (guardianship) and its relevance to ecosystem science
    • Identify and classify living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) components in ecosystems
    • Describe how kaitiakitanga principles guide environmental care in Aotearoa
    • Observe and record ecosystem components in a local environment

    Success Criteria (WILF - What I'm Looking For) | Ngā Paearu:

      I can explain what kaitiakitanga means and give examples of kaitiaki actions
    • I can identify at least 5 biotic and 5 abiotic components in an ecosystem
    • I can describe the difference between living and non-living ecosystem parts
    • I can connect scientific concepts to Māori environmental perspectives

    Warm-up (Whakawhanaungatanga)

    • Whakataukī: "Toitū te marae o Tāne, toitū te marae o Tangaroa, toitū te tangata" – discuss meaning for environmental care.
    • Pair-share: where have you seen kaitiaki actions in your community?

    🌿Cultural Context | Horopaki Ahurea

    🏛️ Connection to Mangakōtukutuku College Values

    Whaiora: Wellbeing - Caring for collective health and prosperity

    whaiora by encouraging students to understand kaitiakitanga (guardianship) as active care for ecosystem wellbeing—recognizing that the health of our natural environment directly supports the physical, spiritual, and cultural wellbeing of all communities in Aotearoa.

    • Approach Māori content with respect and openness
    • Recognize students' diverse cultural backgrounds and experiences
    • Create space for Māori students to share their perspectives
    • Consult with whānau and local iwi for culturally sensitive topics

    Concept Build

    • Introduce ecosystem terms: organism, habitat, abiotic/biotic, interactions.
    • Map local places using Māori names; note significant species/mahinga kai.

    Field Preparation

    • Distribute Field Journal and Survey Checklist.
    • Safety, karakia, roles (observer, recorder, photographer, kaitiaki leader).

    Success Criteria

    • Explain kaitiakitanga in your own words with a local example.
    • Identify at least 5 components and 3 interactions in the selected ecosystem.
