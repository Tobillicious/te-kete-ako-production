Who Was Ngāpare Hopa?

Unit: Hopa - The Scholar and the People | Lesson 4.1

Year 10 | Social Studies, Sociology | 75 minutes

🏛️ School Value: Whaiara (Rising Up)

As a wahine Māori achieving Oxford doctorate in the 1970s, Hopa demonstrated rising up through academic excellence while staying connected to her people.

Overview: This lesson introduces Dr. Ngāpare Hopa, the first Māori woman to earn a doctorate from Oxford University (D.Phil in Sociology, 1977). Students will explore her groundbreaking academic career, her community-centered research on urban Māori, and how she broke gender and cultural barriers in 1970s academia.

🎯 WALT: We are learning to understand Ngāpare Hopa's pioneering role as the first Māori woman with an Oxford doctorate

✅ Success Criteria:

  • Explain the significance of being "first Māori woman" with Oxford doctorate
  • Describe the double barriers she faced (gender + ethnicity)
  • Identify her main research focus (urban Māori communities)
  • Connect her achievement to Whaiara (rising up)

👩‍🎓 Hopa's Achievements

  • 🎓 First Māori woman Oxford D.Phil (1977, Sociology)
  • 📚 Research focus: Urban Māori wellbeing, identity, community
  • 👥 Community-centered approach: Research WITH Māori, not ON them
  • 🏫 Academic career: University lecturer, researcher, mentor to future Māori scholars
  • 📊 Policy impact: Her research influenced government programs for urban Māori

📝 Activity: The Double Barrier

In the 1960s-70s, Hopa faced TWO major barriers:

Barrier 1: Gender

Academia was male-dominated. Few women earned doctorates. Even fewer led university departments or research teams.

Barrier 2: Ethnicity

Māori were underrepresented in universities. Racist assumptions about Māori intelligence persisted. Few Māori had PhDs of any kind.

Discussion: How might these TWO barriers together be harder than either alone?
