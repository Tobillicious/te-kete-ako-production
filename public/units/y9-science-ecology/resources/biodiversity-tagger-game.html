    Biodiversity Tagger Game

    ğŸƒ Biodiversity Tagger Game

    Type: Active Learning Game | Duration: 30-40 minutes | Location: Outdoor space

    Game Purpose

    An energetic outdoor game that teaches predator-prey dynamics, food webs, and the importance of biodiversity in ecosystems. Students embody different native NZ species and experience firsthand how ecosystem balance works.

    Learning Objectives

    • Understand predator-prey relationships in NZ ecosystems
    • Experience how species depend on each other (food webs)
    • Recognize the impact of invasive predators on native species
    • Learn native species names in te reo MÄori

    Setup & Materials

    Space Requirements:

    Large outdoor area (school field, park) - approximately 30m x 30m

    20-40 students (scales well with more/fewer players)

    How to Play

    Game Setup (5 minutes)

    1. Divide students into 4 groups (distribute evenly)
    2. Assign species roles and give colored bibs
    3. Explain boundaries and "forest zones" (safe areas for plants)
    4. Review rules and demonstrate tagging

    Round 1: Balanced Ecosystem (10 minutes)

    Species Distribution:

    • 40% Plants (stay in zones)
    • 30% Insects
    • 25% Birds
    • 5% Predators (only 2-3 students)

    Rules:

    • ğŸŸ¡ Insects must "visit" plants (touch) to survive
    • ğŸ”µ Birds must tag insects to survive
    • ğŸ”´ Predators can tag birds or insects
    • Tagged players go to "extinction zone" and sit out for 30 seconds
    • After 30 seconds, they "reproduce" and return to game

    Objective: Survive! Each species tries to maintain their population.

    Round 2: Invasive Predator Invasion! (10 minutes)

    Change: ADD 5-10 more predators (recruit from other species)

    Observe what happens to the ecosystem when predators increase!

    Round 3: Conservation Intervention (10 minutes)

    Add "Kaitiaki" role: 2-3 students wear special vest

    Kaitiaki Powers:

    • Can "tag out" predators (send to extinction zone for 1 minute)
    • Can "protect" birds by standing near them (safe zone)
    • Represent predator control and conservation efforts

    Can the ecosystem recover with kaitiaki intervention?

    Scoring & Data Collection

    Debrief Questions (10 minutes)

    Gather students in circle and discuss:

    1. What happened to bird populations in Round 2? Why?
    2. How did it feel to be a bird with many predators?
    3. What role did the kaitiaki play in Round 3?
    4. How does this game reflect real NZ ecosystems?
    5. What conservation strategies work in real life?
    6. Why is biodiversity (having many species) important?

    Variations & Extensions

    For Younger Students (Y7-8):

    • Simplify to 3 species (plants, birds, predators)
    • Shorter rounds (5 minutes each)
    • More frequent "reproduction" (return after 20 seconds)

    For Older Students (Y10+):

    • Add habitat destruction (reduce playing area each round)
    • Introduce climate change effects (random events)
    • Add resource scarcity (limited plant zones)
    • Calculate actual population growth rates

    Cross-Curricular Links:

    • Math: Graph population changes, calculate percentages
    • PE: Warm-up game, fitness activity
    • Te Reo MÄori: Practice species names, ecological vocabulary
    • Drama: Students embody animal behaviors

    ğŸ¯ Teacher Tips

    Safety:

    • Clear boundaries - no running outside marked area
    • Tag gently - touch only, no pushing/grabbing
    • Watch for collisions - blow whistle to pause if needed
    • Bring water - it's an active game!
    • Sun protection for outdoor play

    Management:

    • Rotate species roles between rounds so everyone experiences all roles
    • Have observers if class is large - they can track data
    • Use whistle signals: 1 blast = freeze, 2 blasts = resume, 3 blasts = game over
    • Debrief is crucial - the learning happens in reflection!

    Assessment:

    • Observe participation and understanding during play
    • Use debrief questions for formative assessment
    • Follow-up with written reflection or diagram of food web
    • Connect to ecosystem unit learning objectives
