📖 Lesson Overview

This lesson introduces students to Princess Te Puea Hērangi, one of the most influential Māori leaders of the 20th century. Students will explore her early life, her connection to the Kīngitanga (Māori King Movement), and her emergence as a leader during times of crisis. This lesson establishes the foundation for understanding her pivotal role in revitalizing Tainui and building Tūrangawaewae marae.

🎯 Learning Objectives

WALT (We Are Learning To):

We are learning to understand the significance of Te Puea Hērangi's life and her role in the Kīngitanga.

✅ Success Criteria (I will be successful if I can...):

  • ✓ Describe Te Puea's whakapapa (genealogy) and connection to the Māori King Movement
  • ✓ Identify three key challenges she faced in her early leadership
  • ✓ Explain what the Kīngitanga is and why it was important to Māori in the Waikato region
  • ✓ Articulate how Te Puea's leadership exemplifies the value of Whaiora (Wellbeing)

📚 Key Vocabulary

Kīngitanga

The Māori King Movement, established in 1858 to unite Māori tribes

Ariki

High chief, noble person of highest rank

Tūrangawaewae

A place to stand, a place where one has rights of residence and belonging

Tainui

A major Māori iwi (tribe) of the Waikato region

Marae

The communal and sacred meeting grounds of Māori people

📝 Lesson Activities (75 minutes)

🌅 DO NOW / Whakawhanaungatanga

0-5 min

Prompt: "What qualities make a great leader? Write down three qualities and one example of a leader (historical or contemporary) who has those qualities."

Students write 2-3 sentences. This activates prior knowledge about leadership and sets the stage for learning about Te Puea.

👨‍🏫 Explicit Teaching / Direct Instruction

5-15 min

Introduce Learning Objectives:

  • Share WALT and Success Criteria
  • Use "Do Now" responses to discuss leadership qualities
  • Introduce Te Puea as woman who led her people through devastating times

Introduce the Kīngitanga:

  • Established 1858 to unite Māori tribes
  • Provide alternative to Crown authority
  • Protect Māori land and sovereignty
  • Show image of Kīngitanga flag

Te Puea's Whakapapa:

  • Descended from Māori Kings Tāwhiao and Mahuta
  • Granddaughter of King Tāwhiao
  • Born 1883 in Whatiwhatihoe, Waikato
  • Show map of Waikato region and Ngāruawāhia

👥 Guided Practice / Student Activity

15-40 min

📄 Reading Activity:

Distribute "Te Puea Hērangi: A Life of Service" handout. Students read about:

  • Te Puea's early life and education
  • Challenges: 1918 influenza pandemic, poverty, loss of land
  • Her emergence as a leader in times of crisis

📅 Timeline Creation (In Pairs):

Create a timeline of Te Puea's life highlighting:

  1. Birth & Early Life (1883-1910)
  2. Emerging as Leader (1911-1918)
  3. The Pandemic & Its Aftermath (1918-1920s)

💡 Show WAGOLL timeline example before students begin

Pairs share their timelines with the class.

✍️ Independent Practice / Application

40-60 min

🌿 Connecting to Whaiora (Wellbeing):

Writing Prompt: "How did Te Puea's leadership focus on Whaiora (Wellbeing) for her people? Consider physical, mental, spiritual, and family wellbeing."

💡 WAGOLL Paragraph Example:

"Te Puea's leadership demonstrated deep commitment to Whaiora across all dimensions of wellbeing. Physically, she cared for influenza orphans and ensured her people had food and shelter. Mentally and spiritually, she worked to restore their sense of mana and connection to their ancestors. For family wellbeing, she built Tūrangawaewae marae as a place where Tainui could gather, heal, and rebuild their community. Her holistic approach showed she understood that true wellbeing requires addressing all aspects of life, not just material needs."

✏️ Student Writing Guidelines:
  • Use specific examples from the handout
  • Consider all four dimensions: physical, mental, spiritual, family
  • Explain HOW her actions supported wellbeing (don't just list them)
  • Aim for 150-200 words

🎯 Plenary / Wrap-up

60-75 min

Review & Discussion:

  • Review WALT and Success Criteria - did we achieve them?
  • Class Discussion: "Te Puea is often called 'The Mother of the Nation.' Why do you think she earned this title?"
  • Exit Ticket: "What is one challenge Te Puea faced that you want to learn more about in the next lesson?"

🎯 Differentiated Instruction

💚 Support Strategies:

  • Provide partially completed timeline with key dates filled in
  • Offer sentence starters for writing task
  • Pair with peer mentor for timeline activity
  • Simplified handout version available

🔥 Extension Challenges:

  • Research other Māori Kings and create family tree
  • Compare Kīngitanga to other indigenous sovereignty movements
  • Read excerpts from Michael King's "Te Puea: A Life"
  • Create presentation on Kīngitanga relevance today

📊 Assessment

✅ Formative Assessment:

  • Timeline completion (accuracy, key events included, analysis)
  • Quality of pair discussion and class participation
  • "Connecting to Whaiora" paragraph
  • Exit ticket responses

📝 Summative Assessment:

This lesson provides foundational knowledge for the rest of the unit. Assess whether students understand Te Puea's whakapapa, the significance of the Kīngitanga, and the concept of holistic wellbeing. This will support their learning in upcoming lessons.

🧑‍🏫 Teacher Implementation Notes

🌿 Culturally Responsive Pedagogy:

  • Mana Wahine: Te Puea is exceptional example of Māori women's leadership (traditional through whakapapa + innovative adapting to modern challenges)
  • Waikato Context: If teaching in Waikato, this is local history - students may have whānau connections to Tainui or Tūrangawaewae
  • Respect for Kīngitanga: Living institution - current Māori King Kiingi Tuheitia continues this tradition
  • Wellbeing Framework: Connect to Te Whare Tapa Whā (four-sided house model of Māori health)

💭 Post-Lesson Reflection Questions:

  • Did students grasp the significance of the Kīngitanga?
  • Were they able to understand whakapapa and its importance?
  • How did students respond to Te Puea as a female leader?
  • Did exit tickets reveal genuine interest in learning more?
  • Were Waikato/Tainui students able to share their connections?
