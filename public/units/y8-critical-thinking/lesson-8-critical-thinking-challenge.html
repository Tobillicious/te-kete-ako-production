    📚Ngā Whāinga Ako | Learning Objectives

    NZ Curriculum: Phase 3-4 (Years 7-8) | Adaptable for: Years 8-8

    WALT (We Are Learning To) | Ngā Whāinga:

      Apply all critical thinking skills learned in this unit to real-world problems
    • Evaluate complex issues using evidence, logic, and multiple perspectives
    • Integrate Māori and Western approaches to critical analysis
    • Demonstrate deep thinking through written analysis and oral presentation

    Success Criteria (WAGOLL - What a Good One Looks Like) | Ngā Paearu:

      I can analyze a complex issue using evidence from multiple reliable sources
    • I can identify logical fallacies, bias, and assumptions in arguments
    • I can explain both Māori and Western perspectives on the issue
    • I can present my analysis clearly with well-reasoned conclusions

    Critical Thinking Challenge

    Year 8 Critical Thinking Unit | 45 minutes (+ homework component)

    Summative assessment - Real-world application of critical thinking skills

    👨‍🏫 Learning Objectives

    • Synthesize all critical thinking skills learned in unit
    • Analyze complex real-world NZ issues using evidence-based reasoning
    • Create persuasive arguments while acknowledging opposing viewpoints
    • Use digital tools effectively to research and present findings
    • Demonstrate cultural sensitivity and awareness in analysis

    Assessment Focus:

    • Whakaaro (critical thinking) - evidence evaluation, bias recognition
    • Kōrero (communication) - clear, persuasive presentation
    • Rangahau (research) - credible sources, fact-checking
    • Whakatōhea (respectful debate) - acknowledging multiple perspectives

    Resources Needed:

    • Chromebooks with internet access
    • Access to Anchor.fm (podcast option)
    • Google Slides/Canva for presentations
    • Peer feedback forms (digital or print)
    • Assessment rubrics
    • Issue briefing sheets

    🎯 Challenge Launch: Real Issues, Real Thinking 10 minutes

    The Challenge:

    You are a member of a government advisory group tasked with analyzing a current NZ issue and making evidence-based recommendations. Your analysis must demonstrate sophisticated critical thinking and cultural awareness.

    Issue Option A: Vaping Among Young People

    Current Situation: NZ has seen rapid increase in youth vaping rates despite restrictions

    • Health concerns vs. harm reduction arguments
    • Māori youth disproportionately affected
    • Industry marketing vs. public health messaging
    • School enforcement challenges

    Issue Option B: Climate Change Action vs. Economic Growth

    Current Situation: Balancing environmental protection with economic development

    • Zero Carbon Act implementation challenges
    • Rural vs. urban perspectives
    • Māori environmental knowledge vs. Western science
    • Intergenerational equity concerns

    Issue Option C: Social Media Age Verification

    Current Situation: Calls to restrict social media access for under-16s

    • Mental health impacts vs. digital rights
    • Privacy concerns with age verification
    • Digital divide and equity issues
    • International approaches and effectiveness

    Selection Process: Students choose their preferred issue or teacher assigns to ensure even distribution across options.

    📋 Assessment Options: Choose Your Challenge Format 5 minutes

    Option 1: Policy Podcast (45 minutes total)

    Format: 8-10 minute podcast using Anchor.fm

    Structure:

    • Issue introduction with current data (2 minutes)
    • Multiple perspectives analysis (4 minutes)
    • Evidence evaluation and fact-checking (2 minutes)
    • Reasoned recommendations (2 minutes)

    Requirements: Cite 4+ credible sources, address counter-arguments, show cultural awareness

    Option 2: Visual Policy Brief (45 minutes total)

    Format: 8-slide Google Slides presentation + 5-minute recorded presentation

    Structure:

    • Problem definition with evidence (2 slides)
    • Stakeholder perspectives (2 slides)
    • Critical analysis of arguments (2 slides)
    • Recommendations with justification (2 slides)

    Requirements: Visual data representation, credible sources, acknowledgment of limitations

    Option 3: Live Debate Contribution (45 minutes total)

    Format: Structured class debate with prepared position

    Preparation:

    • Research brief (written, 300 words)
    • Opening argument (2 minutes spoken)
    • Response to counter-arguments (throughout debate)
    • Closing synthesis (1 minute)

    Requirements: Evidence-based arguments, respectful engagement, acknowledgment of complexity

    🔍 Research & Analysis Phase 25 minutes in-class + homework

    Phase 1: Source Investigation (10 minutes)

    • Identify 4+ credible sources from different perspectives
    • Use fact-checking tools (Google Fact Check Explorer, Snopes)
    • Evaluate source bias and reliability
    • Document sources using provided template

    Required Source Types:

    • Government/official data (stats.govt.nz, ministry websites)
    • Academic research or expert analysis
    • News reporting from reputable outlets
    • Community/iwi perspectives (where relevant)

    Phase 2: Critical Analysis (15 minutes)

    • Identify key stakeholders and their interests
    • Map arguments for/against different positions
    • Evaluate evidence quality and identify gaps
    • Consider cultural dimensions and Te Ao Māori perspectives

    Analysis Framework:

    • Evidence Evaluation: Is this fact, opinion, or interpretation?
    • Bias Detection: What interests might influence this perspective?
    • Logical Structure: Do the conclusions follow from the premises?
    • Missing Voices: Whose perspectives are not represented?

    Homework Component: Complete research and prepare chosen assessment format (estimated 60-90 minutes)

    📊 Peer Review & Final Presentations 5 minutes setup

    Peer Feedback Process (Next Lesson)

    Digital Peer Review: Students use Google Sheets to provide structured feedback

    Feedback Categories:

    • Evidence Use: How well did they support arguments with credible sources?
    • Multiple Perspectives: Did they fairly represent different viewpoints?
    • Critical Analysis: How sophisticated was their evaluation of arguments?
    • Cultural Awareness: Did they consider Māori perspectives where relevant?
    • Presentation: How clearly did they communicate their analysis?

    Feedback Questions:

    • What was the strongest part of their critical thinking?
    • What perspective or evidence could strengthen their analysis?
    • How well did they address potential counter-arguments?

    📊 Summative Assessment Rubric

    Critical Analysis & Evidence Use (25 points)

    Excellent (23-25): Uses multiple credible sources, evaluates evidence quality, identifies bias, demonstrates sophisticated reasoning
    Good (18-22): Uses credible sources, shows some evidence evaluation, generally sound reasoning
    Developing (13-17): Uses some sources, limited evidence evaluation, basic reasoning
    Beginning (0-12): Few or unreliable sources, minimal evidence evaluation, weak reasoning

    Multiple Perspectives & Counter-Arguments (20 points)

    Excellent (18-20): Fairly represents multiple viewpoints, addresses counter-arguments thoughtfully
    Good (14-17): Shows some understanding of different perspectives, acknowledges opposing views
    Developing (10-13): Limited perspective-taking, minimal counter-argument consideration
    Beginning (0-9): Single perspective, no consideration of opposing views

    Cultural Awareness & Sensitivity (15 points)

    Excellent (14-15): Demonstrates clear understanding of cultural dimensions, respectful integration of Māori perspectives
    Good (11-13): Shows some cultural awareness, attempts to include diverse perspectives
    Developing (8-10): Basic cultural awareness, limited integration of diverse viewpoints
    Beginning (0-7): Little cultural awareness or sensitivity demonstrated

    Communication & Presentation (20 points)

    Excellent (18-20): Clear, engaging presentation; appropriate use of digital tools; well-organized
    Good (14-17): Generally clear presentation, competent use of tools, organized
    Developing (10-13): Basic presentation skills, some organizational issues
    Beginning (0-9): Unclear presentation, poor organization, ineffective tool use

    Application of Unit Concepts (20 points)

    Excellent (18-20): Clearly applies critical thinking vocabulary and concepts learned throughout unit
    Good (14-17): Shows some application of unit concepts and vocabulary
    Developing (10-13): Limited application of unit learning
    Beginning (0-9): Minimal evidence of unit concept application

    Total: ___/100 points

    🎯 Differentiation & Support Strategies

    For Advanced Learners:

    • Challenge to analyze international comparisons or policy alternatives
    • Require synthesis of 6+ sources from diverse perspectives
    • Ask them to propose original solutions or compromises
    • Encourage exploration of underlying philosophical or ethical frameworks

    For Developing Learners:

    • Provide pre-selected, vetted sources to analyze
    • Offer templates for argument structure and analysis
    • Allow collaborative partnerships for research and preparation
    • Break assessment into smaller, scaffolded steps

    For ESL/Language Support:

    • Provide key vocabulary glossaries
    • Allow extra time for research and preparation
    • Offer sentence starters and paragraph templates
    • Consider allowing presentation in Te Reo Māori where appropriate

    🔗 Unit Culmination & Next Steps

    Celebrating Learning:

    • Create class gallery of best critical thinking examples
    • Invite school leaders or community members to hear presentations
    • Submit outstanding work to school newsletter or local media
    • Connect students with local youth advisory groups

    Ongoing Application:

    • Encourage use of skills in other subject debates and discussions
    • Connect with school's student council or environmental group
    • Suggest family dinner table discussions using critical thinking approaches
    • Link to future social studies and English assignments

    Assessment Follow-Up:

    • Individual conferences to discuss growth and next steps
    • Portfolio reflection connecting all unit learning
    • Goal-setting for continued critical thinking development
    • Peer appreciation activity recognizing different strengths
