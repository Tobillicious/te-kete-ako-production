    Asking the Right Questions

    Year 8 Critical Thinking Unit | 45 minutes

    Socratic questioning & evidence-based problem-solving

    👨‍🏫 Learning Objectives

    • Apply Socratic questioning techniques
    • Distinguish between surface and deep questions
    • Use evidence-based reasoning to solve historical mysteries
    • Understand how Māori oral histories function as evidence
    • Develop systematic inquiry skills

    Key Vocabulary:

    • Pakirehua (to question, inquire)
    • Pātai (question, ask)
    • Kōrero tuku iho (oral traditions passed down)
    • Socratic questioning - systematic method of inquiry
    • Evidence hierarchy - ranking reliability of sources

    🔍 Starter: Question Quality Sorting 8 minutes

    Display these questions about climate change:

    Shallow Questions:

    • "Is climate change real?"
    • "Who talks about climate change the most?"
    • "When did people start caring about the environment?"

    Deep Questions:

    • "What evidence would convince skeptics that climate change requires urgent action?"
    • "How do indigenous knowledge systems like mātauranga Māori help us understand environmental change?"
    • "What are the underlying assumptions in different climate change solutions?"

    Student Task: Pairs identify the difference, create one more example of each type

    🦅 Main Activity: The Mystery of the Missing Moa 25 minutes

    Historical NZ Mystery Investigation

    The Scenario:

    Moa were massive flightless birds that lived in New Zealand for millions of years. Archaeological evidence shows they disappeared around 600-700 years ago, shortly after Polynesian settlement. But what exactly happened? Your job is to solve this mystery using evidence and expert questioning.

    Evidence Package:

    Clue 1: Moa bones found in middens (ancient rubbish dumps) alongside tools and oven stones

    Clue 2: Māori oral traditions mention hunting large birds, but few specific moa stories survive

    Clue 3: Pollen records show forest clearance began soon after human arrival

    Clue 4: Moa egg fragments found in archaeological sites throughout NZ

    Clue 5: No evidence of moa bones in sites dated after 1400 CE

    Clue 6: Climate records show no major environmental changes during extinction period

    Student Investigation Process (Groups of 4):

    1. Initial Hypothesis (5 minutes): What do you think happened to the moa?
    2. Evidence Analysis (10 minutes): Examine each clue, discuss what it suggests
    3. Expert Questions (10 minutes): Generate questions you'd ask specialists

    Types of Experts & Sample Questions:

    Archaeologist: "What dating methods did you use? How certain are these dates?"

    Māori Elder: "What do oral traditions say about relationships with large birds?"

    Paleontologist: "How quickly can large bird populations decline? What are the warning signs?"

    Ecologist: "What role did moa play in forest ecosystems? What changed when they disappeared?"

    Teacher Facilitation Guide:

    • Encourage students to question the evidence itself: "How do we know this bone is 600 years old?"
    • Push for deeper inquiry: "What assumptions are we making about Māori hunting practices?"
    • Connect to modern parallels: "How is this similar to current species extinctions?"
    • Emphasize multiple contributing factors rather than single causes

    💬 Cultural Connection: Māori Oral Histories as Evidence 7 minutes

    Understanding Kōrero Tuku Iho

    Discussion Questions:

    • Why might there be few specific moa stories in Māori oral tradition?
    • How do we respectfully use oral histories in scientific investigation?
    • What can oral traditions tell us that archaeological evidence cannot?
    • How should researchers work with iwi when studying Māori history?

    Key Point: Oral histories and scientific evidence can complement each other when used respectfully and carefully.

    📊 Assessment Rubric

    Question Formulation (4 points):

    • 4 - Excellent: Questions probe deeply, show understanding of different expertise areas, build on evidence
    • 3 - Good: Questions are relevant and show some depth, connect to evidence
    • 2 - Developing: Questions are basic but appropriate, some connection to evidence
    • 1 - Beginning: Questions are superficial or disconnected from evidence

    Evidence Analysis (4 points):

    • 4 - Excellent: Evaluates evidence quality, identifies patterns, acknowledges limitations
    • 3 - Good: Shows understanding of evidence, makes some connections
    • 2 - Developing: Basic understanding of evidence, few connections made
    • 1 - Beginning: Little analysis of evidence quality or reliability
