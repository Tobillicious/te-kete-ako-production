    Lesson 1: He aha te Whakaaro Māramatanga?

    🌿 Cultural Context | Horopaki Ahurea

    Whakataukī

    "Mā te mōhio ka ora, mā te ora ka mōhio"

    "Through knowledge comes wellbeing, through wellbeing comes knowledge"

    🏛️ Connection to Mangakōtukutuku College Values

    Whaiora: Wellbeing - Caring for collective health and prosperity

    This learning connects to the value of whaiora by encouraging students to develop thinking skills that promote individual and collective wellbeing through reasoned decision-making and respectful dialogue.

    🛡️ Cultural Safety Considerations

    • Approach Māori content with respect and openness
    • Recognize students' diverse cultural backgrounds and experiences
    • Create space for Māori students to share their perspectives
    • Consult with whānau and local iwi for culturally sensitive topics

    📚 Ngā Whāinga Ako | Learning Objectives

    NZ Curriculum: Phase 3-4 (Years 7-8) | Adaptable for: Years 8-8

    WALT (We Are Learning To) | Ngā Whāinga:

    • Define critical thinking and explain its importance in daily life
    • Analyze Māori proverbs (whakataukī) to identify logical reasoning
    • Distinguish between good and poor reasoning in real-world examples
    • Connect critical thinking to both te ao Māori and Western perspectives

    Success Criteria (WILF - What I'm Looking For) | Ngā Paearu:

    • I can define critical thinking in my own words using English or te reo Māori
    • I can explain the meaning of at least one whakataukī and its connection to reasoning
    • I can give examples of both strong and weak reasoning from daily life
    • I can explain how both Māori and Western traditions value careful thinking

    🌿 Ngā Tikanga Ako | Māori Pedagogical Approaches

    This resource lends itself to authentic Māori pedagogical practices:

    • Ako: Reciprocal teaching and learning - students and teachers learn together
    • Tuakana-teina: Peer teaching with more experienced students supporting others
    • Wānanga: Deep collaborative discussion and knowledge sharing
    • Mahi tahi: Working together towards shared understanding

    💡 Teacher: Consider how these approaches can be authentically integrated into your lesson delivery.

    💡 Differentiation: Look for related resources at different NZ Curriculum phases to provide appropriate scaffolding for diverse ākonga. Consider creating learning pathways that connect resources at increasing complexity.

    What is Critical Thinking?

    Year 8 Critical Thinking Unit | 45 minutes

    Students explore critical thinking through Māori and Pākehā perspectives

    📝 Materials Needed

    • Whiteboard/markers
    • Student worksheets (printable below)
    • Chromebooks/tablets for Google Jamboard
    • Māori proverb cards
    • Examples of good/poor reasoning scenarios

    👨‍🏫 Teacher Preparation Notes

    Key Concept: Critical thinking involves analyzing arguments, questioning assumptions, and making reasoned judgments.

    Cultural Connection: Traditional Māori decision-making used careful consideration of multiple perspectives (whakatōhea - gathering to discuss).

    Vocabulary:

    • Whakaaro (thought/thinking)
    • Māramatanga (understanding/enlightenment)
    • Pātai (question)
    • Whakataukī (proverbs)

    Lesson Structure

    🚀 Starter Activity 10 minutes

    Quick Think-Pair-Share:

    Question: "When did you last change your mind about something important? What made you change it?"

    Teacher Script:

    "Today we're going to explore how we think about thinking itself. In te ao Māori, our ancestors spent hours in hui (gatherings) carefully considering all sides of important decisions. Let's see what critical thinking means to you."

    Instructions:

    1. Think individually (2 minutes)
    2. Share with partner (3 minutes)
    3. Volunteer pairs share with class (5 minutes)

    📜 Main Activity: Whakataukī Analysis 20 minutes

    Māori Proverb Investigation

    Focus Proverb: "He kōrero, he kōrero; he kai, he kai"

    Translation: "Words are words; food is food" (Words have consequences - choose them carefully)

    Student Task (Groups of 3-4):

    1. Read the proverb and translation
    2. Discuss: What does this teach about thinking before speaking?
    3. Find a modern example where words had serious consequences
    4. Connect to critical thinking: How does this proverb show good reasoning?

    Extension Proverbs:

    • "Kia whakatōmuri te haere whakamua" - "Walk backwards into the future" (Learn from the past)
    • "He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tangata, he tangata, he tangata" - "What is the most important thing? People, people, people"

    Teacher Facilitation Tips:

    • Walk between groups, asking guiding questions
    • Encourage connections to social media, news, politics
    • Help students see how proverbs contain logical reasoning
    • Emphasize both Māori and Western critical thinking traditions

    💻 Digital Activity: Google Jamboard 10 minutes

    Good vs. Poor Reasoning Examples

    Instructions:

    1. Open Google Jamboard (teacher shares link)
    2. Two columns: "Good Reasoning" and "Poor Reasoning"
    3. Students add sticky notes with examples from their lives
    4. Examples: Social media posts, family decisions, school choices, news stories

    Prompting Questions:

    • "What makes some arguments stronger than others?"
    • "How do you know when someone is using good evidence?"
    • "What are warning signs of poor thinking?"

    📝 Wrap-up & Definition 5 minutes

    Class Discussion:

    "Based on our activities today, how would you define critical thinking? Use both English and te reo Māori if you can."

    Expected Student Responses:

    • Thinking carefully before deciding
    • Asking good questions
    • Looking at evidence
    • Considering different viewpoints
    • Learning from the past (whakapapa)

    Teacher Summary:

    "Critical thinking is the skill of analyzing arguments, questioning assumptions, and making reasoned judgments. In te ao Māori, this connects to whakatōhea - carefully gathering different perspectives before making important decisions."

    📊 Assessment: Exit Ticket

    Students complete before leaving:

    1. Define critical thinking in your own words (English or te reo Māori)
    2. Give one example of good critical thinking from today's lesson
    3. Name one situation where you could use better critical thinking

    Assessment Rubric:

    • Excellent (4): Clear definition, strong example, specific personal application
    • Good (3): Good definition, relevant example, general application
    • Developing (2): Basic understanding, unclear example
    • Beginning (1): Minimal understanding shown

    👥 Peer Assessment Protocol

    Tuakana-Teina Approach:

    1. Two stars: What did they do well?
    2. One wish: What could be improved?
    3. Connection: How does this relate to our learning?

    Encourage manaakitanga (respect) in all feedback

    • Photos of practical work
    • Peer feedback received
