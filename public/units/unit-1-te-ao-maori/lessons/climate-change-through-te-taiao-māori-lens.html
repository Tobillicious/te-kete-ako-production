Climate Change Through Te Taiao Māori Lens

Year Levels: 10-12 | Subject: Science | Duration: 3-4 lessons

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand climate change through both scientific and Māori worldviews
  • Explore the concept of kaitiakitanga (guardianship) in environmental management
  • Analyze how traditional Māori knowledge (mātauranga Māori) informs climate adaptation
  • Develop strategies for sustainable living based on indigenous knowledge
  • Connect NZC Science achievement objectives to Māori environmental perspectives

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori Considerations

This unit integrates Te Ao Māori (Māori worldview) with climate science. Key concepts include:

  • Whakapapa - The interconnectedness of all living things
  • Mauri - The life force present in all elements of nature
  • Kaitiakitanga - Guardianship and sustainable management of resources
  • Mana whenua - The authority and rights of local iwi over their land

Cultural Safety Note: When discussing Māori knowledge systems:

  • Always acknowledge the source of any traditional knowledge
  • Recognize that some knowledge may be tapu (sacred) to specific iwi
  • Consult with local kaumātua (elders) where possible
  • Use te reo Māori terms correctly and respectfully

Understanding Climate Change Through Dual Lenses

Activity 1: Comparing Worldviews

Compare these two perspectives on climate change:

Western Science Perspective Te Ao Māori Perspective
Focuses on greenhouse gas emissions and temperature data Views climate as part of Papatūānuku (Earth Mother) - a living system
Seeks technological solutions Emphasizes balance and reciprocal relationships (utu)
Uses computer modeling for predictions Relies on generations of environmental observations (tohu)

Discussion Questions:

  1. How might these perspectives complement each other?
  2. What traditional Māori practices could inform modern climate solutions?
  3. How does the concept of whakapapa change how we view environmental impacts?

Activity 2: Traditional Environmental Indicators

Māori ancestors used natural signs (tohu) to predict weather and seasonal changes:

Plant Indicators

Flowering of kōwhai signaled time to plant kūmara

Animal Behavior

Tītī (muttonbird) migration patterns indicated seasonal changes

Lunar Cycles

Maramataka (Māori lunar calendar) guided planting and fishing

Task: Research how climate change is affecting these traditional indicators. Create a brief report comparing historical observations with current changes.

Extension Activities

1. Local Iwi Research

Investigate how your local iwi/hapū are responding to climate change. What specific challenges do they face?

2. Maramataka Project

Create a modern version of the Māori lunar calendar that incorporates climate change observations.

3. Comparative Study

Research how other indigenous cultures (e.g., Inuit, Aboriginal Australians) understand climate change.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge:

  • Students should have basic understanding of climate change science
  • Familiarity with some Māori concepts is helpful but not essential

Differentiation Strategies:

  • Support: Provide vocabulary lists with definitions of Māori terms
  • Extension: Have students interview local Māori environmental experts

Cultural Considerations:

  • Be aware that some students may have whānau connections to environmental issues
  • Consider inviting a local kaumātua or Māori environmental expert to speak
  • Always pronounce Māori words correctly - practice beforehand if needed
