Lesson 1: Data Sovereignty – Who Owns Your Data?

Whakataukī

"He tangata takahi manuhiri, he marae puehu."

A person who mistreats guests has a dusty marae.

Application: When tech companies extract data without permission or benefit-sharing, they violate digital hospitality and reciprocity.

Learning Objectives

  • Define data sovereignty and distinguish from data privacy/security.
  • Explain why Indigenous data sovereignty is a unique issue (collective rights, cultural context, historical exploitation).
  • Analyse real-world cases where Māori data control was at stake.
  • Articulate the CARE Principles framework.

Hook: The Hidden Data Economy (10 min)

Show students: "Your phone tracks your location 24/7. Your search history is sold to advertisers. Your face is in facial recognition databases."

Questions:

  • Who owns this data?
  • Who profits from it?
  • Can you delete it?
  • If data about you feels invasive, imagine data about your entire iwi, whakapapa, or sacred sites...

Core Concepts (20 min)

Data Sovereignty vs. Data Privacy

  • Privacy: Individual right to control personal information.
  • Security: Technical protection against breaches.
  • Sovereignty: Collective governance rights—Indigenous nations decide how data about their people/lands/culture is collected, stored, used, and shared.

Why It Matters for Māori

  • Historical extraction of knowledge without consent (museums, genetics, land records).
  • Health data used without benefit-sharing → inequitable outcomes.
  • AI trained on Māori language/culture without iwi permission or control.
  • Census/government data collected about Māori but not governed by Māori.

Case Study 1: Genomic Research Controversy

In the early 2000s, researchers collected DNA from Māori participants for genetic studies. Later, this data was used for research that Māori communities had not consented to, including studies that reinforced harmful stereotypes. This violated tino rangatiratanga (self-determination) over biological and cultural data.

Discussion: What should have happened? Who should control genetic data? How can research benefit communities, not just extract from them?

Case Study 2: Stats NZ Iwi Data

After the 2018 Census, Stats NZ planned to share iwi-level data with researchers. Iwi leaders protested: "This is our data about our people. We should decide how it's used." The result: new data governance partnerships giving iwi more control.

Task: Students research current Stats NZ Māori data governance policies. What changed? What's still contested?

CARE Principles Framework

Developed by Global Indigenous Data Alliance to complement FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) principles:

  • C – Collective Benefit: Data use should benefit Indigenous communities, not just researchers.
  • A – Authority to Control: Indigenous peoples govern data ecosystems (who accesses, how it's used).
  • R – Responsibility: Those handling data have obligations to do no harm and respect cultural protocols.
  • E – Ethics: Data practices align with Indigenous values, rights, and wellbeing.

Activity: Use CARE Checklist (Coming soon) to evaluate a digital platform (e.g., ancestry website, health tracker, social media).

⚡ Why This Matters NOW
  • AI systems (ChatGPT, facial recognition) are being trained on Māori language/culture data—often without permission.
  • Government agencies increasingly use algorithms to make decisions (welfare, justice, health)—are they fair to Māori?
  • Social media companies monetise user data—Indigenous communities get no share of profits from their cultural content.

Students will inherit this digital landscape. They need skills to critique it and change it.

🧑‍🏫 Teacher PD Note

This is a politically and emotionally charged topic. Frame as critical digital literacy, not political advocacy, but don't shy from real issues. Centre student voice—especially Māori students who may have direct experiences with data misuse or identity exploitation. Provide opt-out options for sensitive discussions. Link to Te Mana Raraunga resources and consider inviting a Māori data scientist or digital rights advocate as guest speaker.
