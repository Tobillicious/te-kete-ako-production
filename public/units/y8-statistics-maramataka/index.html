Y8 Statistics: Māramataka—Data in the Night Sky

Explore how ancestors used the lunar calendar (māramataka) to predict patterns in nature, fishing, planting—then apply statistical thinking to contemporary data sets grounded in te ao Māori.

Kaupapa | Big Ideas

  • Patterns reveal knowledge: Māramataka tracked lunar phases, tides, weather, and seasonal activities—this is data science.
  • Statistics tell stories: Numbers represent real phenomena; cultural context shapes interpretation.
  • Prediction requires observation: Both mātauranga and statistics use past patterns to inform future decisions.
NZ Curriculum L4 Statistics Data Literacy Patterns & Relationships

Learning Outcomes

  • Interpret māramataka (lunar calendar) as a pattern-recognition system.
  • Collect, organise, and display data using tables, bar graphs, line graphs.
  • Calculate mean, median, mode, and range from cultural data sets.
  • Analyse trends and make evidence-based predictions.
  • Critique data representations for bias or missing cultural context.

Assessment

  • Investigation: Māramataka moon phase data + local phenomenon (tides, fish catch, plant growth)
  • Graph Portfolio: Display same data 3 ways; justify best choice
  • Statistical Report: Analyse cultural data set (e.g., iwi population trends, treaty settlements, te reo speaker numbers) with commentary

Rubric: statistical accuracy + cultural interpretation.

Unit Sequence (5 weeks)

L1: Māramataka—The Lunar Calendar

Introduce māramataka phases (Tangaroa, Rongo, Whiro etc.); connect to tides, fishing, planting.

L2: Collecting Cultural Data

Survey design; gather moon phase + outcome data (student-led investigation).

L3: Tables, Bar Graphs, Line Graphs

Organise māramataka data; choose appropriate displays; critique examples.

L4: Mean, Median, Mode

Calculate central tendency using cultural data sets (fish catch by moon phase).

L5: Trends & Predictions

Identify patterns in time-series data; make predictions; compare to traditional knowledge.

L6: Data Bias & Cultural Context

Analyse how data can misrepresent Māori (census issues, health stats); advocate for better data sovereignty.

Teacher PD Notes

  • Māramataka variation: Different iwi use different systems (28-30 days, varying names). Use a general model or consult local iwi for specific calendar.
  • Validation: Don't claim māramataka is "just astrology" or "primitive." It's empirical observation refined over centuries—statistical thinking before statistics existed.
  • Contemporary use: Many Māori still use māramataka for fishing, planting, health; it's not historical curiosity.
  • Data ethics: When using Māori demographic data, discuss sovereignty and respectful interpretation.

🌙 Māramataka Essentials

The lunar calendar divides the month into phases, each with characteristics:

  • Tangaroa: Moon full/bright → good for fishing, planting above-ground crops
  • Rongo: Peaceful, calm → ideal for delicate work, learning
  • Whiro: Moon dark → poor for important tasks; rest and reflect
  • Tāmatea variants: Various productive/unproductive days

Students investigate: "Does fish catch actually vary by moon phase?" They collect data, graph it, and compare to traditional predictions.

📊 Cultural Data Sets to Explore

  • Stats NZ iwi population data (1991-2023 trends)
  • Te reo Māori speaker numbers by age group
  • Treaty settlement $ amounts & dates (patterns over time)
  • Māori representation in university enrolments (STEM vs Arts)
  • Coastal fishing yields by season & location (link to māramataka predictions)

All data sets must be used respectfully—discuss who collected it, for what purpose, and how Māori benefit (or don't) from this data.
