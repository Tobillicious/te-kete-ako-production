Lesson 1: Te Māramataka—Reading Patterns in the Moon

Whakataukī

"Te Mārama e whiti ana, he tohu tēnā."

The shining moon, that is a sign.

The moon guides decision-making—this lesson explores how observing lunar patterns creates knowledge.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the māramataka system and its purpose (agricultural, fishing, health, spiritual timing).
  • Identify key moon phases and their traditional associations.
  • Recognize māramataka as empirical pattern observation (data science).
  • Design a simple data collection investigation linking moon phases to a local phenomenon.

Hook: Moon Phase Mystery (10 min)

Show students: 3 months of data on fish catches from a Māori fishing collective. Catches vary dramatically—some days huge, others poor.

Question: "What pattern do you see? What might explain it?"

Students hypothesise. THEN reveal: The data aligns with moon phases. Introduce māramataka as the traditional system for predicting this.

Māramataka Basics (20 min)

What is Māramataka?

The Māori lunar calendar dividing the month into ~30 named nights, each with distinct energy/characteristics. Used to guide:

  • Fishing (best catch on Tangaroa nights)
  • Planting (above-ground crops on waxing moon, root crops on waning)
  • Health & wellbeing (Te Whare Tapa Whā aligns with moon cycles)
  • Spiritual practices (karakia, ceremonies)

Key Phases (Simplified Model)

Moon PhaseName (example)Characteristics
New MoonWhiroDark, rest, reflect; poor for planting/fishing
Waxing (growing)Tū/Rākau variantsBuilding energy; plant above-ground crops
Full MoonTangaroaBright, abundant; excellent fishing, communal activity
Waning (shrinking)Rongo/Tāmatea variantsCalm, contemplative; plant root crops

Note: Iwi variations exist; this is a teaching model. Always cite sources and acknowledge diversity of practice.

Class Investigation Design (15 min)

Question: "Does the moon phase actually affect [local phenomenon]?"

Options to investigate:

  • Local fishing yields (partner with whānau who fish)
  • Tide heights (use LINZ tide data)
  • Plant growth (class bean experiment over 4 weeks)
  • Student mood/energy (self-report survey—fun but subjective!)

Students decide: What data will we collect? How? Over how long? (Use Investigation Planner)

Homework: Moon Phase Observation

Over 1 week, observe and sketch the moon each night. Note the phase. Predict: What māramataka phase is it? Use a māramataka resource to check.

Reflection: How might tracking this pattern help with planning activities or understanding natural cycles?

🧑‍🏫 Teacher PD Note

Māramataka is experiencing a revival—many Māori communities are reclaiming this knowledge for wellbeing, education, and connection to te taiao. Invite a local kaumātua or māramataka practitioner to co-teach if possible. Emphasize this is living, current knowledge, not historical artifact. Frame statistics lessons as learning why māramataka works (pattern recognition, data-driven prediction).
