Years of Anger: The Protest Movements

Unit: Walker - The Challenge to the Narrative | Lesson 1.3

Year Level: Year 10 | Subject: Social Studies, History, Civics | Duration: 75 minutes

⚡ School Value: Whaiara (Rising Up)

This lesson explores how Māori activists in the 1970s demonstrated Whaiara by rising up against injustice, using their voices to demand change, and challenging systems that oppressed their people.

⚠️ Cultural Sensitivity Notice for Teachers

This lesson deals with protest, anger, and conflict. Some students may have whānau who were activists; others may have whānau with different perspectives. Create a respectful space for diverse viewpoints. Present activism as a response to genuine injustice, not as "troublemaking."

📖 Lesson Overview

This lesson examines the Māori protest movements of the 1970s, focusing on Ngā Tamatoa (The Young Warriors) and other activist groups. Students will explore the causes of Māori anger and activism, analyze the strategies and goals of these movements, and understand Dr. Ranginui Walker's role as both participant and chronicler of this pivotal period in New Zealand history.

🎯 WALT (We Are Learning To)

We are learning to understand the causes and consequences of the Māori protest movements of the 1970s.

✅ Success Criteria (I will be successful if I can...)

  • ✓ Identify at least three reasons why Māori activists protested in the 1970s
  • ✓ Describe the strategies and goals of Ngā Tamatoa and other protest groups
  • ✓ Explain Dr. Ranginui Walker's role in and perspective on these movements
  • ✓ Analyze the effectiveness of protest as a tool for social change
  • ✓ Connect this topic to the school value of Whaiara (Rising Up)

📚 Key Vocabulary

  • Activism: Vigorous campaigning to bring about political or social change
  • Protest: A statement or action expressing disapproval of or objection to something
  • Ngā Tamatoa: "The Young Warriors" - a Māori activist group founded in 1970
  • Civil Disobedience: The active refusal to obey certain laws as a form of peaceful protest
  • Tino Rangatiratanga: Self-determination, sovereignty, autonomy

🔗 Curriculum Connections

New Zealand Curriculum - Social Sciences

  • Achievement Objective: Level 5 - "Understand how the ideas and actions of people in the past have had a significant impact on people's lives"
  • Achievement Objective (Civics): Level 5 - "Understand how systems of government in New Zealand operate and affect people's lives"

Cross-Curricular Links:

  • English: Analyzing persuasive texts (protest slogans, speeches, manifestos)
  • Media Studies: Examining how the media portrayed Māori activism

🛠️ Resources & Preparation

Teacher Preparation:

  • Print copies of the "Ngā Tamatoa: The Young Warriors" handout (1 per student)
  • Prepare images/posters from 1970s Māori protests (Waitangi Day protests, Land March 1975, etc.)
  • (Optional) Find video clips of protests or interviews with activists from this era
  • Important: Review content with cultural sensitivity - some students may have whānau who participated in these protests

⭐ WAGOLL (What A Good One Looks Like)

Model Graphic Organizer:

Grievances (What were they angry about?) Strategies (How did they protest?) Goals (What did they want?)
Land confiscation and loss of ancestral lands Protests at Waitangi Day celebrations Return of confiscated lands
Te reo Māori dying - only 26% of Māori could speak it by 1970 Petitions to government for te reo in schools Official status for Māori language
Poverty and discrimination in urban areas Marches and demonstrations Equal rights and opportunities

Model "Connecting to Whaiara" Paragraph:

"The protest movements of the 1970s powerfully demonstrated the value of Whaiara (Rising Up). When Māori activists saw injustice - land loss, language decline, systemic discrimination - they chose to rise up rather than accept the status quo. Groups like Ngā Tamatoa used marches, petitions, and occupations to demand change. While not all protests achieved immediate results, they were effective in raising public awareness and eventually leading to the Waitangi Tribunal and the Māori Language Act. Protest can be effective when it's organized, persistent, and focused on clear goals. However, it requires courage and often involves personal sacrifice, as many activists faced arrest and social criticism. The effectiveness of protest depends on whether those in power are willing to listen and change."

⏱️ Lesson Sequence (75 minutes)

Time Activity Description & Teacher Guidance
0-5 min DO NOW
Whakawhanaungatanga		 Greet students. DO NOW: "When you see something unfair, do you: (a) stay silent, (b) speak up, or (c) take action? Explain your choice." Students write 2-3 sentences.
5-15 min Explicit Teaching
Direct Instruction		 Introduce WALT and Success Criteria. Use "Do Now" to introduce the concept of activism. Explain that by the 1970s, urban Māori (from Lesson 1.2) faced ongoing discrimination, poverty, and loss of cultural identity. Show images from protests. Introduce Ngā Tamatoa: founded at Auckland University in 1970, led by young Māori demanding change. Explain Dr. Walker was both participant and historian of this movement - his column "Years of Anger" documented this era.
15-40 min Guided Practice
Student Activity		 Distribute "Ngā Tamatoa: The Young Warriors" handout. Students read about the group's formation, goals, and actions. In small groups (3-4), students complete a graphic organizer with three sections: 1) Grievances (What were they angry about?), 2) Strategies (How did they protest?), 3) Goals (What did they want to achieve?). Groups share findings with class. Teacher facilitates discussion on effectiveness of different strategies.
40-60 min Independent Practice
Application		 Connecting to Whaiara: Students write a paragraph answering: "How did the protest movements of the 1970s demonstrate the value of Whaiara (Rising Up)? Do you think protest is an effective way to create change?" Show WAGOLL paragraph before they begin. Encourage students to use specific examples from the lesson and to consider both successes and limitations of the protests.
60-75 min Plenary
Wrap-up		 Review WALT and Success Criteria. Class discussion: "Dr. Walker wrote that the 1970s were 'Years of Anger.' Was this anger justified? Was it productive?" Exit ticket: "What is one question you have about the Māori protest movements that you want to explore further?"

🎯 Differentiated Instruction & Extension

💚 For Students Who Need Extra Support:

  • Provide a partially completed graphic organizer with some grievances, strategies, and goals already filled in
  • Offer sentence starters: "The protest movements demonstrated Whaiara by ___. I think protest is/is not effective because ___."
  • Pair with a peer mentor for the group activity

🔥 For Students Who Need a Challenge:

  • Research a specific protest event (e.g., 1975 Land March, Bastion Point occupation, Raglan golf course protest) and create a timeline or presentation
  • Compare Māori activism in the 1970s to other civil rights movements (e.g., American Civil Rights Movement, South African anti-apartheid movement)
  • Read excerpts from Dr. Walker's "Years of Anger" columns and analyze his perspective on the protests
  • Debate: "Is civil disobedience ever justified?"

📊 Assessment

Formative Assessment:

  • Graphic organizer completion (Grievances/Strategies/Goals)
  • Quality of group discussion and class participation
  • "Connecting to Whaiara" paragraph
  • Exit ticket responses

Summative Assessment:

This lesson builds critical understanding for Lesson 1.4 (Waitangi Tribunal) and Lesson 1.5 (Ka Whawhai Tonu Mātou). The teacher should assess whether students grasp the connection between activism and systemic change.

🧑‍🏫 Kaiako Notes & Reflections

Culturally Responsive Pedagogy:

  • Critical: This lesson deals with protest, anger, and conflict. Some students may have whānau who were activists; others may have whānau with different perspectives. Create a respectful space for diverse viewpoints
  • Framing: Present activism as a response to genuine injustice, not as "troublemaking." Emphasize the courage and commitment of protesters
  • Student Voice: If students have family stories about this era, invite them to share (but don't pressure anyone)
  • Balance: Acknowledge that protest was controversial and not universally supported, even within Māori communities. Teach students to analyze multiple perspectives
  • Connection to Present: Help students see the legacy of 1970s activism in contemporary Māori movements (e.g., te reo Māori revitalization, Treaty settlements)

Connection to Unit Theme:

This lesson is pivotal: it shows how Dr. Walker moved from observing history to participating in it. His "Years of Anger" column gave voice to the protest generation and challenged the dominant narrative that Māori should be grateful and passive.

Post-Lesson Reflection:

  • Did students understand why Māori were angry in the 1970s?
  • Were they able to analyze protest strategies critically (not just accept or reject them wholesale)?
  • How did students respond to the concept of "justified anger"?
  • Did the exit tickets reveal genuine curiosity about the protest movements?
  • Were any students uncomfortable with the content? How can I support them?

Teacher Preparation Note:

Consider inviting a guest speaker who participated in 1970s activism (or showing a video interview) to make this history personal and real for students. Ensure cultural protocols are followed for any guest visitors.

Historical Context:

The 1970s protests led directly to the establishment of the Waitangi Tribunal (Lesson 1.4) and broader recognition of Māori rights. Help students see this lesson as part of a larger story of struggle and change.
