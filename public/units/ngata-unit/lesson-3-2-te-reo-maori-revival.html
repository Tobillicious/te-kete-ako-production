Te Reo Māori Revival: Saving a Language

Unit: Ngata - The Renaissance Leader | Lesson 3.2

Year 10 | Social Studies, Te Reo Māori | 75 minutes

🏛️ School Value: Whaiora (Wellbeing)

Language is central to cultural wellbeing. Ngata understood that without te reo Māori, Māori identity and connection to ancestors would be lost.

Overview: By the early 1900s, te reo Māori was in serious decline. Urban migration, English-only schools, and government policies were rapidly eroding the language. Ngata recognized this as an existential threat and dedicated decades to preservation through his monumental work collecting mōteatea (traditional chants and songs) and promoting language use.

🎯 WALT: We are learning to understand how Ngata's work helped save te reo Māori from near-extinction

✅ Success Criteria:

  • Explain why te reo Māori was declining in the early 1900s
  • Describe what mōteatea are and why they're culturally significant
  • Analyze how Ngā Mōteatea collection preserved language and culture
  • Connect language preservation to Whaiora (wellbeing)

🚨 The Crisis: Te Reo in Decline (1900-1950)

Why Was Te Reo Māori Dying?

1. Urban Migration

As Māori moved to cities for work (see previous unit on Walker), they left behind the rural communities where te reo was still spoken daily. Cities were English-speaking environments.

2. English-Only Schools

From 1867, Native Schools taught only in English. Children were punished for speaking te reo. By 1900s, most Māori children grew up speaking English as their first language.

3. Loss of Kaumātua (Elders)

The last generation of native speakers - those who grew up speaking ONLY te reo - were aging and dying. With them, thousands of years of oral tradition, songs, and knowledge were disappearing.

⚠️ By 1950: Less than 25% of Māori could speak te reo fluently

📚 Ngata's Response: Ngā Mōteatea (The Ancient Songs)

What Are Mōteatea?

Mōteatea are traditional Māori chants - poetic songs that tell stories, preserve history, express emotions, and transmit cultural knowledge. They're NOT just "old songs" - they're:

  • 📖 Historical records - documenting migrations, battles, alliances
  • 🗺️ Geographical knowledge - describing landscapes, place names, resources
  • 💚 Emotional expression - love songs, laments, celebrations
  • 📚 Language repositories - preserving archaic vocabulary, poetic forms, proper pronunciation
  • 🌿 Cultural values - teaching tikanga, whakapapa, relationships

The Urgency:

"These songs were passed down orally for generations. If the kaumātua who knew them died without someone recording them, THOUSANDS of years of Māori knowledge would disappear forever. Ngata realized: It's now or never."

⛰️ The Monumental Work: 20+ Years of Collection

📖 Volume 1 (1928)

Songs of love, lamentation, and celebration - 200+ mōteatea collected

📖 Volume 2 (1961)

Historical and tribal songs - completed by Pei Te Hurinui Jones after Ngata's death

📖 Volume 3 (1970)

Songs of canoe migrations and tribal histories - over 1000 mōteatea total across all volumes

🏆 Result: Over 1,000 traditional songs preserved in written form with translations and annotations

🎵 Activity: Listen & Analyze a Mōteatea (30 minutes)

Instructions:

Your teacher will play a recording of a mōteatea from Ngata's collection. Listen carefully, then work through this analysis:

Analysis Questions:

  1. First Impressions: How does it make you feel? What emotions do you hear in the voice/melody?
  2. Language: Can you pick out any te reo Māori words you recognize? (Even if it's just "e" or "ki" - that counts!)
  3. Content (after teacher explains): What story is being told? Who/what is it about?
  4. Cultural Significance: What might this mōteatea have been used for? (e.g., remembering ancestors, teaching history, expressing grief)
  5. Preservation Value: What would have been lost if Ngata hadn't recorded this? Why does it matter?

💡 Teacher Tip:

Recommended mōteatea: "Pōkarekare Ana" (familiar melody) or "Ka Mate" (haka, recognizable). Play audio from maoridictionary.co.nz

🌿 Beyond Mōteatea: Ngata's Other Language Work

📖 Translating the Bible

Worked on improved Māori translation of the Bible - important because many Māori were Christian and this kept te reo alive in churches

🏫 Promoting Te Reo in Schools

Advocated for te reo Māori to be taught in schools, though limited success during his lifetime - planted seeds for Kōhanga Reo movement later

🎙️ Radio Broadcasts

Used new technology (radio) to broadcast te reo and traditional songs - reaching Māori across the country

📝 Writing & Publishing

Wrote articles, speeches, and books in te reo Māori - showing the language was sophisticated enough for modern topics

🔗 Connection to Modern Language Revival

Ngata's Work Laid the Foundation For:

1970s-80s: Kōhanga Reo (Language Nests)

1987: Te Reo Becomes Official Language

1990s-2000s: Kura Kaupapa Māori

Today: Te Wiki o te Reo Māori

📊 Assessment: Language = Wellbeing Essay (20 minutes)

Writing Task:

Write a 200-250 word essay answering this question:
"How did Ngata's work to save te reo Māori connect to Whaiora (Wellbeing)? Why is language essential to cultural wellbeing?"

Structure Your Essay:

  • Introduction: State your main argument (language IS essential to wellbeing because...)
  • Evidence 1: What happens when a language dies? (loss of identity, connection to ancestors, cultural knowledge)
  • Evidence 2: What specific work did Ngata do? (Ngā Mōteatea, promoting in schools, etc.)
  • Connection to Whaiora: Explain how language preservation = cultural wellbeing
  • Conclusion: Why does Ngata's work still matter today?

🎵 Example Mōteatea: "E Kore Koe e Ngaro"

"E kore koe e ngaro he kākano i ruia mai i Rangiātea"

You will never be lost, for you are a seed sown from Rangiātea (the ancestral homeland)

— This whakataukī, preserved by Ngata, reminds Māori that their identity is eternal and rooted in deep ancestry

Research ONE modern te reo Māori revitalization initiative and create a comparison poster:

  • Kōhanga Reo (language nests)

👩‍🏫 Teacher Implementation Notes

Lesson Sequence (75 min):

  • 0-10 min: Review Lesson 3.1, introduce language decline crisis
  • 10-25 min: Teach about mōteatea (what they are, why important)
  • 25-40 min: Listen to mōteatea, complete analysis activity
  • 40-45 min: Explain Ngā Mōteatea volumes and Ngata's collection process
  • 45-65 min: Students write Whaiora essay
  • 65-75 min: Share essays, connect to modern language revival

Resources Needed:

  • Audio recording of a mōteatea (YouTube or maoridictionary.co.nz)
  • Device to play audio for whole class
  • Printed lyrics/translation (if available)
  • Writing materials for essay

Cultural Considerations:

  • Some mōteatea are tapu (sacred) or belong to specific iwi - choose general ones suitable for classroom
  • Explain context before playing - show respect for the tradition
  • If you have fluent te reo speakers in class, invite them to share (but don't pressure)
