Hook: The Pattern Gallery Walk (10 min)

Display images of k≈çwhaiwhai from different wharenui around Aotearoa (use Te Ara Encyclopedia or similar).

Students observe silently, then pair-share:

  • What do you notice? (colours, shapes, repetition)
  • What patterns or rules do you see?
  • What might these designs represent?

Concept Build: Symmetry & Reflection (20 min)

Line Symmetry (Reflection)

A shape has line symmetry if you can fold it along a line and both halves match perfectly. The fold line is the axis of symmetry.

Demo: Show a koru motif. Draw the vertical axis. Fold paper‚Äîboth sides mirror.

Activity: Students use Symmetry Worksheet to:

  1. Identify axes of symmetry in 4 k≈çwhaiwhai images.
  2. Draw the line(s) of symmetry on each image.
  3. Count: How many axes? (some have 1, some have multiple)

Guided Practice: Draw a Koru (15 min)

  1. Teacher models: Draw a koru (spiral) on one side of a vertical line.
  2. Reflect across the line to create symmetric pair.
  3. Students try on grid paper, then check with a mirror or by folding.

Differentiation: Provide tracing templates for students who need support; challenge advanced students to add colour/shading symmetrically.

Mathematical Vocabulary

  • Symmetry: Balance; identical on both sides.
  • Line of symmetry: The "mirror line."
  • Reflection: Flip over a line.
  • Congruent: Same shape and size.

Cultural Vocabulary

  • K≈çwhaiwhai: Painted rafter patterns.
  • Koru: Spiral (new life, growth).
  • Pitau: Unfurling fern frond.
  • Mangopare: Hammerhead shark (strength, determination).
  • Wharenui: Meeting house.

Success Criteria

  • I can identify at least one line of symmetry in a k≈çwhaiwhai pattern.
  • I can draw a simple symmetric koru using reflection.
  • I can explain what my pattern represents (mathematical AND cultural reasoning).
