🏔️ Ko Wai Au? Building Your Pepeha

Year Level: 10+ | Subject: Te Reo Māori | Duration: 50 minutes

Context: Cultural Identity & Language Foundations

🌿 Whakataukī - Proverb

"Poua te kōhatu, whakatōkia te rākau, me pēhea ai a Tāne?"

"The stone is set, the tree is planted, but what of the people?"

Our identity is connected to our land, our people, and our whakapapa. A pepeha helps us understand where we belong in the world.

🎯 Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Objectives

  • Understand the structure and purpose of pepeha in Māori culture
  • Learn key vocabulary for describing geographical and personal connections
  • Create a personal pepeha reflecting individual identity and whakapapa
  • Practice pronunciation and presentation skills in te reo Māori
  • Develop cultural understanding of the importance of place and ancestry

📖 Lesson Structure - Hanganga Akoranga

🌅 Whakatuwera - Opening (10 minutes)

Whakapapa Warm-up

Question for reflection: "What places are important to you? What mountains, rivers, or areas make you feel connected?"

Think-Pair-Share:

  1. Think: 2 minutes silent reflection
  2. Pair: Share with partner in English
  3. Share: Few volunteers share with class

This connects students to the concept before introducing Māori structure

📚 Ako - Main Teaching (25 minutes)

Part 1: Understanding Pepeha (10 minutes)

Pepeha Example - Tauira

Ko Taranaki te maunga (Taranaki is my mountain)

Ko Whanganui te awa (Whanganui is my river)

Ko Te Āti Haunui-a-Pāpārangi te iwi (Te Āti Haunui-a-Pāpārangi is my tribe)

Ko Ngāti Rangi te hapū (Ngāti Rangi is my sub-tribe)

Ko Rānana te marae (Rānana is my marae)

Ko Tane te tangata (Tane is my person/ancestor)

Ko Mere ahau (I am Mere)

🗣️ Pronunciation Guide - Aratohu Whakahua

Ko[koh]"is" or "am"
te[teh]"the"
maunga[mah-oo-nga]mountain
awa[ah-vah]river
iwi[ee-vee]tribe
hapū[hah-poo]sub-tribe
marae[mah-rah-eh]meeting grounds
ahau[ah-hah-oo]I/me

Part 2: Pepeha Structure (8 minutes)

The Pattern - Te Ritenga

Pepeha follow a traditional structure connecting us to:

  1. Maunga (Mountain): Spiritual connection, permanence
  2. Awa/Moana (River/Sea): Life source, flow of life
  3. Iwi (Tribe): Broader cultural family
  4. Hapū (Sub-tribe): Closer kinship group
  5. Marae (Meeting place): Spiritual and social center
  6. Tipuna (Ancestor): Connection to the past
  7. Personal name: Individual identity within collective

Part 3: Creating Adaptations (7 minutes)

For Those Without Māori Whakapapa

Pepeha can be adapted respectfully for all students:

  • Use geographical features from your area
  • Include cultural background respectfully
  • Connect to places that are meaningful to you
  • Honor your own ancestors and heritage

Example adaptation:

Ko Rangitoto te maunga, Ko Waitematā te moana, Ko Tāmaki-makau-rau tōku kāinga, Ko James ahau

🎨 Whakangātahi - Practice (10 minutes)

Build Your Pepeha - Worksheet Activity

Step 1: Fill in the template (5 minutes)

Ko _____________ te maunga

Ko _____________ te awa/moana

Ko _____________ te iwi/whenua

Ko _____________ te hapū/kāinga

Ko _____________ te marae/kura

Ko _____________ tōku tipuna/whānau

Ko _____________ ahau

Step 2: Practice pronunciation with partner (3 minutes)

Step 3: Add personal touches or artistic elements (2 minutes)

🌟 Whakaatu - Sharing (5 minutes)

Voluntary Presentations

Students can choose to:

  • Present their full pepeha to the class
  • Share just their name and one connection
  • Explain one interesting choice they made
  • Simply listen respectfully to others

Always voluntary - respecting that some information may be private or still being discovered.

📚 Rauemi - Resources Needed

  • Map of New Zealand showing major geographical features
  • Audio recordings of pepeha examples (if available)
  • Pepeha worksheet templates
  • Colored pencils/pens for artistic additions
  • Cultural context information sheets
  • Pronunciation guide handouts

🎯 Differentiation - Rereketanga

🌟 Extension Activities:

  • Research the history and significance of their chosen geographical features
  • Create a visual/artistic representation of their pepeha
  • Interview whānau members about family connections to places
  • Learn about the iwi/hapū connected to their area

🤝 Support Strategies:

  • Provide completed examples from different cultural backgrounds
  • Allow use of English names for places if te reo names are unknown
  • Peer buddy system for pronunciation practice
  • Option to create shorter version focusing on key elements

🌍 Cultural Sensitivity:

  • Emphasize that this is about connection, not appropriation
  • Respect students who may not know their whakapapa
  • Include diverse cultural backgrounds respectfully
  • Provide alternatives for recent immigrants or adopted students

🏠 Homework - Mahi Kāinga

Whānau Connection Project

Option 1: Interview a family member about your family's connections to places. Add this information to your pepeha.

Option 2: Research the Māori names and histories of places in your pepeha.

Option 3: Create an artistic representation of your pepeha (drawing, collage, digital design).

Cultural Option: If you have Māori whakapapa, ask whānau to help you create a more detailed and accurate pepeha.

📊 Assessment - Aromatawai

Criteria Achieved Merit Excellence
Cultural Understanding Shows basic understanding of pepeha purpose Good understanding with personal connections Deep understanding with thoughtful, meaningful connections
Language Use Follows basic pepeha structure Good structure with clear pronunciation Excellent structure, pronunciation, and confidence
Personal Connection Makes basic personal connections Meaningful personal connections evident Deep, reflective personal connections with explanation

🔗 Cross-Curricular Links

  • Social Studies: Geography of New Zealand, cultural identity, migration patterns
  • English: Personal narrative, oral presentation skills, cultural literature
  • Art: Visual representation of identity, traditional and contemporary Māori art
  • History: Whakapapa, genealogy, family histories and migration stories

📝 Teacher Notes - Kōrero a te Kaiako

Cultural Considerations:

  • Be prepared for students who don't know their whakapapa - this is common and sensitive
  • Some students may discover meaningful information about their heritage
  • Emphasize respect and understanding rather than performance
  • Consider inviting local kaumātua or Māori language experts to assist

🌟 Extension Activities for Future Lessons

  • Marae Visit: Visit local marae to understand the physical and spiritual significance
  • Guest Speakers: Invite kaumātua to share traditional pepeha
  • Art Integration: Create visual pepeha using traditional or contemporary Māori art forms
  • Technology: Record pepeha videos for digital portfolio
  • Community Connection: Share pepeha with local iwi education coordinators

He mihi - Acknowledgment

This lesson acknowledges the importance of whakapapa and cultural identity in education. We honor all students' backgrounds while learning about Māori cultural practices with respect and understanding.

"Ehara taku toa i te toa takitahi, engari he toa takitini"

"My strength is not as an individual, but as a collective"
