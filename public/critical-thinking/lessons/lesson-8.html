    âš ï¸ Logical Fallacies: Spotting Weak Arguments (20 minutes)

    Teacher Input (12 mins): Logical fallacies are errors in reasoning that make arguments weak or misleading. Let's explore the most common ones:

    Ad Hominem

    What it is: Attacking the person instead of their argument

    Example: "You can't trust her climate change research - she's too young to understand science."

    Why it's weak: Age doesn't determine argument validity

    Straw Man

    What it is: Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack

    Example: "Environmentalists want to ban all cars and make us walk everywhere!"

    Why it's weak: Distorts the actual position

    Slippery Slope

    What it is: Claiming one action will lead to extreme consequences without evidence

    Example: "If we allow phones in class, next students will demand TVs, then they'll never study!"

    Why it's weak: Assumes inevitable chain of events

    False Dilemma

    What it is: Presenting only two options when more exist

    Example: "Either we build more prisons or crime will spiral out of control."

    Why it's weak: Ignores other solutions (prevention, rehabilitation)

    Appeal to Popularity

    What it is: Claiming something is true because many people believe it

    Example: "Everyone thinks this policy is bad, so it must be wrong."

    Why it's weak: Popularity doesn't equal truth

    Hasty Generalization

    What it is: Drawing broad conclusions from limited evidence

    Example: "I met two rude tourists, so all tourists are rude."

    Why it's weak: Sample size too small

    Is there enough evidence?

    Is the evidence correct?

    Does it actually support the claim?
