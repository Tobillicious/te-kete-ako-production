    Identifying Bias in News Reporting

    Critical Thinking Unit - Lesson 2 of 22 | Year 9-10 | Social Studies

    🎯 Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Intentions

    Students Will Learn

    • 📊 Five types of bias in news reporting (selection, framing, source, language, cultural)
    • 🌿 How Te Ao Māori values (whakapapa, manaakitanga, kaitiakitanga) apply to balanced storytelling
    • 🔍 A bias detection toolkit with five critical questions
    • 📰 How to compare mainstream and Māori media perspectives on the same issue

    Students Will Demonstrate

    • ✅ By identifying at least three types of bias in news articles with specific examples
    • ✅ By comparing mainstream and Māori media coverage of the same issue
    • ✅ By rewriting biased headlines to be more balanced while maintaining accuracy
    • ✅ By completing a 300-word comparative analysis using the bias detection toolkit

    🌿 He Whakataki | Introduction (5 minutes)

    Teacher Prompt: Building on our media analysis skills from Lesson 1, we now focus on identifying bias in news reporting. Ko te kaupapa o tēnei akoranga he aro ki ngā kōrero rongonui me te māramatanga o ngā taha rua o te kōrero. (The purpose of this lesson is to examine news stories with awareness of multiple perspectives.)

    Bias can be subtle or obvious, intentional or unintentional. In Aotearoa New Zealand, we must consider both mainstream media perspectives and Te Ao Māori (Māori world view) approaches to balanced reporting.

    Hook Question: "Can you think of a time when you read or heard about the same event from two different sources and got very different impressions? What made them different?"

    🌿 Cultural Context | Horopaki Ahurea

    Whakataukī

    "Ko te akoranga te pūtake o te ora"

    Whaimana: Integrity - Standing firm in truth and principle

    This learning connects to whaimana by empowering students to stand firm in truth and principle, critically examining media bias to uphold integrity in their understanding of news and current events.

    🛡️ Cultural Safety Considerations

    • Approach Māori content with respect and openness
    • Recognize students' diverse cultural backgrounds and experiences
    • Create space for Māori students to share their perspectives
    • Consult with whānau and local iwi for culturally sensitive topics

    📊 Ngā Momo Taha Rua | Types of Bias (15 minutes)

    Teacher Input (10 mins): Bias can appear in many forms in news reporting. Let's explore each type with examples:

    1. Selection Bias

    What it is: Choosing which stories to cover or ignore

    Example: Covering protests by one group extensively while ignoring similar actions by another

    2. Framing Bias

    What it is: How the story is presented (angle, context)

    Example: Framing a treaty settlement as "taxpayer money" vs. "justice for historical wrongs"

    3. Source Bias

    What it is: Who is quoted and who isn't

    Example: Quoting government officials but not iwi representatives in stories about Māori issues

    4. Language Bias

    What it is: Word choices that influence perception

    Example: "Protesters" vs. "Activists" vs. "Kaitiaki" - each carries different connotations

    5. Cultural Bias

    What it is: Assumptions based on dominant cultural norms

    Example: Explaining Māori concepts through a Pākehā lens without centering Māori voices

    🏞️ NZ Example: Ihumātao Coverage

    Compare these two headlines about the same event:

    Headline A: "Protesters occupy sacred Māori land in standoff with police"

    Headline B: "Kaitiaki protect ancestral whenua from development"

    Analysis: Both describe the Ihumātao situation but frame it very differently. Headline A emphasizes conflict ("standoff with police") and uses the term "protesters" which can carry negative connotations. Headline B centers Māori perspectives by using "kaitiaki" (guardians) and "whenua" (land), framing the action as protection rather than occupation.

    Class Discussion (5 mins): Which types of bias do you see in each headline? How might each headline influence readers' perceptions?

    🌺 Te Ao Māori Perspectives on Media Bias (10 minutes)

    Teacher Input (5 mins): He maha ngā kōrero Māori mō te tika me te pono o ngā kōrero. (There are many Māori narratives about truth and balance in storytelling.) Traditional Māori communication values provide a framework for balanced reporting:

    • Whakapapa: Understanding connections and context - Stories should show relationships and historical background
    • Manaakitanga: Respect in representation - People and communities should be portrayed with dignity
    • Kaitiakitanga: Responsibility in storytelling - Journalists have a duty to protect truth and community wellbeing
    • Tika, pono, aroha: Correctness, truth, and compassion - Reporting should be accurate, honest, and empathetic

    🔍 Activity: Comparing Perspectives (20 minutes)

    Setup: Provide students with access to computers/devices and a list of current NZ news topics (e.g., Māori Language Week, treaty settlements, environmental issues).

    Task: Working in pairs, find two NZ news articles about the same issue from:

    1. Mainstream source: NZ Herald, Stuff, RNZ, Newshub
    2. Māori media source: Te Karere, Māori Television, Waatea News, Te Ao Māori News

    Analysis Framework: For each article, examine:

    • ✓ Headline wording - What emotions or assumptions does it create?
    • ✓ Sources quoted - Whose voices are centered? Whose are missing?
    • ✓ Language used - How are people/groups described?
    • ✓ Information included/excluded - What context is provided or left out?
    • ✓ Cultural values - Which of the Te Ao Māori values are present or absent?

    Share (5 mins): Each pair presents one key difference they found to the class.

    Differentiation: Advanced learners can identify which types of bias (selection, framing, source, language, cultural) are present in each article.

    🔧 Bias Detection Toolkit (15 minutes)

    Teacher Input (5 mins): Use these five critical questions to identify potential bias in any news article:

    1. Whose voices are centered? Whose are missing?
      Check if all relevant perspectives are represented, especially Māori voices on Māori issues.
    2. What assumptions does the reporter seem to make?
      Look for unstated beliefs about what's "normal" or "controversial."
    3. Are technical terms or te reo Māori explained? How?
      Notice if Māori concepts are explained respectfully or dismissed/simplified.
    4. Does the imagery support one perspective over others?
      Photos and graphics can reinforce bias through what they show or hide.
    5. How might the story change if told from another viewpoint?
      Imagine the story from a Māori, youth, or community perspective.

    ✏️ Interactive Activity: Rewrite the Headlines (10 minutes)

    Task: Take these biased headlines and rewrite them to be more balanced. Work individually for 5 minutes, then share with a partner.

    1. Original: "Māori activists disrupt city traffic with protest"
      Bias: "Disrupt" is negative; "activists" can be dismissive
    2. Original: "Government caves to iwi demands in treaty settlement"
      Bias: "Caves" implies weakness; "demands" sounds unreasonable
    3. Original: "Controversial new Māori ward established"
      Bias: "Controversial" frames it negatively without context

    Guiding Questions:

    • What assumptions does each original headline make?
    • How can you maintain accuracy while reducing bias?
    • What neutral or positive language could you use instead?
    • How might Māori media frame the same story?

    Share (5 mins): Volunteers share their rewritten headlines. Class discusses which versions are most balanced and why.

    📝 Aromatawai | Assessment Task (20 minutes)

    Individual Written Analysis: Submit a 300-word analysis comparing two news reports on the same event, identifying at least three types of bias. Use the bias detection toolkit and rubric below to guide your work.

    Your Analysis Should Include:

    1. ✅ Introduction: Brief description of the event and the two sources
    2. 🔍 Bias Identification: At least 3 types of bias with specific examples from each article
    3. 🌿 Cultural Perspective: How Te Ao Māori values are present or absent in each article
    4. 💭 Comparative Analysis: How the two sources differ in their framing and representation
    5. 🎯 Conclusion: Which article provides more balanced coverage and why

    Assessment Rubric

    Criteria Advanced (4) Proficient (3) Developing (2) Beginning (1)
    Bias Identification Identifies 4+ types of bias with specific, detailed examples Identifies 3 types of bias with clear examples Identifies 2 types of bias with some examples Identifies 1 type of bias or examples are vague
    Cultural Awareness Shows deep understanding of Te Ao Māori perspectives with specific reference to values Demonstrates awareness of cultural perspectives and mentions Te Ao Māori values Some recognition of cultural factors Limited cultural awareness
    Analysis Quality Insightful analysis with nuanced understanding and clear comparisons Clear analysis with good understanding and comparisons Basic analysis with some understanding Superficial analysis with limited comparison
    Writing Quality Well-structured, clear paragraphs, proper citations Clear structure, good paragraphs, citations present Basic structure, some organization issues Poor structure, difficult to follow

    📊 Assessment & Next Steps

    Formative Assessment

    Teachers should observe and assess:

    • ✓ Participation in comparing perspectives activity - Are students identifying differences?
    • ✓ Quality of rewritten headlines - Do they reduce bias while maintaining accuracy?
    • ✓ Written analysis task - Does it demonstrate understanding of all 5 bias types?
    • ✓ Cultural awareness - Are students considering Te Ao Māori values in their analysis?
    • ✓ Use of bias detection toolkit - Are students applying the 5 critical questions?

    Homework & Extension

    Required Homework:

    Complete your 300-word comparative analysis if not finished in class. Submit via [platform] by [date].

    Extension (Optional):

    • Create a "Bias Spotter" guide for your whānau to use when reading news
    • Research the history of Māori media in NZ (e.g., establishment of Te Karere, Māori Television)
    • Interview a journalist or media professional about how they avoid bias
    • Analyze international coverage of a NZ event - how do overseas media frame our stories?
