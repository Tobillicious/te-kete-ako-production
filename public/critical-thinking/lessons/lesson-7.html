    Advanced Research & Information Evaluation

    Critical Thinking Unit - Lesson 7 of 22 | Year 9-10 | Social Studies

    🌿 Cultural Context | Horopaki Ahurea

    Whakataukī

    "Mā te whakaaro nui e hanga te whare"

    "With great thought, the house is built"

    Research requires careful, methodical thinking - like building a whare, we must lay strong foundations of credible information.

    🏛️ Connection to Mangakōtukutuku College Values

    Whaiora: Wellbeing - Seeking knowledge that contributes to collective wellbeing

    This learning connects to whaiora by empowering students to research information that supports the wellbeing of their communities, using credible sources to make informed decisions that benefit everyone.

    🛡️ Cultural Safety Considerations

    • Recognize Māori oral histories (kōrero tuku iho) as valid research sources
    • Understand that Western academic standards are not the only measure of credibility
    • Value mātauranga Māori alongside Western knowledge systems
    • Acknowledge that research about Māori should ideally be led by Māori
    • Consult with whānau and local iwi when researching culturally sensitive topics

    🎯 Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Intentions

    Students Will Learn

    • 🔍 The CRAAP Test for evaluating source credibility (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose)
    • 📚 How to distinguish between primary, secondary, and tertiary sources
    • 🌿 How mātauranga Māori and oral histories fit into research frameworks
    • 🔗 Advanced search techniques using Boolean operators and filters

    Students Will Demonstrate

    • ✅ By applying the CRAAP Test to evaluate 3 sources on a research topic
    • ✅ By identifying primary vs. secondary sources in a research scenario
    • ✅ By using advanced search techniques to find credible NZ sources
    • ✅ By creating an annotated bibliography with 5 credible sources

    🌿 He Whakataki | Introduction (5 minutes)

    Teacher Prompt: Building on our digital literacy skills from Lesson 6, we now focus on advanced research techniques. In an age of information overload, knowing HOW to find and evaluate credible sources is a superpower.

    Hook Question: "You need to research climate change for a school project. You find information on:

    • A government science website
    • A blog by someone who says they're a scientist
    • A Wikipedia article
    • An interview with kaumātua about environmental changes they've observed

    Which sources would you use? Why? How would you decide?"

    📊 The CRAAP Test: Evaluating Sources (20 minutes)

    Teacher Input (10 mins): The CRAAP Test is a powerful tool for evaluating any information source. Let's explore each criterion:

    C - Currency

    When was it published/updated?

    ✓ Is the information current enough for your topic?

    ✓ Are links functional?

    Example: A 2010 article about social media may be outdated; a 1840 Treaty document is a valuable primary source.

    R - Relevance

    Does it relate to your research question?

    ✓ Is it at an appropriate level (not too basic/advanced)?

    ✓ Is it specific to your context (e.g., NZ-focused)?

    Example: US gun laws aren't relevant for NZ policy research.

    A - Authority

    Who is the author/publisher?

    ✓ What are their credentials/expertise?

    ✓ Is the publisher reputable?

    Example: A climate scientist has more authority on climate than a random blogger.

    A - Accuracy

    Can you verify the information?

    ✓ Are sources cited?

    ✓ Can you find the same info elsewhere?

    Example: Claims without evidence or citations are less reliable.

    P - Purpose

    Why does this information exist?

    ✓ To inform, persuade, sell, entertain?

    ✓ Is there bias or hidden agenda?

    Example: A company website may be biased toward their product.

    🔍 Activity: CRAAP Test Practice (10 minutes)

    Setup: Provide students with 3 sources on the same topic (e.g., Māori Language Week) - one highly credible, one questionable, one clearly unreliable.

    Task: Working in pairs, apply the CRAAP Test to each source. Create a simple table:

    Criterion Source 1 Source 2 Source 3
    Currency ✓ or ✗ ✓ or ✗ ✓ or ✗

    Share (5 mins): Which source would you use for research? Why?

    🌺 Mātauranga Māori & Research (15 minutes)

    Teacher Input (10 mins): Traditional research frameworks often prioritize written, peer-reviewed sources. But mātauranga Māori includes oral histories, whakapapa, and lived experiences that are equally valid.

    Types of Māori Knowledge Sources:

    • Kōrero tuku iho: Oral histories passed down through generations - highly credible within Māori communities
    • Whakapapa: Genealogical connections that establish authority and relationship to knowledge
    • Kaumātua interviews: Primary sources of lived experience and traditional knowledge
    • Māori academic sources: Research by Māori scholars using kaupapa Māori methodologies
    • Iwi archives: Historical documents, photos, and records held by iwi organizations

    Applying CRAAP to Māori Sources:

    Currency: Oral histories may be ancient but still current and relevant

    Relevance: Is the source from the relevant iwi/hapū for your research?

    Authority: Does the person have whakapapa connection? Are they recognized by their community?

    Accuracy: Can it be verified through other kaumātua or iwi records?

    Purpose: Is it shared for education, cultural preservation, or community benefit?

    Class Discussion (5 mins): Why is it important to include Māori sources when researching topics related to Aotearoa New Zealand? What might we miss if we only use Western academic sources?

    📚 Primary, Secondary & Tertiary Sources (15 minutes)

    Teacher Input (10 mins): Understanding the difference between source types helps you choose the right evidence for your research.

    Primary Sources

    Original, first-hand evidence

    Examples:

    • Treaty of Waitangi document
    • Diary entries, letters
    • Photographs, artwork
    • Interview transcripts
    • Kaumātua oral histories
    • Research data, surveys

    Use when: You need direct evidence or want to analyze original material

    Secondary Sources

    Analysis/interpretation of primary sources

    Examples:

    • History textbooks
    • Academic journal articles
    • Documentaries
    • News analysis
    • Biographies
    • Literature reviews

    Use when: You need expert analysis or want to understand different perspectives

    Tertiary Sources

    Summaries/compilations of primary & secondary sources

    Examples:

    • Encyclopedias
    • Wikipedia
    • Dictionaries
    • Fact books
    • Bibliographies

    Use when: You need background info or want to find primary/secondary sources

    🎯 Activity: Source Type Sorting (5 minutes)

    Task: Categorize these sources as Primary, Secondary, or Tertiary:

    1. A photograph of the 1975 Māori Land March
    2. A Wikipedia article about the Māori Land March
    3. A news article analyzing the impact of the Māori Land March
    4. An interview with Dame Whina Cooper (march leader)
    5. A history textbook chapter about the march
    6. An encyclopedia entry defining "land rights"

    Answers: 1-Primary, 2-Tertiary, 3-Secondary, 4-Primary, 5-Secondary, 6-Tertiary

    🔍 Advanced Search Techniques (10 minutes)

    Teacher Input (5 mins): Boolean operators and search filters help you find exactly what you need.

    Boolean Operators:

    • AND: Narrows search (e.g., "Māori AND education" finds pages with both terms)
    • OR: Broadens search (e.g., "Māori OR indigenous" finds pages with either term)
    • NOT: Excludes terms (e.g., "Māori NOT Australia" excludes Australian content)
    • "Quotes": Exact phrase (e.g., "Treaty of Waitangi" finds that exact phrase)
    • site: Search specific website (e.g., "climate change site:govt.nz")

    💻 Activity: Search Challenge (5 minutes)

    Task: Use advanced search techniques to find:

    1. Government sources about Māori language revitalization (use site:govt.nz)
    2. Articles about either "te reo Māori" OR "Māori language"
    3. Information about Māori education but NOT in Australia

    Share: What sources did you find? How did the operators change your results?

    📝 Aromatawai | Assessment Task (20 minutes)

    Annotated Bibliography Task: Choose a research topic related to Aotearoa NZ (e.g., environmental issues, Māori culture, social justice). Find and evaluate 5 credible sources.

    For Each Source, Include:

    1. ✅ Full Citation: Author, title, publication, date, URL (if online)
    2. 🔍 CRAAP Evaluation: Brief assessment of Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose
    3. 📚 Source Type: Primary, secondary, or tertiary?
    4. 💭 Annotation (2-3 sentences): What does this source say? How will you use it in your research?

    Success Criteria:

    • ✓ 5 credible sources from diverse types (at least 1 primary, 1 Māori source)
    • ✓ All sources pass the CRAAP Test
    • ✓ Proper citations in a consistent format
    • ✓ Clear, thoughtful annotations showing understanding
    • ✓ Evidence of advanced search techniques used

    📊 Assessment & Next Steps

    Formative Assessment

    Teachers should observe and assess:

    • ✓ CRAAP Test application - Are students evaluating all 5 criteria?
    • ✓ Source type identification - Can students distinguish primary/secondary/tertiary?
    • ✓ Search technique use - Are students using Boolean operators effectively?
    • ✓ Cultural awareness - Are students including Māori sources?
    • ✓ Annotation quality - Do annotations show critical thinking?

    Homework & Extension

    Required Homework:

    Complete your annotated bibliography with 5 credible sources. Bring printed/digital copies to next lesson.

    Extension (Optional):

    • Interview a kaumātua or community elder - practice evaluating oral history as a primary source
    • Create a "Research Toolkit" poster with CRAAP Test and Boolean operators
    • Compare how the same topic is covered in mainstream vs. Māori media sources
    • Explore Te Ara Encyclopedia or Papers Past for NZ-specific research
