    Creative Writing Inspired by Whakataukī

    Whakataukī (Māori proverb): "Ehara tāku toa i te toa takitahi, engari he toa takitini."
    "My strength is not as an individual, but as a collective."

    Learning Objectives

    • Understand the meaning and significance of whakataukī in Te Ao Māori.
    • Analyse how traditional wisdom can inspire modern creative writing.
    • Write a short story, poem, or personal reflection inspired by a whakataukī.
    • Reflect on how cultural perspectives shape storytelling.

    Cultural Context & Safety Notes

    Whakataukī are traditional Māori proverbs that carry deep wisdom, values, and lessons. They are often used in speeches, education, and daily life to guide behaviour and thinking.

    Cultural Safety:

    • Always pronounce Māori words correctly—ask for help if unsure.
    • Treat whakataukī with respect, as they hold cultural significance.
    • Encourage students to explore meanings but avoid misrepresentation.

    Activity: Writing with Whakataukī

    Step 1: Explore Whakataukī

    Read the following whakataukī and discuss their meanings:

    • "He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tāngata, he tāngata, he tāngata."
      "What is the most important thing in the world? It is people, it is people, it is people."
    • "Kia kaha, kia māia, kia manawanui."
      "Be strong, be brave, be steadfast."

    Step 2: Choose & Reflect

    Select one whakataukī that resonates with you. Write a short reflection on what it means to you.

    Step 3: Creative Writing

    Write a short story, poem, or personal narrative inspired by your chosen whakataukī. Consider:

    • How does the whakataukī connect to your own experiences?
    • Can you create a fictional story that embodies its message?
    Share Your Writing

    Assessment Suggestions

    • Completion & Effort: Did the student engage with the whakataukī and produce a piece of writing?
    • Depth of Reflection: Does the writing show understanding of the whakataukī’s meaning?
    • Creativity & Originality: Is the writing imaginative and unique?

    Extension Activities

    • Whakataukī Art: Create a visual artwork (digital or hand-drawn) that represents your chosen whakataukī.
    • Oral Storytelling: Present your story aloud, using expressive language and gestures.
    • Comparative Study: Compare a whakataukī with a proverb from another culture. How are they similar or different?

    Adaptations for Different Learners

    • Struggling Writers: Provide sentence starters or allow verbal storytelling.
    • Advanced Writers: Challenge them to write in multiple genres (e.g., a poem AND a short story).
    • ESOL Students: Offer translations and encourage writing in their first language if helpful.
