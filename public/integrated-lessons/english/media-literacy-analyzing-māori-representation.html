    Media Literacy: Analyzing Māori Representation

    Learning Objectives

    • Analyze how Māori people and culture are represented in media.
    • Understand the impact of stereotypes and biases in media.
    • Develop critical thinking skills to evaluate media portrayals.
    • Explore ways to promote accurate and respectful representation.

    Cultural Context & Safety Notes

    This lesson examines media portrayals of Māori, which may include historical and contemporary stereotypes. Some content may be sensitive, so ensure:

    • A safe space for discussion is maintained.
    • Students are encouraged to speak respectfully.
    • Māori perspectives are centered, not debated.
    • If Māori students share personal experiences, listen without challenge.

    Lesson Activities

    1. Warm-up: Media Perception (10 mins)

    Question: "When you think of Māori representation in media, what images or stories come to mind?" (Think-Pair-Share)

    2. Case Study Analysis (30 mins)

    Watch or analyze excerpts from:

    • News segments (e.g., Māori land protests vs. Pākehā protests)
    • TV shows/films (e.g., Whānau, Boy, Once Were Warriors)
    • Advertising (e.g., tourism ads featuring Māori culture)

    Guiding Questions:

    • How are Māori characters portrayed?
    • Who is telling the story? (Māori or non-Māori creators?)
    • Are there stereotypes? If so, what impact might they have?

    3. Group Discussion (20 mins)

    Compare findings and discuss:

    • What patterns do you notice?
    • How does media shape public perception of Māori?
    • What would better representation look like?

    Assessment Suggestions

    Option 1: Written reflection (300-500 words) on a media example, analyzing representation.

    Option 2: Create a short media critique (video or slides) highlighting positive/negative portrayals.

    Option 3: Rewrite a news headline or scene to be more culturally accurate.

    Extension Activities

    • Research Māori filmmakers/journalists and their work (e.g., Taika Waititi, Mihingarangi Forbes).
    • Compare Māori representation in NZ media vs. Indigenous representation in other countries.
    • Create a storyboard for a short film that challenges stereotypes.

    Adaptations for Diverse Learners

    • Visual learners: Use infographics or storyboards.
    • Struggling writers: Allow verbal responses or bullet points.
    • Advanced students: Investigate media ownership and bias.
    • Māori students: Invite (but don’t require) personal insights.
