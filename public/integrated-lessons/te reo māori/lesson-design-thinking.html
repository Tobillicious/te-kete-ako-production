Design Thinking: Problem-Solving with Māori Values

"Mā te huruhuru ka rere te manu"

"Adorn the bird with feathers so it can fly" - Equip people to succeed

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the design thinking process
  • Apply creative problem-solving techniques
  • Integrate Māori values into solution design
  • Work collaboratively on design challenges

The Design Thinking Process

5 Stages of Design Thinking

  1. Empathize (Aroha): Understand the user and their needs
  2. Define (Tautuhi): Clearly articulate the problem
  3. Ideate (Whakaaro): Generate creative solutions
  4. Prototype (Tauira): Build a testable version
  5. Test (Whakamātau): Try it out and refine

Māori Design Values

Integrating Te Ao Māori into Design

  • Whanaungatanga: Design for collective benefit, not just individuals
  • Kaitiakitanga: Consider environmental sustainability
  • Manaakitanga: Ensure solutions show care and hospitality
  • Tikanga: Respect cultural protocols and appropriateness

Design Challenge: School Improvement

Group Activity

Challenge: Design a solution to improve an aspect of school life

Step 1: Empathize

  • Interview 3 students about their experiences
  • What frustrates them? What do they wish was better?
  • Consider diverse perspectives (different year levels, cultures)

Step 2: Define the Problem

Write a problem statement: "[User] needs [solution] because [insight]"

Example: "Year 9 students need a quiet study space because they struggle to concentrate in noisy areas"

Step 3: Ideate Solutions

  • Brainstorm 10+ ideas (no judgment yet!)
  • Consider how Māori values could enhance each idea
  • Select the 3 most promising ideas

Step 4: Prototype

  • Create a simple model, sketch, or plan
  • Doesn't need to be perfect - just testable!
  • Include cultural elements in your design

Step 5: Test & Refine

  • Get feedback from potential users
  • What works? What needs improvement?
  • Iterate based on feedback

Case Study: Māori-Led Design

Example: Marae Design

Traditional marae design demonstrates sophisticated design thinking:

  • Empathy: Designed for community gatherings and cultural practices
  • Sustainability: Using local materials (kaitiakitanga)
  • Symbolism: Every element has meaning (whakapapa)
  • Adaptability: Spaces serve multiple functions

Modern Māori designers continue these principles while adapting to contemporary needs

Assessment

Design Challenge Portfolio

Document your design thinking process:

  1. Problem identification and research
  2. Sketches and brainstorming notes
  3. Prototype photos/descriptions
  4. Test results and feedback
  5. Final refined solution
  6. Reflection on how Māori values enhanced your design

Extension Activities
