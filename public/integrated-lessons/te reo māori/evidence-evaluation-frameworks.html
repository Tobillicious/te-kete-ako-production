Evidence Evaluation: Building Strong Arguments

Types of Evidence

TypeDescriptionStrength
Statistical Numbers, data, surveys Strong if from reliable source
Expert testimony Quotes from authorities Strong if expert is credible
Anecdotal Personal stories Weak alone, good for context
Historical Past events, documents Strong if primary sources
Scientific Research studies Very strong if peer-reviewed

Evaluating Evidence Quality

Key Questions:

  1. Relevance: Does it directly support the claim?
  2. Sufficiency: Is there enough evidence?
  3. Credibility: Is the source trustworthy?
  4. Representativeness: Does it reflect the full picture?
  5. Timeliness: Is it current enough?

Māori Evidence Types

Traditional Forms of Evidence:

  • Whakapapa: Genealogical records proving connections
  • Kōrero tuku iho: Oral histories passed down
  • Whakairo: Carvings encoding historical events
  • Waiata: Songs preserving knowledge
  • Whenua: The land itself as evidence (ahi kā - kept fires burning)

In Waitangi Tribunal cases, oral evidence from kaumātua is given equal weight to written documents!

Practice: Evaluating Claims

Claim: "Māori were the first settlers of Aotearoa"

Evaluate this evidence:

  1. "Archaeological findings show Māori arrived around 1280-1300 CE" - Scientific evidence
  2. "My koro told me our iwi has been here forever" - Oral tradition
  3. "Whakapapa connects us back to Hawaiki" - Cultural record

Discussion: How do you reconcile different types of evidence? What makes evidence credible?

Assessment

  • Find and evaluate 5 sources on a research topic using CRAAP test
  • Write a paragraph using 3 different types of evidence
  • Create an evidence evaluation checklist
  • Compare Western and Māori concepts of evidence
