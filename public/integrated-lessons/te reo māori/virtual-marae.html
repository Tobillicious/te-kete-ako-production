What is a Marae?

The marae is the heart of Māori community life. It includes:

  • Marae ātea: Open courtyard (where ceremonies happen)
  • Wharenui: Meeting house (where hui occur)
  • Wharekai: Dining hall (where kai is shared)
  • Wharepaku: Toilet facilities
  • Urupā: Cemetery (often nearby)

Pōwhiri - Welcome Ceremony

Stages of Pōwhiri:

  1. Karanga (Call): Women call visitors onto the marae
  2. Mihimihi (Greetings): Formal speeches of welcome and response
  3. Waiata (Song): Support speeches with song
  4. Hongi (Press noses): Share breath of life
  5. Kai (Food): Hospitality through shared meal

Tikanga - Protocol and Etiquette

Important Things to Know:

  • Shoes: Remove before entering wharenui
  • Food: Never eat in the wharenui (sacred space)
  • Sitting: Don't sit on tables or pillows (for heads)
  • Respect: Listen during speeches, don't interrupt
  • Photography: Ask permission first
  • Silence phones: Show respect during ceremonies

The Wharenui - A Living Ancestor

The meeting house represents a tupuna (ancestor):

  • Koruru (gable): The head
  • Maihi (bargeboards): The arms
  • Tahuhu (ridgepole): The backbone
  • Heke (rafters): The ribs
  • Pou (posts): Named ancestors

When you enter, you enter the embrace of your ancestor!

Marae Etiquette Scenarios

What would you do?

  1. You're visiting a marae for the first time. You're hungry. Can you eat in the wharenui?

    Answer: No - food is not allowed in the wharenui. Wait for kai to be served in the wharekai.

  2. An elder is giving a whaikōrero (speech). Your phone rings. What do you do?

    Answer: Silence it immediately. Phones should already be on silent during ceremonies.

  3. You want to take a photo of the beautiful carvings. What should you do?

    Answer: Ask permission from your hosts first. Some areas may be tapu (sacred).
