üî¨ Hook: Traditional Knowledge IS Science (10 min)

The Kawakawa Question

MƒÅori have used kawakawa (Piper excelsum) for hundreds of years to treat cuts, infections, and inflammation. Modern analysis shows kawakawa contains:

  • Myristicin: Anti-inflammatory and analgesic (pain-relieving) properties
  • Polyphenols: Antioxidant compounds
  • Sesquiterpenes: Antimicrobial agents that fight infection
  • Essential oils: Multiple therapeutic compounds

ü§î Question: How did MƒÅori know kawakawa worked for these specific conditions BEFORE we had chemistry labs?

üî¨ Student Investigation: Plant Chemistry Lab (30 min)

Safety-First Investigation

Working in groups of 3-4, students will investigate the chemical properties of medicinal plants using safe laboratory methods:

‚ö†Ô∏è Safety Requirements:

  • Teacher supervision required at all times
  • No tasting or ingesting ANY plants
  • Wear gloves when handling plants
  • Wash hands thoroughly after lab
  • Use only teacher-provided plant materials

Investigation Steps:

  1. Observe: Examine the plant's physical characteristics (leaf shape, texture, smell)
  2. Extract: Create a simple water infusion (tea) by steeping leaves in hot water
  3. Test pH: Use indicator paper to measure acidity/alkalinity
  4. Test for compounds: Use simple chemical tests to detect tannins, oils, alkaloids
  5. Document: Record observations, color changes, and results
  6. Research: Compare findings with known traditional uses

Guiding Questions:

  • What chemical properties did you observe?
  • How do these properties relate to traditional medicinal uses?
  • What does this tell you about the sophistication of traditional knowledge?

üíä Case Study: Aspirin's Natural Origins

For thousands of years, cultures worldwide (including MƒÅori with certain native plants) used willow bark and related plants for pain and fever. Why? These plants contain salicylic acid - the compound that became modern aspirin.

The Journey:

  1. Traditional Knowledge: Centuries of use show willow bark relieves pain
  2. Modern Isolation: Scientists extract salicylic acid (1800s)
  3. Modification: Create acetylsalicylic acid (aspirin) - less stomach irritation
  4. Mass Production: Synthetic production makes it globally accessible

Discussion:

  • Should traditional knowledge holders receive recognition/compensation when their knowledge leads to profitable drugs?
  • How can we prevent biopiracy (theft of traditional knowledge)?
  • What role should Indigenous communities play in research on their traditional medicines?

‚öñÔ∏è Extension: Ethics & Intellectual Property

The Biopiracy Problem

When pharmaceutical companies patent drugs based on traditional Indigenous knowledge without compensation or recognition, it's called biopiracy. This is a form of ongoing colonization.

Real Example:

Multiple companies have attempted to patent mƒÅnuka honey properties developed through MƒÅori traditional knowledge, without acknowledgment or benefit-sharing with MƒÅori communities.

Debate Topics:

  • Should traditional knowledge be patentable at all?
  • How can Indigenous communities protect their intellectual property?
  • What does fair benefit-sharing look like?
  • How do we balance global health needs with Indigenous rights?
