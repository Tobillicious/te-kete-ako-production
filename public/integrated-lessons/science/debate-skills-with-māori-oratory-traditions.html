    Cultural Context & Safety Notes

    Whaik≈çrero is a sacred MƒÅori tradition of formal speech-making, typically performed by men on marae. While we adapt some elements for classroom use, we must respect its cultural significance:

    • Traditionally, whaik≈çrero is performed by men, but in educational contexts, all students may participate
    • Always acknowledge the iwi/tribe when referencing specific whaik≈çrero examples
    • Begin with a karakia (prayer) if appropriate for your school context
    • Be aware some students may have whƒÅnau connections to expert orators - handle with sensitivity
    • Encourage students to share only what they're comfortable with regarding personal connections

    Lesson Content

    1. Understanding Whaik≈çrero Structure

    Traditional whaik≈çrero follows a specific structure:

    1. Tauparapara - Opening incantation to establish speaker's credibility
    2. Whakapapa - Connections to people and place
    3. Kupu whakatepe - Main message or argument
    4. Whaikupu - Supporting evidence or stories
    5. Poroporoaki - Closing remarks

    Activity: Watch this video example of whaik≈çrero and identify these elements.

    2. Contemporary Debate Structure

    Compare with standard debate format:

    1. Introduction - Hook and thesis
    2. Arguments - Main points with evidence
    3. Rebuttal - Addressing opposing views
    4. Conclusion - Summary and call to action

    Discussion: What similarities and differences do you notice? How might each approach be effective in different contexts?

    3. Hybrid Debate Activity

    Topic: "Our school should incorporate more MƒÅori cultural practices in daily routines."

    Instructions:

    1. Divide into groups of 4-5
    2. Prepare arguments using a blend of both structures:
      • Begin with a personal connection (similar to whakapapa)
      • Include traditional proverbs (whakataukƒ´) where relevant
      • Use evidence and logical arguments
      • Close with an impactful statement
    3. Each group presents their arguments (3-4 minutes each)
    4. Audience provides feedback using the assessment criteria

    Adaptations for Diverse Learners

    Support Strategies:

    • ESOL students: Provide sentence starters, allow use of first language for planning
    • Anxious speakers: Offer options to present to small groups or record speeches
    • Physical disabilities: Ensure accessible speaking spaces, allow seated presentations
    • Neurodiverse students: Provide clear structure templates, allow fidget tools
    • Cultural considerations: Be flexible with participation requirements based on cultural comfort levels

    Teacher Implementation Guide

    Before the Lesson:

    1. Consult with your school's MƒÅori cultural advisor if available
    2. Prepare video examples and any necessary equipment
    3. Print structure guides and assessment rubrics
    4. Arrange seating for group work and presentations

    During the Lesson:

    1. Introduction (15 mins): Explain objectives, show video example, discuss cultural protocols
    2. Analysis (20 mins): Compare structures in small groups, share findings
    3. Preparation (25 mins): Groups research and prepare hybrid debates
    4. Presentations (30 mins): Each group presents, peers provide feedback
    5. Reflection (10 mins): Whole class discussion on what worked well

    After the Lesson:

    1. Provide individual feedback using rubric
    2. Display exemplary work or recordings (with permission)
    3. Follow up with interested students about extension opportunities
    4. Reflect on what cultural elements resonated most with students
