    Week 5 Lesson 1: Design Challenge Launch

    Students launch their culminating society design project, using all their systems thinking and government analysis to create a comprehensive blueprint for an ideal community that balances individual rights with collective wellbeing.

    Te Whakatinanatanga Hapori - Bringing Community to Life

    Creating a society is one of humanity's greatest challenges. Today you become architects of community, designing how people will live, work, govern themselves, and care for each other and the environment. Drawing on everything you've learned about systems thinking, government types, and cultural values, you'll create a society that reflects your deepest beliefs about justice, fairness, and human flourishing.

    "He tāngata, he tāngata, he tāngata"

    It is people, it is people, it is people - society exists to serve human wellbeing and potential.

    Ngā Whāinga Ako - Learning Intentions

    Students Will Understand

    • Society design requires balancing competing values and needs
    • All system components must work together coherently
    • Different cultural values lead to different societal solutions
    • Every choice has consequences and trade-offs

    Students Will Apply

    • Systems thinking to design coherent society structures
    • Knowledge of government systems to choose appropriate governance
    • Cultural values to guide decision-making processes
    • Evidence-based reasoning to justify design choices

    Ngā Mahi - Lesson Activities (75 minutes)

    1. Society Architect Briefing - The Challenge Unveiled (15 mins)

    🏗️ Your Mission as Society Architects

    A new planet has been discovered with perfect living conditions. 500 volunteer families (approximately 2000 people) from diverse backgrounds have been selected to establish the first human settlement. You are the architect team responsible for designing how this society will function.

    Key Challenge Parameters:
    • Population: 2000 people from diverse cultural, economic, and educational backgrounds
    • Resources: Abundant natural resources but limited advanced technology
    • Timeline: Must function successfully from Day 1 (no gradual development)
    • Goal: Create a just, sustainable, and thriving society for current and future generations

    Values Foundation Exercise

    Before designing structures, establish your society's core values. Each team selects their top 5 values from the list below, then ranks them in order of priority:

    Justice & Fairness
    Individual Freedom
    Community Unity
    Environmental Sustainability
    Economic Prosperity
    Cultural Diversity
    Innovation & Progress
    Security & Safety
    Tradition & Heritage
    Efficiency & Order

    2. Society Design Stations - Component Deep-Dive (35 mins)

    Structured Exploration: Teams rotate through 5 design stations, spending 7 minutes at each to develop detailed plans for every aspect of their society.

    Station 1: Government System & Decision-Making

    Critical Design Questions:
    • Who has the authority to make decisions that affect everyone?
    • How are leaders chosen and how long do they serve?
    • What decisions require community input vs. expert judgment?
    • How will you prevent abuse of power?
    • How will minority voices be protected?
    Design Tools & Templates:
    • Government structure flowchart template
    • Decision-making process diagrams
    • Leadership selection criteria worksheet
    • Power balance and checks system design
    • Democratic, monarchic, council, or hybrid options
    Cultural Lens: How might iwi governance principles (rangatiratanga, whakatōhea, manaakitanga) influence your government design?

    Station 2: Rights, Laws & Justice System

    Critical Design Questions:
    • What fundamental rights will every person have?
    • What responsibilities come with those rights?
    • What laws are absolutely essential for community function?
    • How will conflicts and crimes be handled?
    • How will justice focus on restoration vs. punishment?
    Design Tools & Templates:
    • Bill of Rights drafting template
    • Essential laws priority ranking
    • Justice system flowchart design
    • Conflict resolution process map
    • Restorative vs. punitive justice options
    Cultural Lens: How might traditional Māori approaches to conflict resolution and community healing inform your justice system?

    Station 3: Economic System & Resource Distribution

    Critical Design Questions:
    • How will work be organized and resources distributed?
    • Will there be money, trade, or sharing systems?
    • How will you ensure everyone's basic needs are met?
    • What happens if someone cannot or will not contribute?
    • How will you prevent extreme inequality?
    Design Tools & Templates:
    • Economic system comparison chart
    • Resource allocation planning grid
    • Work organization structure design
    • Basic needs guarantee framework
    • Market, command, mixed, or gift economy options
    Cultural Lens: How might principles of kaitiakitanga and resource stewardship shape your economic system design?

    Station 4: Culture, Values & Social Organization

    Critical Design Questions:
    • How will you celebrate and preserve cultural diversity?
    • What shared traditions and values will unite the community?
    • How will education and knowledge be organized?
    • What role will arts, music, and creativity play?
    • How will you welcome new members and handle cultural change?
    Design Tools & Templates:
    • Cultural diversity celebration framework
    • Shared tradition and ritual planning
    • Education system structure design
    • Arts and creativity integration plan
    • Cultural change adaptation process
    Cultural Lens: How might concepts of whakapapa, manaakitanga, and whakatōhea create strong community bonds while respecting individual identity?

    Station 5: Environment, Sustainability & Future Planning

    Critical Design Questions:
    • How will you protect and sustainably use the environment?
    • What happens during natural disasters or crises?
    • How will you plan for population growth?
    • What legacy will you leave for future generations?
    • How will your society adapt to change over time?
    Design Tools & Templates:
    • Environmental protection framework
    • Disaster response and resilience plan
    • Future growth planning template
    • Seven-generation impact assessment
    • Adaptive change management system
    Cultural Lens: How might traditional Māori approaches to environmental stewardship and seven-generation thinking guide your sustainability planning?

    3. Systems Integration Workshop - Making It All Work Together (20 mins)

    Coherence Challenge: Teams must ensure all their design components work together as a functioning system with no contradictions or gaps.

    Systems Integration Checklist

    Internal Consistency Check:
    • Do your government and economic systems support the same values?
    • Are your rights and laws compatible with your cultural goals?
    • Does your justice system align with your conflict resolution approach?
    • Can your environmental plan support your population growth?
    Practical Functionality Test:
    • Can decisions actually be made efficiently with your government system?
    • Will your economic system provide for everyone's basic needs?
    • Are your laws enforceable with your justice system?
    • Does your society have mechanisms to adapt and change?

    Scenario Stress Test:

    Test your society design against challenging scenarios:

    Economic Crisis

    Resources become scarce. How does your system respond?

    Cultural Conflict

    Two groups have fundamentally different values. How do you resolve this?

    Leadership Crisis

    Your leader makes a deeply unpopular decision. What happens?

    Rapid Growth

    Your population doubles in 10 years. Can your systems adapt?

    4. Project Blueprint & Development Plan (5 mins)

    Launch Strategy: Teams create their project development timeline and choose their presentation format for the week-long design challenge.

    Week-Long Project Timeline

    Day 1: Plan

    Finalize design, assign roles, create project schedule

    Day 2: Design

    Create detailed systems, draft constitution, design visuals

    Day 3: Create

    Build presentation materials, test scenarios, refine details

    Day 4: Present

    Society showcase, peer evaluation, "Most Just Society" vote

    Day 5: Reflect

    Individual reflection, project celebration, unit synthesis

    Presentation Format Options:

    • Society Constitution + Visual Display: Written founding document with poster exhibition
    • Interactive Society Fair: Booth where visitors experience your society
    • Documentary Video: 5-minute film showcasing your society in action
    • Government Simulation: Live demonstration of decision-making process
    • Day-in-the-Life Drama: Role-play showing how citizens experience your society
    • Society Pitch: Formal presentation to "convince investors" to join

    Aromatawai - Assessment Framework

    Project Assessment Criteria

    CriteriaExcellent (4)Good (3)Developing (2)
    Systems IntegrationAll components work together coherently with clear connectionsMost components connect logically with minor inconsistenciesSome components work together but gaps or contradictions exist
    Evidence-Based ReasoningChoices clearly justified using unit learning and cultural knowledgeMost choices supported with relevant evidence and reasoningSome justification provided but limited use of evidence
    Cultural IntegrationThoughtful integration of Te Ao Māori and diverse cultural perspectivesGood cultural awareness with some meaningful integrationBasic cultural awareness but limited integration
    Innovation & CreativityCreative solutions that go beyond conventional approachesSome creative elements while maintaining practical viabilityConventional approaches with limited creative elements

    Whakamutunga - Challenge Reflection

    Today you began the most complex and important work humans do: creating societies that serve all people fairly and sustainably. You've learned that every choice has consequences, every system affects all others, and every society reflects the values of its creators. Over the next week, you'll discover that designing a just society is challenging not because we don't know what's right, but because balancing competing needs and values requires wisdom, compromise, and constant adaptation.

    "He whakatōhea, he manaaki tangata"

    Through collaborative effort and mutual care, we create communities where all people can flourish.
