🦅 Tohu: Traditional Environmental Indicators

Long before modern climate monitoring, Māori observed environmental indicators (tohu) to understand seasonal patterns and ecosystem health. These indicators are now revealing climate change impacts:

🐦 Bird Migration Patterns

Traditional indicator: Arrival of kuaka (godwit) signaled seasonal changes

Climate change impact: Migration timing shifting by 5-7 days earlier due to warming temperatures

🌊 Tidal Patterns & Sea Level

Traditional indicator: Tide markers and seasonal coastal patterns

Climate change impact: Rising sea levels affecting traditional fishing grounds and coastal settlements

🌸 Plant Flowering Times

Traditional indicator: Kōwhai flowering signaled kūmara planting time

Climate change impact: Flowering occurring earlier, disrupting agricultural timing

❄️ Frost Patterns

Traditional indicator: First and last frost dates guided planting and harvesting

Climate change impact: Frost-free period extending, affecting plant and animal life cycles

🌿 Kaitiakitanga: Indigenous Climate Leadership

Kaitiakitanga means guardianship - the responsibility to protect and care for the environment for future generations. Māori communities are leading climate action in Aotearoa:

🌊 Protecting Waterways

Many iwi have won legal recognition for rivers as living entities (e.g., Te Awa Tupua - Whanganui River), enabling environmental protection

🌳 Reforestation Projects

Iwi-led native forest restoration using traditional ecological knowledge to rebuild biodiversity

⚡ Renewable Energy

Māori-owned geothermal and solar projects providing clean energy while respecting whenua (land)

🐟 Sustainable Fisheries

Applying rāhui (temporary bans) and traditional management to protect marine ecosystems
