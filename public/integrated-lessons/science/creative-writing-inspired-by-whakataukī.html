    Creative Writing Inspired by Whakataukī

    Traditional wisdom in modern expression

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

    • Understand the purpose and significance of whakataukī in Te Ao Māori
    • Analyse how whakataukī convey wisdom through metaphor and imagery
    • Create original creative writing pieces inspired by whakataukī
    • Apply literary techniques such as metaphor, simile, and symbolism
    • Reflect on personal connections to the wisdom in whakataukī

    NZ Curriculum Links: English Level 4-5, Social Sciences, The Arts

    Cultural Context & Safety Notes

    Cultural Context: Whakataukī (proverbs) are treasured expressions of Māori wisdom that have been passed down through generations. They encapsulate worldviews, values, and guidance for living well. Each whakataukī connects to specific cultural knowledge, historical events, or ancestral teachings.

    Cultural Safety:

    • Whakataukī should be treated with respect as they carry ancestral wisdom
    • When interpreting whakataukī, acknowledge there may be multiple valid interpretations
    • Always attribute whakataukī appropriately when using them
    • Be mindful that some whakataukī may have deeper cultural significance than surface translations suggest
    • Encourage students to approach whakataukī with curiosity and respect for their origins

    Understanding Whakataukī

    Whakataukī are poetic expressions of wisdom in Te Reo Māori. They often use nature metaphors and ancestral references to convey deeper meanings about life, relationships, and values.

    Ehara tāku toa i te toa takitahi, engari he toa takitini
    My strength is not that of an individual, but that of the collective
    This whakataukī emphasizes the importance of community and collaboration over individual achievement.
    Kāore te kūmara e kōrero mō tōna ake reka
    The kūmara (sweet potato) does not speak of its own sweetness
    This teaches humility - we should not boast about our own abilities or achievements.
    He kōtuku rerenga tahi
    The white heron of a single flight
    This refers to something rare and special that occurs only once, like a distinguished visitor.
    Ki te kotahi te kākaho, ka whati; ki te kāpuia, e kore e whati
    If a reed stands alone, it can be broken; if it is in a group, it cannot be broken
    This emphasizes the strength found in unity and working together.

    Creative Writing Activities

    Activity 1: Whakataukī Story Creation

    Choose one whakataukī that resonates with you. Write a short story (300-500 words) that illustrates the wisdom in this whakataukī through characters and events.

    Steps:

    1. Select a whakataukī that speaks to you
    2. Brainstorm how this wisdom could be shown through a narrative
    3. Create characters who embody or learn this wisdom
    4. Develop a plot where this wisdom is tested or proven
    5. Write your story, making sure to show rather than tell the wisdom

    Activity 2: Modern Whakataukī Creation

    Create your own modern whakataukī that conveys wisdom relevant to today's world. Consider issues like digital citizenship, environmental awareness, or social relationships.

    Steps:

    1. Identify a piece of wisdom you want to share
    2. Think of a metaphor or imagery that could represent this wisdom
    3. Write your whakataukī in both English and (if possible) Te Reo Māori
    4. Write a brief explanation of its meaning

    Activity 3: Whakataukī Poetry

    Write a poem inspired by the imagery and wisdom of a whakataukī. Your poem can be in any form (free verse, haiku, etc.) but should reflect the essence of the original whakataukī.

    Steps:

    1. Select a whakataukī with strong imagery
    2. Identify the key metaphors and symbols
    3. Brainstorm related images and emotions
    4. Write your poem, focusing on sensory details

    Alternative Assessment Options:

    • Oral presentation explaining their creative piece and its connection to whakataukī
    • Digital story combining written text with visual elements
    • Peer assessment with guided feedback templates
    • Learning journal documenting the creative process

    Extension Activities

    Extension 1: Whakataukī Anthology

    Create a collection of 5-7 original whakataukī with accompanying explanations and creative writing pieces. Organize them thematically and design the anthology as a published work.

    Extension 2: Cross-Cultural Proverb Study

    Research proverbs from other cultures that share similar wisdom to Māori whakataukī. Create a comparative analysis exploring how different cultures express universal truths.

    Extension 3: Whakataukī Performance

    Adapt your creative writing into a performance piece (spoken word, short play, or digital storytelling). Consider how voice, movement, and visual elements can enhance the whakataukī's message.

    Teacher Implementation Guide

    Before the Lesson:

    • Familiarize yourself with the whakataukī included in this resource
    • Prepare examples of creative writing inspired by proverbs or wisdom sayings
    • Consider inviting a Māori elder or cultural advisor to introduce whakataukī (if appropriate and available)
    • Prepare any necessary technology for digital activities
