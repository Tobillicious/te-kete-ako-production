    Te Tiriti o Waitangi / The Treaty of Waitangi

    Aotearoa's Founding Document - Understanding Both Versions

    📺 Video Resource ⏱️ 60 minutes 🎓 Year 7-13 🏛️ Social Studies / History

    📺 Watch: The Treaty of Waitangi Explained

    ▶️ Watch Video on YouTube

    A clear explanation of the Treaty's history, articles, and ongoing significance

    🎯 Learning Objectives (He Whāinga Ako)

    • Understand the historical context of Te Tiriti o Waitangi
    • Identify the three main articles in both versions
    • Analyze differences between English and Māori texts
    • Evaluate the Treaty's ongoing significance in modern Aotearoa
    • Recognize the Treaty as a living document

    📜 Historical Context

    Signed: 6 February 1840 at Waitangi, Bay of Islands

    Signatories: Representatives of the British Crown and over 500 Māori rangatira (chiefs)

    Purpose: Establish a framework for British settlement while protecting Māori rights and interests

    Why Two Versions?

    The Treaty was written in both English and te reo Māori. However, significant differences in translation have led to ongoing debate about its true meaning and intent. Most rangatira signed the Māori version, Te Tiriti o Waitangi.

    📋 The Three Articles

    Article 1: Kāwanatanga / Governance

    English Version

    Māori cede sovereignty to the British Crown

    Te Reo Māori Version

    Māori grant kāwanatanga (governance/governorship) to the Crown

    Key Difference: "Sovereignty" vs "kāwanatanga" - different concepts with different implications for authority

    Article 2: Tino Rangatiratanga / Chieftainship

    English Version

    Crown guarantees Māori "full exclusive and undisturbed possession" of lands, forests, fisheries

    Te Reo Māori Version

    Crown guarantees Māori tino rangatiratanga (absolute chieftainship/self-determination) over their lands, villages, and treasures

    Key Difference: "Possession" vs "tino rangatiratanga" - ownership vs sovereignty/self-determination

    Article 3: Ōritetanga / Equality

    Both Versions (Similar)

    Māori are granted the same rights and privileges as British subjects

    Note: This article is generally consistent between versions

    💬 Key Discussion Points

    Understanding the Treaty

    1. What are the three main articles of Te Tiriti o Waitangi?
    2. Why were there two versions (English and Māori)?
    3. Which version did most rangatira sign? Why is this significant?

    Translation Differences

    1. What are the key differences between "sovereignty" and "kāwanatanga"?
    2. How does "tino rangatiratanga" differ from "possession"?
    3. Why do these translation differences matter today?

    Contemporary Significance

    1. Why is the Treaty of Waitangi still important today?
    2. What is the Waitangi Tribunal and what does it do?
    3. How does the Treaty affect modern New Zealand law and policy?
    4. What does it mean to call the Treaty a "living document"?

    Critical Thinking

    1. Do you think the Treaty has been honored? Provide evidence.
    2. What responsibilities do all New Zealanders have under the Treaty?
    3. How can young people contribute to Treaty-based relationships?

    🌟 Extension Activities

    Research Project

    Research a specific Treaty settlement (e.g., Ngāi Tahu, Tainui). What was claimed? What was the outcome? How has it affected the iwi today?

    Comparative Analysis

    Compare Te Tiriti o Waitangi with another founding document (e.g., US Constitution, Australian Constitution). What's similar? What's unique?

    Creative Response

    Create a visual representation (poster, infographic, video) explaining the Treaty to Year 7 students. Include both versions and key differences.

    Local Connection

    Interview a local kaumātua or iwi representative about what the Treaty means to their community. Share your findings respectfully.

    👨‍🏫 Teacher Notes

    Cultural Sensitivity & Protocol

    CRITICAL: The Treaty of Waitangi is a taonga (treasure) and must be treated with respect. When teaching:

    • Acknowledge both versions have validity and significance
    • Recognize ongoing debates and different perspectives
    • Invite local iwi perspectives when possible
    • Avoid presenting one "correct" interpretation
    • Create safe space for difficult discussions
    • Be aware of students' whakapapa and connections to Treaty issues

    Teaching Approach

    Start with video: Provides accessible overview

    Use primary sources: Show actual Treaty text (both versions)

    Multiple perspectives: Include Māori and Pākehā viewpoints

    Contemporary relevance: Connect to current events and settlements

    Differentiation

    Support: Provide glossary of key terms, simplified article summaries, graphic organizers

    Extension: Research specific Treaty claims, analyze Waitangi Tribunal decisions, explore constitutional implications
