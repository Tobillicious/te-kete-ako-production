    Tukutuku Station Questions

    Inquiry-based learning stations for exploring mathematical concepts in traditional Māori weaving patterns

    Mathematics Year 9 4-6 lessons
    1

    Pattern Recognition & Analysis

    🎯 Focus: Identifying mathematical patterns in tukutuku designs
    • What patterns do you observe?
      Look for repeating shapes, lines, and arrangements. Describe the pattern using mathematical language.
      Extension: Create a pattern key showing the basic unit that repeats.
    • How does the pattern grow?
      Identify if the pattern grows by addition, multiplication, or another rule. Test your hypothesis.
      Extension: Write the algebraic rule for your pattern.
    • What shapes are used?
      Identify geometric shapes (triangles, diamonds, rectangles) and count their occurrences.
      Extension: Calculate the area covered by each shape type.
    2

    Transformation & Symmetry

    🔄 Focus: Exploring geometric transformations in tukutuku patterns
    • Where do you see symmetry?
      Find lines of symmetry (reflectional, rotational, translational). Mark them on your pattern diagram.
      Extension: Calculate the order of rotational symmetry.
    • How can you transform the pattern?
      Demonstrate translation, rotation, and reflection. Record the coordinates before and after.
      Extension: Write transformation matrices for each operation.
    • What happens when you combine transformations?
      Perform two or more transformations in sequence. Describe the result.
      Extension: Determine if the order of operations matters.
    3

    Measurement & Proportional Reasoning

    📏 Focus: Applying measurement concepts to tukutuku patterns
    • What are the dimensions?
      Measure length, width, and angles. Record in both metric and traditional units.
      Extension: Convert between different measurement systems.
    • What is the ratio of shapes?
      Calculate the ratio of different shapes or colors in the pattern.
      Extension: Simplify the ratio and express it in different forms.
    • How much material is needed?
      Calculate the perimeter and area. Estimate material requirements for a full panel.
      Extension: Calculate cost based on material prices.
    4

    Cultural Significance & Storytelling

    🌿 Focus: Understanding the cultural meaning behind mathematical patterns
    • What stories do the patterns tell?
      Research the meaning behind different tukutuku patterns. Connect to iwi stories.
      Extension: Create your own pattern with a personal or whānau story.
    • How are patterns used in tikanga?
      Investigate how tukutuku patterns are used in marae ceremonies and gatherings.
      Extension: Interview kaumatua about pattern significance.
    • What mathematical knowledge did ancestors have?
      Identify advanced mathematical concepts embedded in traditional patterns.
      Extension: Compare traditional and modern mathematical approaches.
    5

    Creative Design & Problem Solving

    🎨 Focus: Applying mathematical understanding to create original patterns
    • Can you design a new pattern?
      Create an original tukutuku pattern using mathematical principles you've learned.
      Extension: Write a mathematical justification for your design choices.
    • How can you solve a design challenge?
      Given constraints (materials, size, colors), optimize your pattern design.
      Extension: Present your solution to the class for feedback.
    • What real-world applications exist?
      Research how these mathematical patterns are used in modern design, architecture, or technology.
      Extension: Create a portfolio showing traditional and modern applications.

    Teacher Implementation Notes

    • Set up 5 stations around the classroom
    • Provide materials: pattern samples, rulers, protractors, graph paper, colored pencils
    • Time: 15-20 minutes per station
    • Students work in small groups of 3-4
    • Rotate groups through all stations over 2-3 lessons
    • Collect student work for assessment portfolio

    📊 Assessment Criteria

    Mathematical Understanding

    • Accurate pattern identification and analysis
    • Correct application of transformation concepts
    • Precise measurements and calculations

    Cultural Connection

    • Understanding of pattern significance
    • Respectful engagement with tikanga Māori
    • Ability to connect mathematics to culture

    Creative Application

    • Original pattern design
    • Mathematical justification of choices
    • Problem-solving approach
