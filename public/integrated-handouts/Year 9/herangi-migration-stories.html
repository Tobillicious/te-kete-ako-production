    Social Sciences & English Year 9-10 2-3 lessons

    Ngā Hīkoi Whakamua: Journeys Forward

    Migration stories that connect past, present, and future

    "E kore au e ngaro, he kākano i ruia mai i Rangiātea" - I will never be lost, for I am a seed sown from Rangiātea

    📖 Personal Migration Stories

    🏠

    Leaving the Marae

    The decision to leave the ancestral marae was never easy. For generations, our whānau had lived in the shadow of the meeting house, where the carvings of our ancestors watched over us. But the city called with promises of work, education, and opportunity.

    "When my father packed our belongings into the old Ford, my grandmother cried. She said we were taking the mana of our ancestors with us, but she feared we might forget how to use it."

    The journey from rural to urban was more than just physical distance. It was a journey between worlds, between the familiar rhythms of marae life and the unfamiliar pace of city existence.

    🏙️

    Finding Our Place

    The city was overwhelming at first. So many people, so much noise, so little connection to the whenua. We lived in a state house in a neighborhood where we were the only Māori family. My mother planted kūmara in the small backyard, trying to create something familiar in this strange new place.

    "At school, I learned to be less Māori to fit in. I stopped speaking te reo in the playground. I pretended I didn't know about my marae, my whakapapa, my identity. It was easier that way, but it felt like I was betraying myself."

    Finding community took time. We eventually connected with other Māori families who had made similar journeys. Together, we created new traditions that honored our past while adapting to our present.

    🌱

    Growing Roots in New Soil

    As the years passed, we learned to navigate between two worlds. During the day, we were city kids, learning in English schools, playing in concrete playgrounds. But in the evenings and weekends, our parents worked hard to keep our culture alive.

    "My father started a kapa haka group in our community hall. Every Saturday, Māori families from all over the city would come together. We weren't just practicing performance; we were practicing being Māori together in this new environment."

    We learned that migration wasn't about leaving our culture behind - it was about carrying it with us and finding new ways to express it. The city became our new marae, our neighborhood our new hapū.

    🌟

    Becoming Bridge Builders

    Today, I see myself as a bridge between worlds. I understand the rural marae where my grandparents still live, and I understand the urban community where I've built my life. I speak both te reo Māori and English, not just out of necessity but out of love for both languages.

    "My children have never known life without both worlds. They navigate between marae and mall with ease. They see no contradiction in being both Māori and urban, traditional and modern. They are the future we dreamed of when we first made the journey."

    Migration has taught us that culture is not tied to place but to people. Wherever Māori gather, there is marae. Wherever we speak te reo, there is home.

    📅 Māori Migration Timeline

    Pre-1940s

    Most Māori lived in rural communities, often near ancestral marae and traditional lands.

    1940s-1950s

    Government policies encouraged Māori to move to cities for work. Many Māori men served in World War II and returned with new experiences and perspectives.

    1960s-1970s

    Urban Māori population grew rapidly. New challenges emerged around cultural identity, language maintenance, and community building in cities.

    1980s-1990s

    Māori cultural renaissance. Kōhanga reo movement began, urban marae were established, and Māori language gained official recognition.

    2000s-Present

    Digital age brings new opportunities for cultural connection. Māori navigate multiple identities while strengthening cultural foundations.

    🎯 Learning Activities

    Activity 1: Migration Interview

    Interview someone in your family or community who has experienced migration. What challenges did they face? How did they maintain their cultural identity?

    Interview Questions:

    • What prompted the migration?
    • What was left behind and what was gained?
    • How was cultural connection maintained?
    • What advice would they give to others?

    Activity 2: Creative Response

    Create a poem, song, or artwork that expresses the feelings and experiences of migration. Use imagery from the stories you've read.

    Creative Elements:

    • Contrast between old and new homes
    • Feelings of belonging or not belonging
    • Cultural symbols and their meanings
    • Hopes for the future

    Activity 3: Community Mapping

    Create a map showing migration journeys in your family or community. Mark significant places and events that shaped these journeys.

    Map Elements:

    • Starting points and destinations
    • Significant stops along the way
    • Challenges and opportunities encountered
    • Cultural connections maintained

    📊 Assessment Criteria

    Historical Understanding

    • Knowledge of Māori migration patterns
    • Understanding of historical context
    • Connection to broader social changes

    Empathy & Perspective

    • Engagement with personal stories
    • Understanding of migration challenges
    • Appreciation of diverse experiences

    Cultural Understanding

    • Recognition of cultural resilience
    • Understanding of identity maintenance
    • Connection to own cultural background
