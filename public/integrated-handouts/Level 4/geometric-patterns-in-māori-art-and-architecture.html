    Geometric Patterns in Māori Art and Architecture

    Cultural Context & Safety Notes

    He Whakapapa: Māori geometric patterns (such as kōwhaiwhai) hold deep cultural significance. They often represent genealogy (whakapapa), nature, and tribal identity. Always approach with respect.

    Kaitiakitanga: Some patterns are sacred (tapu). Ensure students understand that copying exact designs without permission may be inappropriate.

    Learning Objectives (NZC Aligned)

    • Explore symmetry, rotation, and tessellation in Māori patterns (Maths L4: Geometry & Measurement)
    • Understand cultural significance of kōwhaiwhai and tukutuku panels (Social Sciences L4)
    • Create original geometric designs inspired by Māori art (Arts L4)

    Key Concepts

    1. Kōwhaiwhai (Rafter Patterns)

    These repeating patterns use transformations:

    • Reflection symmetry (like the koru spiral)
    • Translation (repeating units)

    2. Tukutuku (Lattice Panels)

    Mathematical features:

    • Grid-based designs (Cartesian coordinates)
    • Tessellation (repeating shapes with no gaps)

    Activity: Design Your Own Kōwhaiwhai

    Materials: Grid paper, rulers, compasses, digital design tools (optional)

    1. Choose a base shape (e.g., koru, triangle)
    2. Apply one transformation (reflection/rotation/translation)
    3. Repeat to create a repeating pattern
    4. Explain the mathematical rules used

    Assessment Suggestions

    Formative: Peer feedback on pattern accuracy

    Summative Rubric:

    Criteria Achieved Merit Excellence
    Mathematical Accuracy Basic symmetry Two transformations Complex tessellation
    Cultural Respect Avoids sacred motifs Explains inspiration Links to local iwi designs

    Extension Activities

    • Digital Design: Use Google Drawings to create animated transformations
    • 3D Models: Build tukutuku panels with popsicle sticks
    • Research Project: Compare Māori patterns to Islamic geometric art

    Teacher Implementation Guide

    1. Pre-lesson: Invite a local kaumātua to discuss pattern meanings (optional)
    2. Starter: Show examples from your local marae (Google Earth works well)
    3. Differentiation:
      • Struggling learners: Provide pre-drawn grids
      • ESOL: Use visual glossaries (Māori/English)
      • Gifted: Challenge with fractal-based designs
    4. Plenary: Students present designs using geometry vocabulary
