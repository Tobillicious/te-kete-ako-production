üåä Wayfinding: Where Culture Meets Science

The Achievement

Polynesian navigators, including MƒÅori ancestors, achieved one of humanity's greatest feats: navigating thousands of kilometers across open ocean without instruments, discovering and settling every habitable island in the Pacific.

The Journey to Aotearoa: MƒÅori ancestors sailed from East Polynesia (likely Hawaiki) around 1250-1300 CE, navigating over 3,000 km of open ocean to reach Aotearoa New Zealand.

üìê Mathematics in Navigation

Calculations Navigators Use:

  • Distance = Speed √ó Time: Estimating how far traveled based on canoe speed
  • Angles: Measuring star heights above horizon (latitude estimation)
  • Ratios: Wave patterns and refraction angles
  • Geometry: Triangulation using multiple stars
  • Mental Mapping: Creating cognitive maps of ocean regions

Traditional navigators performed complex calculations mentally - no calculators or charts!

Cultural Sensitivity

IMPORTANT: Traditional navigation knowledge is sacred and was passed down through specialized training. When teaching:

  • Acknowledge this is simplified introduction to complex knowledge system
  • Recognize that full navigation knowledge took years of apprenticeship
  • Invite local navigators or waka hourua crew to share if possible
  • Celebrate contemporary MƒÅori navigation revival (Te Aurere, Haunui, etc.)
  • Connect to local iwi maritime traditions
