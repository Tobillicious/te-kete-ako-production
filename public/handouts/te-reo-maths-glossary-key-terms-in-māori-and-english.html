    Cultural Context and Tikanga Considerations

    This resource acknowledges the mana (prestige, authority) of Te Reo Māori as an official language of Aotearoa New Zealand. The mathematical terms presented here reflect both traditional Māori knowledge systems and contemporary mathematical language.

    Tikanga Guidelines:

    • Pronounce Māori words correctly - ask for help if unsure
    • Respect that some terms may have deeper cultural meanings beyond mathematics
    • Recognize that Māori mathematical knowledge has its own rich history
    • Use this glossary to support equitable access to mathematics for Māori learners

    Te Reo Maths Glossary

    Tau (Number)
    Number
    A mathematical object used to count, measure, and label.
    He aha te tau e whai ake nei i te 5? (What number comes after 5?)
    Tau ōrite (Even number)
    Even number
    An integer divisible by 2 without remainder.
    Ko te 2, te 4, te 6 ngā tau ōrite. (2, 4, 6 are even numbers.)
    Tau hērite (Odd number)
    Odd number
    An integer not divisible by 2 without remainder.
    Ko te 1, te 3, te 5 ngā tau hērite. (1, 3, 5 are odd numbers.)
    Tau maha (Multiple)
    Multiple
    A number that can be divided by another number without a remainder.
    Ko te 12 he tau maha o te 3. (12 is a multiple of 3.)
    Tāpiri (Addition)
    Addition
    The process of combining numbers to find their total.
    E hia te 5 me te 3? (How much is 5 plus 3?)
    Tangohanga (Subtraction)
    Subtraction
    The process of taking one number away from another.
    E hia te 5 tangohia te 3? (How much is 5 minus 3?)
    Whakarea (Multiplication)
    Multiplication
    Repeated addition of the same number.
    E hia te 5 whakareatia ki te 3? (How much is 5 multiplied by 3?)
    Whakawehe (Division)
    Division
    Splitting into equal parts or groups.
    E hia te 10 whakawehea ki te 2? (How much is 10 divided by 2?)
    Tapatoru (Triangle)
    Triangle
    A three-sided polygon.
    E toru ngā tapa o te tapatoru. (A triangle has three sides.)
    Tapawhā (Rectangle)
    Rectangle
    A four-sided shape with four right angles.
    E whā ngā tapa ōrite o te tapawhā. (A rectangle has four equal sides.)
    Porowhita (Circle)
    Circle
    A round shape where all points are equidistant from the center.
    He porowhita te āhua o te marama. (The moon is shaped like a circle.)
    Taurangi (Variable)
    Variable
    A symbol (usually a letter) that represents an unknown value.
    Ko te x he taurangi. (x is a variable.)
    Tātai (Equation)
    Equation
    A mathematical statement that shows two expressions are equal.
    Ko te 2x + 3 = 7 he tātai. (2x + 3 = 7 is an equation.)
    Pānga (Function)
    Function
    A relation that associates each element of one set to exactly one element of another set.
    Ko te f(x) = x² he pānga. (f(x) = x² is a function.)
    Inenga (Measurement)
    Measurement
    The process of determining the size, length, or amount of something.
    Me ine te roa o te tēpu. (We need to measure the length of the table.)
    Roa (Length)
    Length
    The measurement of something from end to end.
    E hia te roa o te whare? (What is the length of the house?)
    Horahanga (Area)
    Area
    The amount of space inside a two-dimensional shape.
    Me tātai te horahanga o te tapawhā. (We need to calculate the area of the rectangle.)
    Pūmau (Constant)
    Constant
    A value that does not change.
    Ko te π (pi) he pūmau. (π (pi) is a constant.)

    Extension Activities

    1. Create Your Own Glossary Cards

    Choose 5 additional mathematical terms not included in this glossary. Research their Māori translations and create your own glossary cards with definitions and examples.

    2. Mathematical Whakataukī (Proverbs)

    Research traditional Māori whakataukī that relate to mathematical concepts (e.g., counting, patterns, symmetry). Analyze how they reflect mathematical thinking.

    3. Bilingual Problem Solving

    Write and solve 3 word problems using Māori mathematical terms. Present your problems and solutions to the class in both languages.

    4. Cultural Connections

    Investigate how traditional Māori knowledge systems (e.g., star navigation, weaving patterns, carving designs) incorporate mathematical concepts. Present your findings.

    Teacher Implementation Notes

    Suggested Teaching Approaches:

    • Introduce 5-10 terms per week, building gradually
    • Use the terms consistently in classroom instruction
    • Create word walls with the Māori terms prominently displayed
    • Pair students to quiz each other on terms and definitions
    • Incorporate terms into your regular math problems and discussions

    Differentiation Strategies:

    • For beginners: Focus on recognition and pronunciation
    • For intermediate: Practice using terms in simple sentences
    • For advanced: Create original problems using the terms
    • For fluent speakers: Explore deeper cultural connections

    Assessment Ideas:

    • Oral quizzes where students define terms
    • Matching exercises (Māori to English terms)
    • Written explanations of concepts using both languages
    • Student-created presentations on mathematical concepts
