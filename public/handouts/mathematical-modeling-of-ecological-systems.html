    He Whakamōhiotanga | Introduction

    This resource explores how mathematical modeling can help us understand and protect Aotearoa's unique ecosystems, drawing on both Western science and Mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge).

    Cultural Context: Kaitiakitanga

    Kaitiakitanga refers to the Māori concept of guardianship and protection of the environment. Traditional ecological knowledge in Māori culture includes deep understanding of seasonal patterns, species relationships, and sustainable resource management practices that have been developed over centuries.

    Ngā Whāinga Ako | Learning Objectives

    By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

    • Develop mathematical models to represent predator-prey relationships in Aotearoa ecosystems
    • Apply differential equations to model population dynamics
    • Analyze how traditional ecological knowledge informs sustainable resource management
    • Evaluate the effectiveness of different conservation strategies using mathematical reasoning
    • Communicate mathematical findings in culturally responsive ways

    Cultural Safety Considerations

    • Respect that Mātauranga Māori is a living knowledge system with specific cultural protocols
    • Acknowledge the source communities of traditional ecological knowledge
    • Recognize that some knowledge may be tapu (sacred/restricted)
    • Consult with local iwi (tribes) when possible for authentic perspectives
    • Use Māori terms correctly and with proper pronunciation

    Activity 1: Modeling Predator-Prey Relationships

    Te Whakamahi i ngā Tauine Rerekē | Using Differential Equations

    The Lotka-Volterra equations model predator-prey interactions:

    \[ \frac{dx}{dt} = \alpha x - \beta xy \]

    \[ \frac{dy}{dt} = \delta xy - \gamma y \]

    Where:

    • x = prey population (e.g., native birds)
    • y = predator population (e.g., introduced pests)
    • α = prey growth rate
    • β = predation rate
    • δ = predator growth rate
    • γ = predator death rate

    Mātauranga Māori Connection

    Traditional Māori knowledge includes detailed understanding of species relationships. For example, the concept of whakapapa (genealogy) connects all living things, emphasizing interdependence in ecosystems.

    Task

    Using the Lotka-Volterra equations, model the relationship between a native species and an introduced predator in your local area. Consider how traditional knowledge might inform your initial conditions and parameters.

    Activity 2: Sustainable Harvesting Models

    Te Whakatupu Taonga | Resource Management

    Mathematical models can help determine sustainable harvesting rates for resources like fish (ika) or plants (rongoā).

    The Schaefer model for fisheries management:

    \[ H = qE X \]

    \[ \frac{dX}{dt} = rX \left(1 - \frac{X}{K}\right) - H \]

    Where:

    • H = harvest rate
    • q = catchability coefficient
    • E = fishing effort
    • X = fish population
    • r = intrinsic growth rate
    • K = carrying capacity

    Mātauranga Māori Connection

    Traditional Māori practices like rāhui (temporary prohibition) demonstrate sophisticated understanding of sustainable resource management. Rāhui allows ecosystems to recover by temporarily restricting access to resources.

    Task

    Develop a mathematical model to determine optimal harvesting rates for a local resource. Compare your results with traditional practices like rāhui. How do the mathematical and cultural approaches complement each other?

    Ngā Mahi Whakawhānui | Extension Activities

    Extension 1: Complex Systems Modeling

    Develop agent-based models to simulate more complex ecological interactions, incorporating multiple species and environmental factors. Consider how traditional knowledge about ecosystem interconnectedness (whakapapa) might inform your model structure.

    Extension 2: Climate Change Impacts

    Model how climate change might affect traditional food gathering practices (mahinga kai). Incorporate temperature and precipitation data to predict changes in resource availability.

    Extension 3: Cultural Algorithm Development

    Research how principles from Mātauranga Māori could inform the development of new mathematical algorithms for ecological management.

    Ngā Whakaritenga mō ngā Akoranga Rerekē | Learning Adaptations

    For Students Needing Additional Support:

    • Provide pre-made spreadsheet templates for population modeling
    • Use visual representations and graphing tools
    • Break complex problems into smaller, manageable steps
    • Offer sentence starters for written explanations

    For Gifted and Talented Students:

    • Challenge students to develop their own mathematical models
    • Encourage research into specific traditional ecological knowledge systems
    • Have students present their findings to authentic audiences
    • Explore connections to other disciplines (economics, policy, ethics)

    For Māori Medium Education:

    • Deliver content primarily in te reo Māori
    • Incorporate local pūrākau (traditional stories) that illustrate ecological principles
    • Connect mathematical concepts to traditional practices like tukutuku (lattice work) patterns

    Ngā Tohutohu Whakahaere | Implementation Guide

    Before Teaching:

    • Familiarize yourself with basic ecological modeling concepts
    • Research local iwi and their traditional ecological knowledge
    • Prepare digital resources accessible on Chromebooks
    • Test interactive elements to ensure they work on school networks

    Lesson Sequence (5-6 sessions):

    1. Session 1: Introduction to ecological modeling and Mātauranga Māori
    2. Session 2: Predator-prey models and traditional knowledge of species relationships
    3. Session 3: Sustainable harvesting models and rāhui
    4. Session 4: Model analysis and interpretation
    5. Session 5: Assessment task work session
    6. Session 6: Presentations and reflection

    Differentiation Strategies:

    • Provide tiered tasks with varying complexity
    • Offer choice in assessment format (written report, presentation, video)
    • Use mixed-ability grouping for collaborative activities
    • Incorporate hands-on activities like creating physical models
