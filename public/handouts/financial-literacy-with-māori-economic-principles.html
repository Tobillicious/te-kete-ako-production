    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

    • Explain key Māori economic concepts and their relevance today
    • Compare and contrast Māori and Western economic systems
    • Apply principles of manaakitanga (hospitality) and kaitiakitanga (guardianship) to financial decision-making
    • Analyze case studies of Māori businesses applying traditional values
    • Develop a personal financial plan incorporating Māori economic principles

    NZ Curriculum Links: Social Sciences L6-8, Economics L6-8, Business Studies L6-8

    Cultural Context: Māori Economic Systems

    Traditional Māori economics was based on collective wellbeing rather than individual profit. Key concepts include:

    Key Māori Economic Terms

    Click/tap to reveal definitions:

    Utu

    The concept of reciprocity and balance - not just monetary exchange but maintaining social equilibrium through giving and receiving.

    Koha

    A gift or contribution, often given without explicit expectation of return, but maintaining social bonds.

    Manaakitanga

    Hospitality and care for others, including sharing resources to enhance collective wellbeing.

    Kaitiakitanga

    Guardianship of resources for future generations, ensuring sustainable use.

    Whanaungatanga

    Relationship-building and kinship ties that form the foundation of economic interactions.

    Comparing Economic Systems

    Aspect Western Economics Māori Economics
    Primary Focus Individual profit maximization Collective wellbeing (ora)
    Resource View Commodities to be exploited Taonga (treasures) to be protected
    Time Horizon Short-term gains Intergenerational sustainability
    Success Measures GDP, profit margins Community health, cultural continuity

    Case Study Analysis: Modern Māori Businesses

    Read the following scenario and answer the questions:

    Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu: This iwi organization manages assets worth over $1.8 billion while maintaining strong cultural values. They:

    • Invest 20% of profits directly back into community initiatives
    • Prioritize employment of whānau members
    • Maintain sustainable fishing practices despite higher costs

    Discussion Questions:

    1. How does this approach differ from typical corporate behavior?
    2. What traditional Māori values can you identify in their practices?
    3. What might be the long-term benefits of this approach?

    Applying Māori Principles to Personal Finance

    Traditional Māori economic values can inform modern financial decisions:

    • Budgeting with manaakitanga: Allocate funds to support whānau/community
    • Saving with kaitiakitanga: Plan for future generations
    • Investing with whanaungatanga: Consider social impacts of investments
    • Spending with utu: Support businesses that give back

    Assessment Task: Personal Financial Plan

    Create a 12-month financial plan incorporating Māori economic principles. Your plan should include:

    • Income sources and budgeting
    • Saving and investment goals
    • Charitable/community contributions
    • Explanation of how Māori values inform each section

    Extension Activities

    Option 1: Interview a Māori business owner about how they balance cultural values with financial success. Present findings.

    Option 2: Research the concept of tapu and noa in economic contexts. How might these affect business practices?

    Option 3: Create a comparative analysis of two businesses - one Māori-led and one non-Māori - examining their financial and social outcomes.

    Teacher Implementation Notes

    Prior Knowledge: Students should have basic understanding of financial concepts like budgeting and saving.

    Cultural Considerations:

    • Consult with local iwi or Māori advisors if discussing specific tribal practices
    • Be aware that some students may have personal connections to case studies
    • Pronounce Māori terms correctly - practice with students

    Differentiation:

    • For less confident students: Provide template for financial plan
    • For advanced students: Incorporate more complex investment scenarios

    Timing: 4-6 lessons recommended for full implementation
