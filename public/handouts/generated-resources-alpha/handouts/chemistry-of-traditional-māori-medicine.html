Cultural Context

Rongoā Māori refers to traditional Māori healing practices that include the use of medicinal plants (rongoā rākau), spiritual healing, and physical therapies. This resource focuses on the scientific analysis of plant-based medicines while respecting their cultural significance.

When studying rongoā, we acknowledge that Māori knowledge (mātauranga Māori) and Western science offer complementary perspectives on health and healing.

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze the chemical composition of traditional Māori medicinal plants
  • Understand how active compounds interact with human biology
  • Compare traditional knowledge with scientific understanding
  • Evaluate the potential for developing modern medicines from traditional remedies
  • Demonstrate respect for Māori cultural knowledge in scientific contexts

Key Medicinal Plants and Their Chemistry

Kawakawa Kawakawa (Macropiper excelsum) Pain relief, skin conditions, digestive issues Myristicin, elemicin, lignans Anti-inflammatory, analgesic properties confirmed
Mānuka Mānuka (Leptospermum scoparium) Antiseptic, wound healing, cold remedy Methylglyoxal (MGO), flavonoids, triterpenes Strong antibacterial activity, especially in honey
Harakeke Harakeke (Phormium tenax) Wound dressing, splints, antiseptic Polyphenols, polysaccharides Antimicrobial and wound-healing properties
Koromiko Koromiko (Hebe stricta) Diarrhea treatment, skin conditions Phenolic glycosides, tannins Astringent and anti-diarrheal effects confirmed

Case Study: The Science Behind Mānuka Honey

The antibacterial properties of mānuka honey have been scientifically validated and led to a thriving industry. Key chemical aspects:

  • Methylglyoxal (MGO): The primary antibacterial compound that forms from dihydroxyacetone in the nectar
  • Phenolic compounds: Contribute to antioxidant effects
  • Low pH and high sugar content: Create an environment hostile to bacterial growth

Classroom Investigation: Testing Antimicrobial Properties

Note: This should be conducted under proper laboratory safety conditions.

Design an experiment to compare the antimicrobial effects of different honeys (including mānuka) using agar plates and bacterial cultures. Measure zones of inhibition.

Cultural and Safety Considerations

  • Always acknowledge the Māori origins of this knowledge
  • Never harvest native plants without proper permission and guidance
  • Some medicinal plants can be toxic if used incorrectly
  • Laboratory work with plant extracts requires proper safety equipment
  • Consult with local iwi when possible for authentic information

Assessment Suggestions

  1. Research Report: Choose one rongoā plant and analyze its chemistry and traditional uses (40%)
  2. Experimental Design: Propose a scientific investigation to test a medicinal claim (30%)
  3. Reflection: Discuss how Western science and mātauranga Māori can work together (30%)

Extension Activities

  • Investigate how traditional preparation methods (e.g., boiling, fermentation) affect chemical composition
  • Research pharmaceutical drugs derived from traditional medicines worldwide
  • Analyze the economic potential of rongoā-based products in New Zealand
  • Interview a tohunga rongoā (traditional healer) about their practice (with proper protocols)

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

  • Visual learners: Create molecular diagrams of active compounds
  • Kinesthetic learners: Prepare simple plant extracts (under supervision)
  • ESOL students: Provide bilingual vocabulary lists (Māori/English)
  • Advanced students: Research clinical trials involving traditional medicines
  • Struggling students: Focus on one plant in depth rather than multiple examples
