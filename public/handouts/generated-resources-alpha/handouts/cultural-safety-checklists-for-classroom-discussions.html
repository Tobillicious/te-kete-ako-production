    🌿 Ngā Tikanga Ako | Māori Pedagogical Approaches

    This resource lends itself to authentic Māori pedagogical practices:

    • Ako: Reciprocal teaching and learning - students and teachers learn together
    • Tuakana-teina: Peer teaching with more experienced students supporting others
    • Wānanga: Deep collaborative discussion and knowledge sharing
    • Mahi tahi: Working together towards shared understanding

    🛡️ Cultural Safety Considerations

    • Approach Māori content with respect and openness
    • Recognize students' diverse cultural backgrounds and experiences
    • Create space for Māori students to share their perspectives
    • Consult with whānau and local iwi for culturally sensitive topics

    Learning Objectives

    • Understand key principles of cultural safety (manaakitanga) in learning environments
    • Apply culturally responsive practices in classroom discussions
    • Develop skills to create and maintain wā whakaaro (safe thinking spaces)
    • Reflect on personal and institutional practices that support Māori student success
    • Implement practical strategies aligned with Te Tiriti o Waitangi principles

    Cultural Context: Tikanga Considerations

    Key Māori Concepts for Safe Learning Spaces

    Manaakitanga: Creating an environment of care, respect and hospitality where all students feel valued.

    Whanaungatanga: Building positive relationships and connections between all class members.

    Wairuatanga: Recognizing and respecting spiritual dimensions of learning.

    Ako: Understanding that teaching and learning is reciprocal - teachers learn from students.

    Tūrangawaewae: Ensuring every student has a place to stand and contribute with confidence.

    Extension Activities

    1. Cultural Safety Audit

    Work in groups to audit your school environment (physical spaces, policies, practices) for cultural safety. Present findings with recommendations.

    2. Whakataukī (Proverb) Analysis

    Research Māori proverbs about learning and discuss how they could inform classroom practice (e.g., "He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tangata, he tangata, he tangata" - What is the most important thing in the world? It is people, it is people, it is people).

    3. Student Voice Project

    Interview Māori students about their experiences of cultural safety in learning environments (with appropriate ethical considerations).

    Teacher Implementation Notes

    Key Considerations

    • These checklists should be adapted to your specific classroom context
    • Build relationships with your local Māori community for guidance
    • Cultural safety is an ongoing journey - be open to learning and making mistakes
    • Balance high expectations with culturally responsive support

    Implementation Tips

    • Start small - focus on 1-2 checklist items at a time
    • Involve students in developing classroom norms
    • Use the checklists for self-reflection and professional development
    • Pair with a colleague to observe and give feedback on each other's practice
