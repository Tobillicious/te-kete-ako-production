Te Arotake Pāngarau mō ngā Pūnaha Hauropi

Kaupapa: This unit explores how mathematical models can help us understand ecological systems, integrating Western scientific approaches with mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge systems) about environmental relationships.

Learning Objectives

  • Develop mathematical models to represent predator-prey relationships in Aotearoa ecosystems
  • Analyze population data using differential equations and difference equations
  • Understand traditional Māori ecological knowledge about species interdependence
  • Compare and contrast Western scientific and Māori perspectives on ecological balance
  • Apply mathematical modeling to real-world conservation challenges in New Zealand

Cultural Context and Safety Notes

Cultural Considerations: When discussing Māori ecological knowledge, emphasize that this is living knowledge still practiced today, not just historical information. Always acknowledge the iwi or source of any traditional knowledge shared.

Safety: Some students may have strong emotional connections to conservation issues. Be mindful when discussing species decline or extinction.

Mathematical Modeling of Predator-Prey Relationships

Activity 1: The Lotka-Volterra Model

Let's model the relationship between kiore (Polynesian rat) and kākāpō populations:

The basic Lotka-Volterra equations:

\[ \frac{dx}{dt} = \alpha x - \beta xy \]

\[ \frac{dy}{dt} = \delta xy - \gamma y \]

Where:

  • x = prey population (kākāpō)
  • y = predator population (kiore)
  • α = prey growth rate
  • β = predation rate
  • δ = predator growth efficiency
  • γ = predator mortality rate

Interactive Exploration

Use this Desmos simulation to adjust parameters and observe population changes.

Question: What happens when you increase the predation rate (β)? How does this reflect historical observations of kākāpō decline?

Māori Perspectives on Ecological Balance

Traditional Māori knowledge speaks of whakapapa (genealogical connections) between all living things. The concept of mauri (life force) helps explain why populations naturally fluctuate.

Whakataukī (proverb): "Ka ngaro te kākāpō, ka ngaro te kākā, ka ngaro te kererū, ka ngaro te tangata." (When the kākāpō disappears, when the kākā disappears, when the kererū disappears, mankind will disappear.)

Activity 2: Incorporating Traditional Knowledge into Models

Research traditional Māori practices like rāhui (temporary prohibition) and kaitiakitanga (guardianship). How might these concepts be represented mathematically in population models?

Modify the Lotka-Volterra equations to include:

  1. A periodic function representing seasonal rāhui
  2. A conservation term representing kaitiakitanga efforts

Extension Activities

Advanced Modeling Challenge

Develop a three-species model incorporating:

  1. Predator (e.g., stoat)
  2. Primary prey (e.g., kiwi)
  3. Secondary food source (e.g., berries)

Research how Māori lunar calendars (maramataka) relate to species activity cycles and incorporate these periodic patterns into your model.

Teacher Implementation Guide

Step-by-Step Implementation

  1. Preparation (1 lesson):
    • Introduce basic predator-prey concepts using local examples
    • Invite a local kaumatua or expert to discuss traditional ecological knowledge (or use pre-recorded interviews)
  2. Model Development (2-3 lessons):
    • Teach Lotka-Volterra equations with biological interpretation
    • Have students work with the Desmos simulation
  3. Cultural Integration (1-2 lessons):
    • Discuss Māori perspectives on ecological balance
    • Explore how traditional practices might be represented mathematically
  4. Application Project (2-3 lessons):
    • Students select a local ecological relationship to model
    • Incorporate both scientific and cultural knowledge
  5. Reflection and Assessment (1 lesson):
    • Students present findings and reflect on learning

Resources Needed

  • Chromebooks or computers with internet access
  • Graphing software (Desmos, GeoGebra, or Excel)
  • Local ecological data (DOC websites or regional council data)
  • Interviews with Māori knowledge holders (check Landcare Research resources)

Conclusion

This unit demonstrates how mathematical modeling can bridge Western science and mātauranga Māori to better understand and protect Aotearoa's unique ecosystems. By combining these knowledge systems, we develop more holistic approaches to conservation challenges.
