    Cultural Context

    Traditional Māori economic systems were based on principles of reciprocity, sustainability, and collective wellbeing. Key concepts include:

    • Utu - The principle of balance and reciprocity
    • Manaakitanga - Hospitality, generosity and care for others
    • Kaitiakitanga - Guardianship and sustainable management of resources
    • Whanaungatanga - Relationships and kinship connections
    • Tikanga - Correct procedures and protocols

    Tikanga Considerations: When discussing Māori economic systems, it's important to:

    • Recognize the spiritual dimension of resources (mauri)
    • Understand that economic activity was never separated from social and cultural life
    • Acknowledge regional variations in practices between iwi
    • Respect the living nature of these traditions in contemporary Māori business

    Māori Economic Principles in Practice

    Case Study: Traditional Resource Management

    The hākari (feast) system demonstrates Māori economic principles:

    • Food was collected, prepared and distributed according to strict protocols
    • Abundance demonstrated mana (prestige) of the hosts
    • Guests were expected to reciprocate in future events
    • Nothing was wasted - preservation techniques ensured sustainability

    Discussion: How might these principles apply to modern budgeting and financial planning?

    Western Economic Characteristics

    • Focus on individual wealth accumulation
    • Profit maximization as primary goal
    • Resources seen as commodities
    • Short-term financial gains often prioritized
    • Market-driven decision making

    Māori Economic Characteristics

    • Collective wellbeing as primary goal
    • Intergenerational sustainability focus
    • Resources have spiritual dimension (mauri)
    • Reciprocity and balance (utu) central
    • Economic activity integrated with cultural practices

    Modern Applications

    Contemporary Māori businesses often blend traditional values with modern practices:

    • Many iwi corporations prioritize both profit and cultural outcomes
    • Koha-based pricing models in some Māori tourism businesses
    • Employment practices that support whānau development
    • Environmental sustainability as core business principle

    Activity: Research a Māori business and identify how traditional economic principles influence their operations.

    Financial Literacy Applications

    Budgeting with Māori Values

    Create a personal budget that incorporates Māori economic principles:

    1. Allocate funds for koha (gifts/donations)
    2. Include savings for future hui (gatherings)
    3. Plan for sustainable purchases (consider kaitiakitanga)
    4. Budget for supporting whānau needs
    5. Include long-term intergenerational planning

    Financial Decision Maker

    What would you do in these scenarios? Consider both financial and cultural factors:

    Scenario 1: You receive an unexpected $500. Do you:

    Good long-term planning! Consider how this aligns with kaitiakitanga for future generations.

    This shows manaakitanga. Could you do both - help now and save some?

    Excellent kaitiakitanga! Sustainable choices benefit everyone long-term.

    Scenario 2: You're choosing between two jobs:

    Financial security is important. Could you use this position to create positive change?

    This aligns with whanaungatanga. Consider long-term career growth too.

    Smart thinking! Many employers value cultural skills - could you propose adding Māori perspectives?

    Teacher Implementation Notes

    Prior Knowledge: Students should have basic understanding of personal finance concepts (budgeting, saving, investing).

    Cultural Safety:

    • Consult with local Māori experts or kaumātua where possible
    • Be aware that some students may have varying levels of cultural knowledge
    • Create an environment where all perspectives are valued

    Differentiation:

    • For less confident students: Focus on comparing simple financial decisions
    • For advanced students: Explore complex iwi asset management cases
    • Provide glossaries of Māori economic terms

    Assessment Tips:

    • Focus on understanding rather than perfect Māori language use
    • Allow for diverse expressions of cultural understanding
    • Consider group presentations for less confident students

    Glossary of Māori Economic Terms

    Hapori: Community

    Hua: Product, yield, fruit

    Kaitiaki: Guardian

    Koha: Gift, donation (often reciprocal)

    Mana: Prestige, authority

    Mauri: Life force, vitality

    Rahui: Temporary prohibition to conserve resources

    Taonga: Treasure, valued resources

    Tikanga: Correct procedure, custom

    Utu: Reciprocity, balance

    Whakapapa: Genealogy, interconnectedness

    Whānau: Extended family
