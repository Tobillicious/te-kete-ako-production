Exploring Traditional Māori Ecological Knowledge with Mathematics

Learning Objectives (NZC Level 7-8 Mathematics)

  • Apply calculus to model real-world environmental changes.
  • Understand how kaitiakitanga (guardianship) informs sustainable resource management.
  • Use differentiation and integration to analyze population growth and resource depletion.
  • Compare Western mathematical models with mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge).

Cultural Context & Safety Considerations

This lesson integrates mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge) with calculus to explore sustainable environmental practices. Key concepts:

  • Kaitiakitanga: The Māori principle of guardianship over land and resources.
  • Rāhui: A traditional conservation practice restricting resource use.
  • Taiao: The interconnected environment.

Cultural Safety: Ensure discussions respect Māori perspectives. If possible, consult local iwi (tribal) knowledge holders.

Mathematical Modeling Activity

Case Study: Fish Population & Sustainable Harvesting

Traditional rāhui practices regulate fishing to prevent overharvesting. We will model fish population growth using calculus.

Interactive Exploration: Logistic Growth Model

The population P(t) of a fish species follows:

\[ \frac{dP}{dt} = rP \left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) - H \]

Where:

  • r = growth rate
  • K = carrying capacity
  • H = harvest rate (linked to rāhui restrictions)

Task: Use differentiation to find the maximum sustainable yield.

Teacher Implementation Guide

  1. Introduction (20 min): Discuss kaitiakitanga and introduce the fish population case study.
  2. Modeling (30 min): Derive the logistic growth equation and solve for equilibrium points.
  3. Application (20 min): Students calculate sustainable harvest rates under different rāhui conditions.
  4. Discussion (15 min): Compare calculus-based models with traditional knowledge.

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

  • Visual Learners: Graph population models using Desmos.
  • Struggling Students: Provide step-by-step breakdowns of derivatives.
  • Gifted Students: Extend to stochastic models for environmental uncertainty.
