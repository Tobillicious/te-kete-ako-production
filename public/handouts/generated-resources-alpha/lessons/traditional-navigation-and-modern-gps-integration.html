    🌿 Ngā Tikanga Ako | Māori Pedagogical Approaches

    This resource lends itself to authentic Māori pedagogical practices:

    • Ako: Reciprocal teaching and learning - students and teachers learn together
    • Tuakana-teina: Peer teaching with more experienced students supporting others
    • Wānanga: Deep collaborative discussion and knowledge sharing
    • Mahi tahi: Working together towards shared understanding

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

    • Explain key principles of traditional Māori navigation (te kōkōrangi)
    • Describe how celestial bodies, ocean currents, and wildlife were used for wayfinding
    • Compare traditional navigation methods with modern GPS technology
    • Analyze the scientific principles underlying both systems
    • Create a hybrid navigation model combining traditional and modern techniques

    Cultural Context: Tikanga Considerations

    This lesson explores mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge systems) related to navigation (whakatere waka). It's important to approach this topic with respect for the deep ancestral knowledge (mātauranga tuku iho) preserved by Polynesian navigators.

    Key Concepts:

    • Te kōkōrangi: Māori astronomical knowledge
    • Te whānau mārama: The family of light (stars, moon, sun)
    • Te ara o te waka: The path of the canoe
    • Kaitiakitanga: Guardianship of knowledge and environment

    Protocols to Observe:

    • Acknowledge the iwi (tribal) origins of specific navigation knowledge
    • Recognize that some knowledge may be tapu (sacred) and not for public discussion
    • Use te reo Māori terms correctly with macrons where appropriate
    • Emphasize the living nature of this knowledge system

    Check Tonight's Stars

    Key Navigation Indicators:

    Indicator Māori Term Scientific Principle
    Star paths Ara o ngā whetū Celestial navigation using fixed star positions
    Ocean swells Ngaru Wave patterns indicate land masses
    Bird flight patterns Huarahi manu Seabirds return to land at dusk
    Cloud formations Kapua Clouds gather over islands
    Water color Tae o te moana Reflection changes near land

    Modern GPS Technology

    Global Positioning Systems (GPS) use satellite networks to determine precise locations:

    How GPS Works

    1. 24+ satellites orbit Earth
    2. Each transmits its position and time signal
    3. Receivers calculate position by timing signals from ≥4 satellites
    4. Triangulation determines exact location

    Compare & Contrast Activity

    Feature Traditional Navigation GPS Navigation
    Energy Source Natural environment Electrical/battery
    Accuracy ±100km (open ocean) ±5 meters
    Failure Points Cloud cover, storms Satellite outages, battery
    Knowledge Required Years of training Basic operation
    Environmental Impact Minimal Electronic waste

    Hands-on Activity: Hybrid Navigation Model

    Task: Design a navigation system that combines traditional Māori knowledge with modern GPS technology.

    Steps:

    1. Identify 3 traditional navigation methods that could complement GPS
    2. Explain how each method would provide redundancy if GPS failed
    3. Create a diagram of your hybrid system
    4. List advantages of your integrated approach

    Materials:

    • Paper/pencils or digital drawing tools
    • Star charts (provided)
    • Ocean current maps (provided)
    • GPS demonstration (phone app)

    Assessment Rubric

    Criteria Advanced (4) Proficient (3) Developing (2) Beginning (1)
    Understanding of Traditional Methods Accurately explains ≥3 methods with te reo terms Explains 2-3 methods correctly Describes 1-2 methods with some errors Limited understanding shown
    GPS Technology Analysis Detailed explanation of GPS with advantages/limitations Clear explanation of basic GPS function Basic description with some inaccuracies Minimal understanding shown
    Hybrid System Design Innovative integration with clear redundancy plan Logical combination of methods Basic combination with limited integration Little evidence of integration
    Cultural Respect Exceptional use of te reo and cultural protocols Appropriate cultural references Some cultural references present Limited cultural consideration

    Use online tools to simulate a voyage from Hawaiki to Aotearoa using only traditional methods.

    3. Interview Project

    Research contemporary Polynesian voyaging (e.g., Hōkūleʻa) and interview crew members about how they blend traditional and modern methods.

    Teacher Implementation Notes

    Prior Knowledge:

    • Basic understanding of Earth's rotation and celestial movements
    • Familiarity with latitude/longitude concepts
    • Introduction to Māori perspectives in science

    Suggested Timing:

    • Introduction: 1 period
    • Traditional methods: 2 periods
    • GPS technology: 1 period
    • Hybrid activity: 2 periods
    • Presentations/assessment: 1 period

    Differentiation:

    Support: Provide pre-labeled star charts and guided comparison tables
    Extension: Research specific iwi navigation traditions or current waka voyages

    Safety Considerations:

    • If doing night sky observations, follow school safety policies
    • Be mindful of students with visual impairments for star-related activities
