    🌿 Ngā Tikanga Ako | Māori Pedagogical Approaches

    This resource lends itself to authentic Māori pedagogical practices:

    • Ako: Reciprocal teaching and learning - students and teachers learn together
    • Tuakana-teina: Peer teaching with more experienced students supporting others
    • Wānanga: Deep collaborative discussion and knowledge sharing
    • Mahi tahi: Working together towards shared understanding

    💡 Teacher: Consider how these approaches can be authentically integrated into your lesson delivery.

    Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori

    Pūrākau are traditional Māori narratives that carry knowledge, values, and worldviews. They follow specific structures and often contain:

    • Whakapapa (genealogical connections)
    • Kōrero tuku iho (handed down knowledge)
    • Symbolic representations of natural phenomena
    • Lessons about relationships and responsibilities

    Tikanga Considerations

    • Respect the sacred nature of some stories - check with local kaumātua if using specific tribal narratives
    • Ensure proper pronunciation of Māori words (provide audio support if possible)
    • Balance creativity with cultural authenticity
    • Acknowledge sources of stories and inspiration

    Pūrākau Story Structure

    Traditional pūrākau often follow this pattern:

    1. Tīmatanga (Beginning)

    Establish setting and characters, often connected to the natural world

    2. Pakiwaitara (Journey/Challenge)

    The main events where characters face challenges or discoveries

    3. Taumata (Climax)

    The turning point where important lessons are revealed

    4. Whakamutunga (Resolution)

    How the world is changed by the events, often explaining natural phenomena

    5. Whakataukī (Proverb/Lesson)

    The enduring wisdom carried by the story

    Digital Storytelling Activity

    Create Your Digital Pūrākau

    Step 1: Choose a theme connected to te taiao (the natural environment)

    Step 2: Develop your story using the pūrākau structure

    Step 3: Create your digital version using one of these formats:

    • Google Slides storybook
    • Canva animated presentation
    • Simple video using school devices
    • Interactive story using Twine (basic HTML version)

    Step 4: Include at least 3 Māori words/concepts with explanations

    Step 5: Present to your whānau group

    Extension Activities

    1. Comparative Analysis

    Compare a traditional pūrākau with a contemporary Māori story (e.g., from Witi Ihimaera or Patricia Grace). Identify how the structure persists or changes.

    2. Community Connections

    Interview a kaumātua about local stories and create a digital version with their guidance.

    3. Cross-Cultural Study

    Compare pūrākau structure with another indigenous storytelling tradition (e.g., Aboriginal Dreamtime stories).

    Teacher Implementation Notes

    Preparation

    • Familiarize yourself with local pūrākau - consult with Māori colleagues or community experts
    • Prepare examples of digital stories using simple tools
    • Create a list of appropriate themes connected to your local environment

    Differentiation

    • Support: Provide story starters or partial templates
    • Extension: Challenge students to create interactive stories with multiple pathways

    Cultural Safety

    • Establish guidelines for respectful engagement with Māori knowledge
    • Invite Māori students to share their perspectives (without putting them on the spot)
    • Check pronunciation of Māori words using Māori Dictionary

    Technical Notes

    • All suggested tools work on Chromebooks with minimal setup
    • For video projects, recommend simple tools like WeVideo or school-approved alternatives

    👥 Peer Assessment Protocol

    Tuakana-Teina Approach:

    1. Two stars: What did they do well?
    2. One wish: What could be improved?
    3. Connection: How does this relate to our learning?

    Encourage manaakitanga (respect) in all feedback
