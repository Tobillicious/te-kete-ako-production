    Unit Focus: Dr. Ranginui Walker - The Challenge to the Narrative

    Year Level: Year 10 Social Studies | Duration: 5 lessons × 75 minutes

    📋 Unit At a Glance

    Lesson Topic Key Learning
    1.1 Who was Ranginui Walker? Biography, three roles (teacher/academic/activist), dominant narrative concept
    1.2 The Great Migration Rural to urban migration, urban Māori identity, social changes
    1.3 Years of Anger Ngā Tamatoa, 1970s protest movements, activism strategies
    1.4 A Forum for Justice Waitangi Tribunal, Treaty settlements, institutional change
    1.5 Reclaiming the Narrative "Ka Whawhai Tonu Mātou", historiography, legacy

    🎯 Learning Outcomes

    By the end of this unit, students will:

    • Understand the life and significance of Dr. Ranginui Walker as historian, activist, and academic
    • Analyze the impact of the Great Migration on Māori communities and identity
    • Evaluate the role of protest movements (Ngā Tamatoa) in achieving social change
    • Explain the purpose and impact of the Waitangi Tribunal
    • Critically examine how historical narratives are constructed and challenged
    • Connect Walker's work to ongoing issues of tino rangatiratanga and social justice

    📦 Required Resources (Entire Unit)

    Per Student:

    • Exercise book or folder for unit work
    • 5 handouts (1 per lesson) - available in /handouts/walker/

    Teacher Materials:

    • Lesson plans for all 5 lessons (detailed 75-min sequences)
    • Assessment rubrics for formative and summative tasks
    • Extension materials for advanced learners
    • Support scaffolds for students needing help
    • Cultural protocols guide

    🌿 Cultural Considerations

    Before Teaching This Unit:

    1. Consult with whānau: Consider inviting kaumātua or community members who knew of Walker's work
    2. Cultural safety: Some students may have personal connections to the protest movements or Treaty settlements discussed
    3. Te reo Māori: Ensure correct pronunciation of key terms (practice with audio resources if needed)
    4. Balanced perspective: While Walker challenged Pākehā narratives, present his work as adding necessary Māori perspectives, not attacking individuals
    5. Contemporary relevance: Connect historical struggles to current issues with sensitivity

    📊 Assessment Strategy

    Formative Assessment (Ongoing):

    • Daily DO NOW responses (checking engagement and understanding)
    • Pair/group work observations
    • Exit tickets at lesson end
    • Vocabulary checks

    Summative Assessment (End of Unit):

    • Option 1: Extended essay - "How did Dr. Ranginui Walker challenge the dominant narrative of NZ history?"
    • Option 2: Presentation - "A Māori Renaissance Figure" (research Walker or related activist)
    • Option 3: Creative - Timeline poster showing Walker's life in historical context
