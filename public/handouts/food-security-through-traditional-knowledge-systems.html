    Kaupapa Māori Concept

    "Mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge systems) and kaitiakitanga (guardianship) offer sustainable solutions to modern food security challenges."

    Learning Objectives

    • Understand traditional Māori agricultural practices and their ecological principles
    • Analyze how indigenous knowledge contributes to food security
    • Compare traditional and modern approaches to sustainable food production
    • Apply mātauranga Māori concepts to contemporary environmental challenges
    • Develop solutions for local food security issues using traditional knowledge

    Traditional Māori Agricultural Systems

    1. Māra kai (Food gardens)

    Reconstruction of a traditional Māori garden (Source: Te Ara)

    Traditional Māori gardens were carefully planned ecosystems that:

    • Used companion planting to maximize space and nutrients
    • Employed natural pest control methods
    • Followed lunar cycles for planting and harvesting (maramataka)
    • Maintained soil fertility through crop rotation

    Activity: Design Your Māra kai

    Using the principles above, design a school garden that incorporates traditional Māori practices. Consider:

    • Which plants grow well together in your area?
    • How can you manage pests without chemicals?
    • What natural fertilizers could you use?

    2. Rongoā (Medicinal plants)

    Māori cultivated plants for both food and medicine, creating integrated systems where:

    • Medicinal plants were grown alongside food crops
    • Plants served multiple purposes (nutrition, medicine, fiber)
    • Knowledge of plant properties was passed down generations

    3. Ahumoana (Aquaculture)

    Traditional Māori aquaculture systems included:

    • Pātiki (flounder) traps
    • Hīnaki (eel pots)
    • Seasonal harvesting to maintain stocks
    • Protection of spawning grounds

    Modern Applications

    Case Study: Te Wao Nui Project

    Research this urban Māori food forest project in Auckland that combines traditional knowledge with modern permaculture principles.

    Discussion: How does this project address both food security and cultural revitalization?

    Assessment Task

    Create a proposal for a school or community food security project that incorporates at least three traditional Māori practices. Your proposal should include:

    1. Explanation of the traditional practices you're using
    2. How these address modern food security challenges
    3. A plan for implementation
    4. Expected outcomes and benefits

    Extension Activities

    • Interview local kaumātua about traditional food practices in your area
    • Research how climate change might affect traditional food systems
    • Compare Māori food systems with other indigenous agricultural systems
    • Develop a maramataka (lunar calendar) for your region's planting cycle

    Cultural Context & Safety Notes

    • Always acknowledge the source of Māori knowledge
    • Recognize that some knowledge may be tapu (sacred/restricted)
    • Consult with local iwi or kaumātua when possible
    • Use correct pronunciation of Māori terms (provide audio links if possible)
    • Present Māori knowledge as a living, evolving system - not just "historical"

    Teacher Implementation Guide

    Lesson 1: Introduction to Traditional Systems (60 mins)

    1. Begin with a karakia (optional)
    2. Pre-assessment: "What do you know about traditional Māori food systems?"
    3. Present overview of key concepts
    4. Small group research on different aspects (gardens, fishing, preservation)
    5. Group presentations

    Lesson 2: Modern Applications (60 mins)

    1. Case study analysis
    2. Field trip (real or virtual) to local food projects
    3. Begin design project

    Lesson 3: Project Development (2-3 lessons)

    1. Research phase
    2. Draft proposals
    3. Peer feedback
    4. Final presentations

    Adaptations for Diverse Learners

    For students needing more support:

    • Provide template for proposal with sentence starters
    • Offer choice of presentation format (poster, video, etc.)
    • Pair with peer mentor
    • Break project into smaller steps with checkpoints

    For English Language Learners:

    • Provide bilingual glossary of key terms
    • Use visual supports and diagrams
    • Allow use of first language in early planning stages

    For advanced learners:

    • Research deeper into specific traditional practices
    • Develop quantitative measures for project outcomes
    • Connect with local Māori food initiatives
    • Create implementation timeline with milestones

    🌿 Cultural Note: When implementing, consider authentic Māori pedagogical approaches (ako, tuakana-teina, wānanga) and ensure cultural safety throughout.

    ## Comprehensive Teacher Notes ### Cultural Safety Considerations 1. **Protocols**: Begin with appropriate karakia if comfortable, or invite a kaumātua. Acknowledge the mana whenua of your area. 2. **Language Use**: Ensure correct pronunciation of Māori terms. Consider having students practice with each other first. 3. **Knowledge Sharing**: Emphasize that mātauranga Māori is a living knowledge system, not just historical information. 4. **Sensitive Topics**: Some traditional food sources may now be scarce - handle discussions about this with care. ### Implementation Tips 1. **Prior Knowledge**: Assess students' familiarity with Māori perspectives early to address gaps. 2. **Local Connections**: Where possible, connect with local Māori food initiatives or experts. 3. **Hands-on Learning**: Incorporate practical activities like planting according to maramataka. 4. **Digital Options**: For virtual field trips, explore: - Te Papa's online collections - Virtual tours of traditional gardens - Recorded interviews with Māori food experts ### Assessment Alternatives 1. **Formative Options**: - Concept maps showing connections between traditional and modern practices - Reflection journals on learning progress - Peer teaching of specific concepts 2. **Summative Alternatives**: - Create instructional videos about traditional practices - Design infographics comparing food systems - Develop a school-wide food security campaign ### Extension Project Ideas 1. **Community Engagement**: - Partner with local marae on a food project - Organize a traditional food preparation day - Create educational resources for younger students 2. **Research Pathways**: - Investigate scientific validation of traditional practices - Study traditional Māori plant breeding techniques - Explore traditional food preservation methods ### Technology Integration 1. **Digital Tools**: - Use Google Earth to examine traditional land use patterns - Create digital timelines of agricultural changes - Build collaborative documents for research 2. **Accessibility Features**: - Text-to-speech for reading support - Video transcripts for hearing impaired - High contrast versions for visually impaired This resource provides a culturally grounded, curriculum-aligned approach to exploring food security through mātauranga Māori while meeting diverse learning needs in New Zealand classrooms.
