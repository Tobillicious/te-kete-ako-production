    Critical Thinking Skills: Media Literacy & Information Analysis

    Year 9-13 | Developing Sophisticated Analysis Skills

    This comprehensive unit develops students' critical thinking capabilities through systematic analysis of information, media, and knowledge systems. Using New Zealand contexts and Indigenous knowledge frameworks, students learn to evaluate sources, identify bias, construct arguments, and communicate insights effectively.

    "Mā te māramatanga ka kitea ai te tika" - Through understanding, truth will be seen

    Critical thinking illuminates truth and guides wise decision-making

    🌿 Cultural Context | Horopaki Ahurea

    🏛️ Connection to Mangakōtukutuku College Values

    Whaimana: Integrity - Standing firm in truth and principle

    whaiara by encouraging students to engage with content in ways that embody this value.

    • Approach Māori content with respect and openness
    • Recognize students' diverse cultural backgrounds and experiences
    • Create space for Māori students to share their perspectives
    • Consult with whānau and local iwi for culturally sensitive topics

    He Whakataukī - Our Guiding Wisdom

    "Kia mārama ai te pō, kia atahua ai te ao"

    Let the darkness be made clear, so the world may be beautiful. This whakataukī guides our critical thinking journey - we illuminate truth not for its own sake, but to create a more just and beautiful world. Through careful analysis and discernment, we separate truth from deception, wisdom from mere information.

    Critical Thinking Pedagogical Framework

    Core Learning Vision

    • Source Evaluation Mastery: Systematic frameworks for assessing information credibility
    • Bias Recognition: Identifying personal, cultural, and institutional biases in media and sources
    • Argument Construction: Building logical, evidence-based arguments using multiple perspectives
    • Cultural Lens Analysis: Understanding how worldviews shape interpretation and knowledge
    • Effective Communication: Presenting critical insights clearly and persuasively

    Station Rotation Methodology

    • Duration: 10 lessons with 22 interactive resources
    • Structure: 4 stations per lesson, 15-minute rotations
    • Collaboration: Mixed-ability groups with peer teaching
    • Assessment: Portfolio-based with authentic tasks
    • NZ Context: RNZ, NZhistory.govt, and Te Ao Māori integration

    Lesson 1: Information Literacy & Source Evaluation

    "Kia mōhio ai koe ki te taonga o te mātauranga" - Know the treasure of knowledge

    Learning Focus: Foundation Skills

    Students develop systematic approaches to evaluating information sources, learning to distinguish credible information from misinformation. Using CRAAP analysis and Indigenous knowledge validation frameworks, they practice source evaluation with New Zealand examples.

    Station Activities (4)

    • CRAAP Test with NZ Government sources
    • Wikipedia vs academic source comparison
    • Social media fact-checking challenges
    • Mātauranga Māori validation frameworks

    Resources (3)

    • Source Evaluation Matrix
    • NZ Context Source Bank
    • Digital Literacy Toolkit

    Lesson 2: Logical Fallacies & Argument Analysis

    "Kia mārama ai te tapatahi" - Let the truth be clear

    Learning Focus: Argument Deconstruction

    Students learn to identify common logical fallacies and analyze argument structure using New Zealand political examples. They practice constructing logical arguments while understanding cultural differences in reasoning approaches.

    Station Activities (4)

    • Fallacy identification with NZ political speeches
    • Tino Rangatiratanga argument analysis
    • Climate change reasoning challenges
    • Cultural reasoning pattern exploration

    Resources (2)

    • Logical Fallacies Guide (NZ Examples)
    • Argument Analysis Framework

    Lesson 3: Media Bias & News Literacy

    "Kia whakatōhea ai ngā kōrero" - Let the stories be shared collectively

    Learning Focus: Media Analysis with RNZ

    Students analyze media bias and framing using RNZ articles as primary examples. They learn to identify perspective, ownership influences, and cultural framing while developing news literacy skills for the digital age.

    Station Activities (4)

    • RNZ vs commercial media comparison
    • Headline bias analysis workshop
    • Source diversity audit
    • Cultural framing investigation

    Resources (2)

    • RNZ Media Analysis Framework
    • Bias Detection Toolkit

    Lesson 4: Historical Analysis & Evidence

    "Kia maumahara ai tātou ki ō tātou tīpuna" - Remember our ancestors

    Learning Focus: Historical Evidence with NZhistory.govt

    Students develop historical thinking skills using NZhistory.govt resources, learning to evaluate primary sources, understand historical context, and recognize multiple perspectives on past events.

    Station Activities (4)

    • Treaty primary source analysis
    • Land Wars multiple perspectives
    • Women's suffrage evidence evaluation
    • Oral history validation methods

    Resources (2)

    • NZHistory Investigation Toolkit
    • Primary Source Analysis Guide

    Lesson 5: Scientific Thinking & Hypothesis Testing

    "Mā te whakamātau ka mōhio ai" - Through testing we will know

    Learning Focus: Scientific Method & Indigenous Knowledge

    Students explore scientific thinking methods while respecting Indigenous ways of knowing. They learn hypothesis formation, evidence evaluation, and the integration of empirical and traditional knowledge systems.

    Station Activities (4)

    • Hypothesis formation workshop
    • Traditional ecological knowledge validation
    • Research design challenges
    • Evidence vs. anecdote analysis

    Resources (2)

    • Scientific Thinking Framework
    • Knowledge Systems Integration Guide

    Lesson 6: Statistical Reasoning & Data Interpretation

    "Mā nga tatau ka kitea ai te pono" - Through numbers the truth will be seen

    Learning Focus: Data Literacy & Critical Statistics

    Students develop statistical literacy through analysis of New Zealand data, learning to identify misleading statistics, understand correlation vs. causation, and critically evaluate data visualizations and research claims.

    Station Activities (4)

    • NZ Census data analysis challenges
    • Misleading graph detection
    • Correlation vs. causation cases
    • Survey methodology evaluation

    Resources (2)

    • Statistical Reasoning Toolkit
    • Data Visualization Analysis Guide

    Lesson 7: Perspective Taking & Cultural Lenses

    "He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tangata, he tangata, he tangata" - What is the most important thing? It is people

    Learning Focus: Cultural Perspectives & Worldviews

    Students explore how cultural lenses shape interpretation and understanding. Using Te Ao Māori frameworks and diverse cultural perspectives, they learn to recognize their own worldview while respectfully engaging with others.

    Station Activities (4)

    • Cultural lens analysis workshop
    • Pūrākau vs Western narrative structures
    • Multiple perspective historical events
    • Worldview mapping exercise

    Resources (3)

    • Mātauranga Māori Thinking Framework
    • Cultural Perspective Analysis Tool
    • Worldview Comparison Matrix

    Lesson 8: Problem-Solving Frameworks

    "Mā te whakarerekē ka pai ai" - Through transformation it will be good

    Learning Focus: Systematic Problem-Solving

    Students learn systematic approaches to complex problem-solving, integrating Western analytical frameworks with Indigenous holistic thinking. They practice with real New Zealand challenges requiring critical analysis.

    Station Activities (4)

    • Housing crisis analysis framework
    • Environmental problem-solving models
    • Social justice issue investigation
    • Collective vs individual solution approaches

    Resources (2)

    • Problem-Solving Framework Toolkit
    • Holistic Analysis Model

    Lesson 9: Decision-Making Models

    "Kia rite ai ngā whakatau" - Let decisions be appropriate

    Learning Focus: Ethical Decision-Making

    Students explore decision-making models that consider multiple factors, stakeholders, and consequences. They learn ethical reasoning frameworks and practice with scenarios requiring careful value-based choices.

    Station Activities (4)

    • Ethical dilemma decision trees
    • Stakeholder impact analysis
    • Cultural values in decision-making
    • Long-term consequence modeling

    Resources (2)

    • Decision-Making Framework
    • Ethical Reasoning Guide

    Lesson 10: Synthesis & Critical Communication

    "Kia rongo ai te katoa" - Let everyone hear and understand

    Learning Focus: Capstone Communication Project

    Students synthesize their critical thinking learning by creating comprehensive communication pieces that demonstrate analysis skills, cultural competency, and effective argumentation. This culminating lesson showcases growth and application.

    Station Activities (4)

    • Portfolio curation workshop
    • Peer review and feedback
    • Presentation skills development
    • Reflection and goal-setting

    Resources (2)

    • Critical Communication Portfolio
    • Reflection Framework

    Assessment Framework - Critical Thinking Portfolio

    Portfolio Components

    • Source Evaluation Samples: 3 detailed analyses using different frameworks
    • Media Analysis Project: Comprehensive RNZ investigation with bias detection
    • Historical Investigation: Multi-perspective analysis using NZhistory.govt sources
    • Critical Communication Piece: Student choice format demonstrating synthesis
    • Reflection Journal: Growth tracking and metacognitive awareness

    Assessment Criteria

    • Analysis Depth: Systematic application of critical thinking frameworks
    • Cultural Competency: Respectful integration of multiple knowledge systems
    • Evidence Use: Effective selection and evaluation of supporting evidence
    • Communication Clarity: Clear, persuasive presentation of insights
    • Metacognitive Awareness: Reflection on learning and thinking processes

    Unit Reflection & Continued Growth

    Critical thinking is not a destination but a journey of continuous growth. Students complete this unit with practical frameworks for analysis, deep respect for diverse knowledge systems, and communication skills that serve justice and truth.

    "Kia mārama ai te pō, kia atahua ai te ao" - Let darkness be made clear, so the world may be beautiful.

    These critical thinking skills serve students throughout their academic journey and civic participation, enabling thoughtful engagement with complex issues and respectful dialogue across difference.
