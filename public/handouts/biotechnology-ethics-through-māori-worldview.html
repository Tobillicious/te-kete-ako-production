Biotechnology Ethics Through MƒÅori Worldview

Exploring the intersection of science, ethics, and Te Ao MƒÅori

Introduction

Biotechnology offers incredible potential to improve human health, enhance food production, and address environmental challenges. However, these scientific advances raise important ethical questions that require careful consideration from multiple perspectives, including those grounded in Te Ao MƒÅori (the MƒÅori worldview).

This resource explores how MƒÅori ethical frameworks can inform our understanding of biotechnology issues, providing a uniquely Aotearoa New Zealand perspective on these global challenges.

Cultural Note

When engaging with MƒÅori perspectives, it's important to approach with respect and openness. MƒÅori knowledge systems (mƒÅtauranga MƒÅori) are holistic and interconnected, viewing humans as part of the natural world rather than separate from it.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

  • Explain key MƒÅori ethical concepts and their relevance to biotechnology
  • Analyse biotechnology issues through both Western and MƒÅori ethical frameworks

Key MƒÅori Ethical Concepts

Whakapapa (Genealogy and Interconnectedness)

Whakapapa refers to the genealogical connections between all living things, tracing relationships back to the beginning of time. This concept emphasizes that humans are related to the natural world, not separate from it.

Biotechnology application: Genetic modification challenges traditional understandings of whakapapa by creating new relationships between species that don't exist in nature.

Kaitiakitanga (Guardianship)

Kaitiakitanga is the responsibility to care for and protect the environment, resources, and people for future generations.

Biotechnology application: Biotechnology must be evaluated based on its long-term impacts on ecosystems and future generations, not just immediate benefits.

Mana (Authority, Prestige, Spiritual Power)

Mana refers to the spiritual power and authority inherent in all things. Different forms include mana whenua (authority over land), mana tangata (authority through people), and mana atua (spiritual authority).

Biotechnology application: Genetic manipulation may be seen as interfering with the mana of organisms, potentially diminishing their intrinsic value.

Tapu and Noa (Sacred and Common)

Tapu refers to things that are sacred, restricted, or set apart, while noa refers to things that are common, accessible, or free from restrictions.

Biotechnology application: Some biotechnologies may challenge traditional understandings of what should be considered tapu, such as genetic manipulation of human embryos.

Mauri (Life Force)

Mauri is the life force or essence that exists in all living things, connecting them to the spiritual realm.

Biotechnology application: Genetic modification may be viewed as interfering with or altering the mauri of an organism.

Case Studies in Biotechnology Ethics

Case Study 1: Genetic Modification of Native Species

Scenario: Scientists propose genetically modifying kauri trees to make them resistant to kauri dieback disease.

Ethical Analysis Framework

Consider this issue through different ethical lenses:

Ethical Framework Key Questions Potential Perspectives
Utilitarian (Western) Does this create the greatest good for the greatest number? Potential to save an iconic species vs. unknown ecological risks
Kaitiakitanga Does this fulfill our responsibility to protect this taonga for future generations? Preserving kauri vs. altering their fundamental nature
Whakapapa How does this affect the genealogical relationships of kauri? Creating a new lineage separate from natural kauri

Discussion Prompt: How might different iwi (tribes) with kauri in their rohe (territory) view this proposal differently?

Implementation Guide for Teachers

Before Teaching

  1. Familiarize yourself with basic MƒÅori concepts and their relevance to biotechnology ethics.
  2. Research local perspectives - consider reaching out to MƒÅori colleagues or community members for guidance.
  3. Prepare resources - gather articles, videos, and case studies that present diverse viewpoints.
  4. Establish classroom protocols for respectful discussion of sensitive cultural topics.

Lesson Sequence (Suggested 4-5 lessons)

  1. Introduction to Biotechnology Ethics (1 lesson)
    • Explore common biotechnologies and their ethical implications
    • Introduce basic Western ethical frameworks (utilitarianism, deontology, etc.)
  2. MƒÅori Worldview and Ethics (1-2 lessons)
    • Introduce key MƒÅori concepts (whakapapa, kaitiakitanga, mana, tapu/noa, mauri)
    • Compare and contrast with Western frameworks
  3. Case Study Analysis (1-2 lessons)
    • Apply both frameworks to specific biotechnology scenarios
    • Facilitate respectful debate and discussion
  4. Synthesis and Assessment (1 lesson)
    • Students develop their own ethical position on a biotech issue
