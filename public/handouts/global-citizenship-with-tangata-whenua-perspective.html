    He Whakamārama | Overview

    This resource explores global citizenship through the lens of Tangata Whenua (people of the land) perspectives, connecting Māori worldviews with global issues and responsibilities.

    Cultural Context Note

    This resource acknowledges the unique position of Māori as Tangata Whenua of Aotearoa while exploring their role as global citizens. We recognize that Indigenous perspectives offer valuable insights into sustainable relationships with people and planet.

    Ngā Whāinga Ako | Learning Objectives

    By engaging with this resource, students will be able to:

    • Explain key Māori concepts related to global citizenship
    • Analyze how kaitiakitanga (guardianship) applies to global environmental challenges
    • Compare Western and Indigenous perspectives on global citizenship
    • Identify ways to apply whanaungatanga (relationships) to global issues
    • Develop a personal action plan for ethical global citizenship

    Ngā Mahi | Learning Activities

    Activity 1: Global Kaitiakitanga

    Time: 45 minutes

    Resources: Chart paper, markers, internet access

    Instructions:

    1. In small groups, research a global environmental issue (e.g., climate change, plastic pollution, biodiversity loss)
    2. Analyze how principles of kaitiakitanga could inform solutions
    3. Create a visual representation showing traditional Māori environmental practices alongside modern solutions
    4. Present your findings to the class

    Activity 2: Whanaungatanga Across Borders

    Time: 60 minutes

    Resources: Case studies of Indigenous communities globally, comparison template

    Instructions:

    1. Research an Indigenous community from another country (e.g., First Nations Canada, Aboriginal Australians, Sami people)
    2. Identify similarities and differences in their worldview compared to Te Ao Māori
    3. Create a Venn diagram showing these connections
    4. Discuss how these shared Indigenous perspectives could shape a more inclusive form of global citizenship

    Aromatawai | Assessment

    Assessment Options

    Option 1: Global Citizenship Portfolio

    Students create a portfolio demonstrating their understanding of Tangata Whenua perspectives on global citizenship, including:

    • Reflections on key Māori concepts
    • Analysis of a global issue through a Māori worldview
    • Personal action plan for ethical global citizenship

    Option 2: Collaborative Project

    Groups develop a proposal for addressing a global challenge using principles of kaitiakitanga and manaakitanga.

    Ngā Mahi Whakawhānui | Extension Activities

    For Advanced Learners

    Extension 1: Indigenous Rights and Global Governance

    Research the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) and analyze how it aligns with concepts of tino rangatiratanga. Create a presentation on how global governance structures could better incorporate Indigenous perspectives.

    Extension 2: Comparative Indigenous Environmentalism

    Compare Māori environmental practices with those of another Indigenous culture. Identify common principles and unique approaches. Develop a proposal for how these combined approaches could address a specific global environmental challenge.

    Extension 3: Decolonizing Global Citizenship

    Critically examine mainstream concepts of global citizenship through a decolonizing lens. Develop an alternative framework that centers Indigenous worldviews and challenges Western hegemony in global citizenship education.

    Ngā Whakaritenga | Adaptations

    For Diverse Learning Needs

    Visual Learners

    • Provide graphic organizers for comparing concepts
    • Use infographics to illustrate Māori concepts
    • Incorporate videos of Māori perspectives on global issues

    Kinesthetic Learners

    • Create physical representations of concepts using art materials
    • Role-play scenarios applying Māori principles to global challenges
    • Design and build models of sustainable communities

    Students Needing Additional Support

    • Provide vocabulary lists with clear definitions
    • Use sentence starters for reflections
    • Offer templates for organizing research
    • Break activities into smaller, manageable steps

    Māori and Pasifika Students

    • Create opportunities for students to share their cultural knowledge
    • Validate and incorporate students' cultural perspectives
    • Connect learning to students' own communities and experiences

    Ngā Tohutohu mā te Kaiako | Teacher Implementation Guide

    Before Teaching

    • Familiarize yourself with the key Māori concepts in this resource
    • Connect with local Māori experts or community members who could contribute
    • Prepare resources that represent diverse Indigenous perspectives
    • Consider your own positionality and cultural background
