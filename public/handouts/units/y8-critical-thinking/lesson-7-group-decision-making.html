Group Decision-Making

Year 8 Critical Thinking Unit | 45 minutes

Collaborative problem-solving using MƒÅori values & consensus-building

üë®‚Äçüè´ Learning Objectives

  • Apply collaborative decision-making frameworks to real scenarios
  • Use MƒÅori values of whanaungatanga and kotahitanga in group processes
  • Practice consensus-building and compromise strategies
  • Evaluate decision-making processes for fairness and effectiveness
  • Use digital tools for collaborative surveying and data collection

Key Vocabulary:

  • Whakatau tikanga (to establish proper procedure)
  • Whanaungatanga (relationships, connections)
  • Kotahitanga (unity, consensus)
  • Mahi tahi (working together)
  • Consensus - agreement reached by group discussion
  • Compromise - finding middle ground between positions

Resources Needed:

  • Chromebooks with Google Forms access
  • Flipchart paper and markers for each group
  • Printed decision-making worksheets
  • Timer for activities
  • Grant scenario information sheets

ü§ù Starter: Quick Decision Challenge 8 minutes

Group Scenario (Groups of 4-5):

Your group has won a pizza party for the class! You have 3 minutes to decide:
  • What 3 pizza flavors to order (from 8 options)
  • Whether to include garlic bread or dessert with remaining budget
  • When to hold the party (lunch break vs. after school)
Catch: Everyone in the group must be happy with the final decision!

Process Observation:

  1. Round 1 (3 mins): Groups attempt to decide quickly
  2. Reflection (3 mins): What was difficult? What strategies emerged?
  3. Share (2 mins): Groups report their experience

Key Debrief Questions: "Which groups reached consensus? What helped? What hindered the process? How did you handle disagreement?"

üí∞ Main Activity: The $10,000 Community Grant Decision 25 minutes

Collaborative Problem-Solving Challenge

The Situation:

Your class has been selected to allocate a $10,000 community grant from the local council. Three worthy causes have applied, but you can only fund ONE project. Your decision must be made as a class using fair and inclusive processes.

Option A: Kaitiaki Environmental Project

Request: $10,000 to restore native bush area behind school

  • Benefits: Habitat for native birds, outdoor classroom, carbon absorption
  • Impact: Long-term environmental benefit, educational opportunities
  • Community: Involves local iwi, environmental groups, future students
  • Timeline: 2-3 years to see full results

Option B: Rangatahi Sports Equipment Program

Request: $10,000 for sports equipment available to all local schools

  • Benefits: Improved fitness, team building, after-school programs
  • Impact: Immediate use, healthier community, reduced screen time
  • Community: All young people in area, promotes physical wellbeing
  • Timeline: Equipment purchased and distributed within 2 months

Option C: Creative Arts Hub

Request: $10,000 to establish community arts space in unused building

  • Benefits: Music, drama, visual arts programs for all ages
  • Impact: Cultural expression, intergenerational connection, skill development
  • Community: Brings different generations together, preserves cultural practices
  • Timeline: 6 months setup, ongoing programs thereafter

MƒÅori-Inspired Decision Process: Whakatau Rangap≈´

Step 1: Whakat≈çhea (Respectful Discussion) - 8 minutes

  • Form groups of 6-7 students
  • Each person shares initial thoughts without interruption
  • Focus on whanaungatanga - consider community connections
  • Record all perspectives on flipchart paper

Step 2: Whakaaro Tawhiti (Deep Thinking) - 8 minutes

  • Discuss long-term vs short-term impacts
  • Consider: Who benefits most? What are potential problems?
  • Apply values: sustainability, equity, community wellbeing
  • Identify areas of agreement and disagreement

Step 3: Whakatau (Decision) - 6 minutes

  • Seek kotahitanga - unity where possible
  • If consensus isn't reached, explore compromise options
  • Ensure all voices are heard and respected
  • Prepare to explain reasoning to other groups

Step 4: Whakapuaki (Share/Present) - 3 minutes

  • Each group presents their decision and reasoning
  • Listen respectfully to all perspectives
  • Note similarities and differences in approaches

Teacher Facilitation Guidelines:

  • Emphasize Process: Focus on HOW decisions are made, not just WHAT is decided
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Model respectful discussion, ensure all voices heard
  • Manage Conflict: If groups struggle, pause and discuss effective collaboration strategies
  • Time Management: Keep groups on track but allow organic discussion within timeframes

üí¨ Cultural Learning: Traditional MƒÅori Decision-Making 7 minutes

Understanding Hui and Consensus Processes

Key Elements of Traditional MƒÅori Decision-Making:

  • Whakat≈çhea: Everyone has opportunity to speak without interruption
  • Whakaaro nui: Deep consideration of all perspectives and consequences
  • Kotahitanga: Working toward unity and agreement
  • Mana: Respecting the dignity and authority of all participants
  • Whakat≈çhea: Taking time needed for proper consideration

Reflection Questions:

  • How did using these principles affect your group's discussion?
  • What was different about seeking consensus vs. just voting?
  • When might consensus-building be better than majority rule?
  • How could these approaches help in family or friend group decisions?

üìä Assessment Rubric

Collaborative Participation (4 points):

  • 4 - Excellent: Actively contributes, listens respectfully, builds on others' ideas, facilitates inclusion
  • 3 - Good: Participates meaningfully, shows respect for others, contributes to consensus
  • 2 - Developing: Some participation, generally respectful, occasional contributions
  • 1 - Beginning: Limited participation, difficulty with collaborative process

Cultural Values Application (4 points):

  • 4 - Excellent: Demonstrates clear understanding and application of MƒÅori values in group process
  • 3 - Good: Shows some understanding and application of cultural values
  • 2 - Developing: Basic awareness of cultural values, limited application
  • 1 - Beginning: Little evidence of cultural values understanding

Decision-Making Process (4 points):

  • 4 - Excellent: Follows systematic process, considers multiple perspectives, reaches reasoned conclusion
  • 3 - Good: Generally follows process, considers different viewpoints
  • 2 - Developing: Some attempt at systematic approach, limited perspective-taking
  • 1 - Beginning: Unsystematic approach, difficulty considering multiple viewpoints
