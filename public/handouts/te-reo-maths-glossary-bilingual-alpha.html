    Kuputaka Pāngarau - Mathematical Terms in Te Reo Māori

    Te Kete Ako | Mathematics | Years 7-13 | Alpha Build Resource

    Supporting bilingual mathematical learning and cultural identity

    🌟 Cultural Context - Te Taiao Pāngarau (Mathematical Environment)

    This glossary honors the rich mathematical traditions within Te Ao Māori while supporting students to engage with mathematical concepts in both English and Te Reo Māori. Mathematics exists in all cultures - from traditional navigation (kaitiaki moana) to architectural patterns in wharenui.

    Usage Protocol: These terms should be used respectfully and accurately. When in doubt about pronunciation or cultural context, consult with Te Reo Māori speakers or cultural advisors.

    🔍 Search Terms - Rapu Kupu

    How to Use This Glossary:
    • For Students: Use these terms to build mathematical vocabulary in both languages
    • For Teachers: Incorporate Te Reo mathematical terms to support Māori-medium learners
    • For Whānau: Help support mathematical learning at home in your preferred language
    • Pronunciation: Listen to fluent Te Reo speakers when possible for accurate pronunciation

    Basic Operations - Mahi Taketake Years 7-8

    Addition
    Tāpiri
    (TAH-pee-ree)
    The mathematical operation of combining two or more numbers
    Example: 5 + 3 = 8
    Tāpiri: rima me toru, kotahi tekau mā waru
    Subtraction
    Tango
    (TAHN-goh)
    The mathematical operation of taking away one number from another
    Example: 10 - 4 = 6
    Tango: kotahi tekau, tango whā, ono
    Multiplication
    Whakarea
    (fah-kah-REH-ah)
    The mathematical operation of repeated addition
    Example: 4 × 3 = 12
    Whakarea: whā whakarea e toru, kotahi tekau mā rua
    Division
    Whakawehe
    (fah-kah-WEH-heh)
    The mathematical operation of splitting into equal parts
    Example: 15 ÷ 3 = 5
    Whakawehe: kotahi tekau mā rima whakawehe e toru, rima

    Numbers - Tau Years 7-9

    Fraction
    Hautanga
    (HAU-tahn-gah)
    A number representing part of a whole
    Example: ½ = hautanga hawhe (half)
    Decimal
    Ira ā-ira
    (EE-rah AH ee-rah)
    Numbers expressed using a decimal point
    Example: 3.5 = toru ira ā-ira rima
    Percentage
    Ōrau
    (OH-rah-oo)
    A proportion expressed as parts per hundred
    Example: 75% = whitu tekau mā rima ōrau
    Integer
    Tau tōpū
    (TAU toh-POO)
    Whole numbers including negative numbers
    Example: -3, 0, 5 are all tau tōpū

    Geometry - Ahupūngao Years 8-10

    Triangle
    Tapatoru
    (tah-pah-TOH-roo)
    A polygon with three sides and three angles
    Example: Traditional Māori kites often use tapatoru shapes
    Circle
    Porohita
    (poh-roh-HEE-tah)
    A round shape where all points are equal distance from the center
    Example: The poi ball moves in a porohita path
    Rectangle
    Tapawhā roa
    (tah-pah-FAH roh-ah)
    A four-sided shape with opposite sides equal and parallel
    Example: Many traditional woven panels show tapawhā roa
    Angle
    Koki
    (KOH-kee)
    The space between two lines that meet at a point
    Example: The koki of a wharenui roof is carefully calculated

    Measurement - Inenga Years 7-11

    Length
    Roa
    (ROH-ah)
    The measurement of how long something is
    Example: Te roa o te waka - the length of the canoe
    Area
    Rahi
    (RAH-hee)
    The amount of space inside a 2D shape
    Example: Te rahi o te māra - the area of the garden
    Volume
    Rōrahi
    (roh-RAH-hee)
    The amount of space inside a 3D object
    Example: Te rōrahi o te wai - the volume of water
    Weight
    Taumaha
    (TAU-mah-hah)
    How heavy something is
    Example: Te taumaha o te kūmara - the weight of the kumara

    Statistics - Tauanga Years 9-13

    Data
    Raraunga
    (rah-rah-OO-ngah)
    Information collected for analysis
    Example: Raraunga about student achievement
    Average/Mean
    Toharite
    (toh-hah-REE-teh)
    The sum of values divided by the number of values
    Example: Te toharite o nga māka - the average of the marks
    Graph
    Kauwhata
    (KAU-fah-tah)
    A visual representation of data
    Example: He kauwhata e whakaatu ana i nga hua - a graph showing the results
    Probability
    Tūpono
    (TOO-poh-noh)
    The likelihood that something will happen
    Example: Te tūpono o te ua - the probability of rain

    Algebra - Ariā Years 9-13

    Variable
    Taupe
    (TAU-peh)
    A symbol (like x) that represents an unknown number
    Example: Ki te x = 5, ka kitea te taupe
    Equation
    Whārite
    (FAH-ree-teh)
    A mathematical statement showing two expressions are equal
    Example: 2x + 3 = 7 he whārite
    Formula
    Tawhā
    (tah-FAH)
    A mathematical rule or relationship
    Example: Te tawhā mo te rahi: roa × whānui
    Function
    Taumahi
    (TAU-mah-hee)
    A relationship between input and output values
    Example: f(x) = 2x he taumahi hoki

    🏛️ Mathematical Connections in Te Ao Māori

    Traditional Applications:

    • Navigation (Kaitiaki Moana): Complex calculations using stars, currents, and wind patterns
    • Architecture (Whare Whakatū): Precise geometric principles in wharenui construction
    • Weaving (Raranga): Mathematical patterns in kete, korowai, and tukutuku panels
    • Carving (Whakairo): Proportional relationships and symmetry in traditional designs
    • Agriculture (Ahuwhenua): Seasonal calculations and resource management
    • Astronomy (Kōkōrangi): Calendar systems and celestial mathematics

    📚 Teacher Implementation Guide

    Classroom Strategies:

    • Daily Practice: Use one Te Reo mathematical term each lesson
    • Visual Displays: Create bilingual mathematical word walls
    • Cultural Connections: Link mathematical concepts to Māori cultural practices
    • Student Voice: Encourage students to use both languages in mathematical discourse
    • Assessment: Accept mathematical solutions explained in either language

    Supporting All Learners:

    • Te Reo Māori Speakers: Validate home language in mathematical contexts
    • English Language Learners: Use visual and contextual supports
    • Cultural Diversity: Encourage sharing of mathematical terms from all cultures
    • Special Needs: Adapt pronunciation guides and provide audio supports
