    Traditional Māori Navigation Mathematics - Lesson 1

    Name: _________________ Date: __________ Year: 8

    🏛️ Whakataukī (Cultural Opening)

    Māori: "Ko te pae tawhiti whāia kia tata, ko te pae tata whakamaua kia tīna"

    English: "Seek the distant horizons, hold fast to those close at hand"

    Relevance: This whakataukī reminds us that navigation requires both big-picture thinking (distant horizons) and attention to detail (what's close at hand) - just like mathematics!

    📚 Learning Objectives

    • Understand how traditional navigators divided the horizon into 32 directions
    • Calculate angles between star directions
    • Apply mathematical concepts to real-world navigation problems
    • Appreciate the sophistication of Māori mathematical knowledge

    🌿 Cultural Context | Horopaki Ahurea

    🌟 Part 1: Understanding the Star Compass

    Traditional Polynesian navigators, including Māori navigators, divided the horizon into 32 equal directions. This created a sophisticated navigation system based on the rising and setting positions of stars.

    Question 1: Basic Division

    If the full circle around the horizon is 360 degrees, and navigators divided it into 32 equal directions, how many degrees are between each direction?

    Show your working:

    360° ÷ 32 = ___________

    Question 2: Angle Between Directions

    If you're facing one star direction and need to turn to the direction 4 positions clockwise, what angle would you turn through?

    Show your working:

    Number of positions × Degrees per position = ___________

    Question 3: Real-World Application

    A navigator starts facing the direction where the star Matariki (Pleiades) rises. They need to change course to follow a star that is 8 positions counter-clockwise. Calculate:

    1. The angle they need to turn (in degrees)
    2. Whether this is more or less than a right angle (90°)

    Show your working:

    🎨 Part 2: Draw Your Own Star Compass

    Using a protractor and ruler, create your own simplified star compass with 8 equal directions (simplified from 32 for this exercise).

    Question 4: Planning Your Compass

    Before you draw, calculate: If you divide a circle into 8 equal parts, how many degrees are between each direction?

    Show your working:

    360° ÷ 8 = ___________

    Draw your 8-direction star compass here using a protractor

    Question 5: Label Your Compass

    Label the 8 main directions on your compass. Start with North at the top, and mark each direction clockwise:

    • North (0°)
    • Northeast (45°)
    • East (90°)
    • Southeast (135°)
    • South (180°)
    • Southwest (225°)
    • West (270°)
    • Northwest (315°)

    🧮 Part 3: Navigation Mathematics

    Question 6: Multiple Direction Changes

    A traditional navigator makes the following course changes during a voyage:

    • Turns 3 positions clockwise
    • Then turns 2 positions counter-clockwise
    • Then turns 5 positions clockwise

    Calculate the total net change in direction (using the 32-direction system where each position = 11.25°)

    Show your working:

    Question 7: Cultural Connection

    Traditional navigators memorized the positions of many stars. If a navigator needed to know 32 different star positions for rising and 32 for setting, how many star positions in total did they need to memorize?

    Show your working:

    Question 8: Extension Challenge ⭐

    If Earth rotates 360° in 24 hours, how many degrees does it rotate in 1 hour? How does this relate to why star positions change throughout the night?

    Show your working and explain your thinking:

    💭 Part 4: Reflection

    Question 9: Cultural Understanding

    What does the sophisticated mathematical system used by traditional navigators tell you about Māori and Polynesian knowledge systems?

    Question 10: Personal Connection

    How is traditional navigation similar to modern GPS systems? How is it different?

    👩‍🏫 Teacher Answer Key

    Question 1:

    Answer: 360° ÷ 32 = 11.25° between each direction

    Question 2:

    Answer: 4 positions × 11.25° = 45° (which is half a right angle)

    Question 3:

    Answer:

    • a) 8 positions × 11.25° = 90° (counter-clockwise)
    • b) This is exactly one right angle

    Question 4:

    Answer: 360° ÷ 8 = 45° between each direction

    Question 6:

    Answer: 3 CW - 2 CCW + 5 CW = 6 net positions clockwise = 6 × 11.25° = 67.5°

    Question 7:

    Answer: 32 + 32 = 64 total star positions to memorize

    Question 8:

    Answer: 360° ÷ 24 hours = 15° per hour. This explains why stars appear to move across the sky - it's actually Earth rotating!

    Assessment Rubric:

    • Excellent (4): All calculations correct, clear working shown, cultural understanding demonstrated
    • Good (3): Most calculations correct, working shown, basic cultural understanding
    • Satisfactory (2): Some calculations correct, some working shown
    • Needs Improvement (1): Few correct answers, working not shown

    Differentiation Notes:

    • Support: Provide calculators, simplified numbers, worked examples
    • Extension: Research actual star names used by Māori navigators, calculate for 32 directions, investigate spherical navigation
    • EAL: Provide visual diagrams, mathematical vocabulary sheet, paired work opportunities
