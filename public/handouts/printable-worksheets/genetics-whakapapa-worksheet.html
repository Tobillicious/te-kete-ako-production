🏛️ Whakataukī (Cultural Opening)

Māori: "Ko au te awa, ko te awa ko au"

English: "I am the river, the river is me"

Relevance: This whakataukī expresses whakapapa - our deep connection and genealogical links to the natural world. Genetics shows this connection at the molecular level, while whakapapa shows it at the cultural and spiritual level.

📚 Learning Objectives

  • Understand DNA structure and function in inheritance
  • Explore the concept of whakapapa (genealogy/connection)
  • Compare and contrast scientific and cultural perspectives on inheritance
  • Appreciate dual knowledge systems as complementary, not competing

🌿 Part 2: Whakapapa (Māori Knowledge System)

Understanding Whakapapa

Whakapapa (fah-kah-pah-pah) means genealogy, but it's much deeper than just family trees. It expresses:

  • Connection to ancestors (tīpuna)
  • Relationship to the natural world
  • Identity and belonging
  • Responsibilities to future generations
  • Interconnectedness of all things

👩‍🏫 Teacher Answer Key & Teaching Notes

Question 1 - Answers:

  • a) Deoxyribonucleic Acid
  • b) Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Cytosine (C), Guanine (G)
  • c) A gene is a section of DNA that codes for a specific protein/trait

Question 2 - Punnett Square:

Bb × Bb cross gives: 75% brown eyes (BB, Bb, Bb), 25% blue eyes (bb)

Question 5 - Sample Comparison Answers:

  • What whakapapa explains: Identity, cultural heritage, connections to land and ancestors, spiritual relationships
  • How genetics is studied: DNA analysis, Punnett squares, genetic sequencing, laboratory research
  • What whakapapa includes: Human ancestors, natural world, atua (gods), cultural identity, responsibilities
  • Purpose of genetics: Understanding biological inheritance, treating genetic diseases, ancestry research
  • Whakapapa timeframe: From atua through all generations to mokopuna (future generations)

Question 6 - Sample Answer:

Genetics tells us the biological mechanism of inheritance (DNA, genes), while whakapapa tells us the meaning and cultural significance of those connections. Together they give us both the "how" (science) and the "why it matters" (culture).

Question 7 - Key Points:

  • Scientific concerns: Lack of informed consent, potential misuse of genetic data, ethical violations
  • Cultural concerns: Violation of data sovereignty, lack of community control over taonga (genetic information), potential misrepresentation
  • Treaty implications: Article 2 - tino rangatiratanga over taonga (including genetic data)

Question 8 - Ethical Principles Should Include:

  • Full community consultation before research begins
  • Informed consent in culturally appropriate ways
  • Community ownership and control of data
  • Benefits shared with community
  • Cultural protocols respected throughout
  • Right to withdraw participation
  • Transparency about research purposes and funding

Cultural Safety Notes:

  • Sensitive Topic: Whakapapa is deeply personal. Never pressure students to share family information
  • Diverse Backgrounds: Acknowledge all students have whakapapa/genealogies regardless of culture
  • Adoption/Complex Families: Be sensitive to students with complex family situations
  • Privacy: Ensure students understand they control what they share

Assessment Rubric:

  • Excellence (NCEA): Sophisticated understanding of both knowledge systems, thoughtful integration, demonstrates cultural sensitivity
  • Merit: Good understanding of both systems, makes connections between them, shows cultural awareness
  • Achieved: Basic understanding of genetics and whakapapa concepts, demonstrates respect

Extension Activities:

  • Research historical cases of genetic research on indigenous peoples
  • Interview whānau about family whakapapa and compare to DNA ancestry tests
  • Investigate modern Māori-led genetic research projects
  • Create a multimedia presentation exploring your own cultural heritage
