🌿 Understanding Whakataukī

Whakataukī (fah-kah-tow-kee) are traditional Māori proverbs and sayings that carry deep wisdom passed down through generations. They often use metaphor from the natural world to teach life lessons.

Today's Focus: Using whakataukī as inspiration for creative writing!

📖 Part 1: Choose Your Whakataukī

Select ONE whakataukī from the options below (or choose another with teacher approval):

Option 1: He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tangata, he tangata, he tangata.

English: "What is the most important thing in the world? It is people, it is people, it is people."

Theme: Human connection, community, relationships

Option 2: Whāia te iti kahurangi ki te tūohu koe me he maunga teitei

English: "Seek the treasure you value most dearly; if you bow your head, let it be to a lofty mountain."

Theme: Pursuing goals with dignity, ambition, self-respect

Option 3: Kia whakatōmuri te haere whakamua

English: "I walk backwards into the future with my eyes fixed on my past."

Theme: Learning from history, ancestors guiding future

Option 4: Mā te huruhuru ka rere te manu

English: "With feathers, a bird can fly."

Theme: Preparation, having necessary skills/tools, support systems

Option 5: Me mahi tahi tātou mō te oranga o te katoa

English: "We should work together for the wellbeing of everyone."

Theme: Collaboration, collective wellbeing, unity

👩‍🏫 Teacher Resources & Rubric

Assessment Rubric (NCEA-Aligned):

Excellence (8-9):

  • Demonstrates sophisticated understanding of whakataukī meaning
  • Creative writing is compelling, well-structured, and shows strong voice
  • Integrates cultural understanding authentically and respectfully
  • Uses literary techniques effectively (imagery, metaphor, structure)
  • Reflections show deep cultural awareness and critical thinking

Merit (6-7):

  • Good understanding of whakataukī meaning
  • Creative writing is well-structured with clear narrative/theme
  • Shows cultural awareness and respect
  • Uses some literary techniques appropriately
  • Reflections show cultural understanding

Achieved (4-5):

  • Basic understanding of whakataukī
  • Creative writing attempts narrative/theme development
  • Demonstrates basic cultural respect
  • Limited use of literary techniques
  • Basic reflections

Cultural Safety Protocols:

  • Consult before teaching: Check with cultural advisors about appropriate whakataukī use
  • Pronunciation practice: Model correct pronunciation or use audio resources
  • Context matters: Teach the cultural context, not just the English translation
  • Respectful use: Emphasize these are not just "quotes" but traditional wisdom
  • Student choice: Allow students to choose whakataukī that resonates with them

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide sentence starters, story frameworks, graphic organizers for planning
  • Extension: Research whakataukī origins, create illustrated anthology, write in multiple genres
  • EAL: Allow writing in first language then translate, provide additional vocabulary support

Additional Whakataukī Options:

  • "Ehara taku toa i te toa takitahi, engari he toa takitini" - My strength is not individual, but collective
  • "Kia kaha, kia māia, kia manawanui" - Be strong, be brave, be steadfast
  • "Tū whitia te hopo, mairangatia te angitū" - Feel the fear and do it anyway
  • "Ko te pae tawhiti whāia kia tata" - Seek the distant horizons

Extension Activity Ideas:

  • Create an illustrated anthology of class writings
  • Perform as spoken word/poetry slam
  • Create digital storytelling with audio/visual
  • Share with local iwi or community group (with permission)
