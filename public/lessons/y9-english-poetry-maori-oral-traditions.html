🎯 WALT (We Are Learning To)

  • Analyze poetic devices in Māori oral traditions
  • Understand metaphor, repetition, and rhythm

📚 Lesson Flow

1️⃣ Introduction (15 minutes)

Hook: Play audio of traditional waiata (e.g., "E Pari Rā" or "Tūtira Mai Ngā Iwi"). "What makes this different from modern pop songs? What literary devices do you hear?"

Māori Oral Poetry Traditions:
  • Waiata - Songs (love songs, laments, war songs)
  • Karakia - Incantations/prayers (rhythmic, repetitive)
  • Whakataukī - Proverbs (metaphorical wisdom)
  • Pātere - Fast chants (storytelling, genealogy)
Key Features:

Māori poetry is ORAL (meant to be spoken/sung, not written). It uses nature metaphors, repetition for memory, and rhythm for performance. Every word carries layers of meaning (whakapapa connects words to ancestors and land).

2️⃣ Activity 1: Analyze a Waiata (20 minutes)

Text study: Examine "Tūtira Mai Ngā Iwi" (famous waiata by Wiremu Te Tau Huata)

"Tūtira Mai Ngā Iwi" (excerpt)
Tūtira mai ngā iwi
Tātou tātou e
Tūtira mai ngā iwi
Tātou tātou e

Whaia te maramatanga
Me te aroha - e ngā iwi!
Kia ko tapatahi
Kia kotahi rā
Tātou tātou e
Translation:

"Line up together, people / All of us, all of us / Line up together, people / All of us, all of us // Seek after knowledge / And love - O people! / Be one / Be really one / All of us"

Identify Poetic Devices (pairs):
  1. Repetition: "Tūtira mai" repeated (emphasis, memory aid)
  2. Call & response: "Tātou tātou e" (communal participation)
  3. Imperative verbs: Commands ("Whaia" = seek, "Kia" = be)
  4. Abstract concepts: Maramatanga (enlightenment), aroha (love), kotahi (unity)
  5. Collective pronouns: "Tātou" (us/we - inclusive)

Discussion: "What is the purpose of this waiata? How do the poetic devices support that purpose?"

3️⃣ Activity 2: Metaphor & Nature Imagery (15 minutes)

Explore common Māori metaphors: Nature is central to Māori poetry.

Metaphor Examples:
Nature Image Symbolic Meaning
🌊 Te moana (ocean) Vastness, journey, challenge
🌲 Rākau (tree) Ancestors, strength, rootedness
🦅 Manu (bird) Freedom, messages, soul
🌄 Maunga (mountain) Permanence, identity, challenge
💧 Wai (water) Life force, cleansing, flow

Task: Write 3 metaphorical lines using nature imagery to describe an emotion (e.g., "My anger is a volcano, ready to erupt").

4️⃣ Activity 3: Write Your Waiata (15 minutes)

Creative writing: Compose an original waiata-inspired poem.

Structure Options:
  • Option 1 (Traditional): 12-16 lines, repetitive structure, nature metaphors, call to action
  • Option 2 (Hybrid): Mix waiata elements with modern free verse
  • Option 3 (Bilingual): Alternate lines in English and te reo Māori
Themes to Explore:
  • Unity & togetherness (like "Tūtira Mai")
  • Connection to whenua (land)
  • Overcoming challenges
  • Honoring ancestors
  • Environmental kaitiakitanga
Requirements:
  • 12-16 lines minimum
  • At least 2 poetic devices (repetition, metaphor, alliteration, etc.)
  • At least 1 nature metaphor
  • Clear theme/message

5️⃣ Conclusion & Performance (5 minutes)

Oral performance: Volunteers read their waiata aloud (remember: oral tradition!). Class identifies poetic devices used.

Exit ticket: "What is one thing Māori oral poetry does that modern written poetry doesn't?"

💡 Teacher Notes

  • Cultural sensitivity: Encourage use of te reo but don't require it. Some students may feel uncomfortable using Māori language if not confident.
  • Extension: Set poems to music! Students can perform waiata with simple melodies or rhythm.
  • Differentiation: Struggling writers can start with adapting existing waiata structure. Advanced students can research specific waiata types (waiata aroha, waiata tangi, etc.).
  • Real-world connection: Discuss modern Māori musicians blending traditional and contemporary (e.g., Te Vaka, Alien Weaponry).
  • Follow-up: Create class anthology of student waiata. Perform at school assembly or record audio versions.
