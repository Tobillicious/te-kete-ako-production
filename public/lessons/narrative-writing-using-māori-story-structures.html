    He kōrero tuku iho – Stories passed down through generations

    Learning Objectives

    • Understand key elements of traditional Māori storytelling structures
    • Apply pūrākau (Māori narratives) techniques to modern creative writing
    • Create an original narrative using Māori storytelling frameworks
    • Reflect on how cultural narratives shape identity and worldview

    Cultural Context & Safety Notes

    Cultural Context: Traditional Māori narratives (pūrākau) often follow structures that differ from Western storytelling. Key elements include:

    • Whakapapa (genealogical connections)
    • Cyclical time rather than linear progression
    • Deep connection to whenua (land) and natural world
    • Teaching through metaphor rather than direct moral statements

    Safety Notes:

    • Some traditional stories may be tapu (sacred) to specific iwi
    • Always acknowledge the source of any traditional stories used
    • Encourage students to create original stories rather than retell sacred narratives
    • Consult with local kaumātua (elders) if unsure about appropriate content

    Lesson Activities

    1. Understanding Māori Story Structures (20 mins)

    Group Discussion: Compare a traditional Māori story (e.g., Māui and the Sun) with a Western fairy tale. What differences do you notice in:

    • Story structure?
    • Character development?
    • Relationship to nature?
    • Purpose of the story?

    2. Key Elements of Pūrākau (15 mins)

    Introduce the Pūrākau Framework:

    1. Kupu whakataki (Introduction) - Establishes connections to people/place
    2. Huarahi (Journey) - Often involves challenges or tests
    3. Taumata (Climax) - Moment of transformation
    4. Hokinga (Return) - Brings new knowledge back to community

    3. Writing Activity (30 mins)

    Create Your Own Story: Using the Pūrākau Framework, write a modern story that:

    • Connects to a place important to you
    • Includes a journey (physical or emotional)
    • Teaches a lesson without stating it directly
    • Uses natural elements symbolically

    Example starter: "Long before the highway was built, when the hills still whispered to each other at dawn..."

    Assessment Suggestions

    Use this checklist to assess student narratives:

    • Clear use of pūrākau structure (introduction, journey, climax, return)
    • Meaningful connection to place/environment
    • Effective use of symbolic elements
    • Originality and creativity in modern application
    • Cultural respect and appropriateness

    Alternative Assessment: Students record an oral telling of their story, focusing on delivery style inspired by traditional storytelling.

    Extension Activities

    • Research and compare storytelling structures from another indigenous culture
    • Create an illustrated version of the story using traditional Māori design elements
    • Develop the narrative into a short play incorporating waiata (songs) and movement
    • Write a reflection on how using this structure changed their approach to storytelling

    Adaptations for Diverse Learners

    For students needing support:

    • Provide a graphic organizer of the pūrākau structure
    • Offer sentence starters for each section
    • Allow oral storytelling instead of written

    For English Language Learners:

    • Use visual prompts for key concepts
    • Provide bilingual vocabulary lists
    • Encourage use of their first language in planning stages

    Teacher Implementation Guide

    Before the Lesson:

    • Familiarize yourself with examples of pūrākau
    • Prepare printed copies of a traditional Māori story for analysis
    • Create a shared digital document for collaborative brainstorming

    During the Lesson:

    1. Begin with a karakia (optional) to set purposeful atmosphere
    2. Use the comparison activity to highlight cultural differences in storytelling
    3. Explicitly teach the pūrākau framework using visual aids
    4. Circulate during writing time, prompting students to consider symbolic elements

    After the Lesson:

    • Create a class anthology of stories
    • Invite students to share stories in small groups
    • Display stories with accompanying artwork in classroom or school space
