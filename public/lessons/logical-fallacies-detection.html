    Detecting Logical Fallacies

    Logical fallacies are errors in reasoning that undermine the logic of an argument. Learning to spot them helps us evaluate information critically and make better decisions. In Te Ao MÄori, this connects to tika (truth/correctness) and pono (integrity/genuineness) - ensuring our reasoning is sound and our arguments are honest.

    ğŸ” Common Logical Fallacies

    1. Ad Hominem (Attack the Person)

    What it is: Attacking the person making an argument rather than addressing the argument itself.

    Example: "You can't trust Sarah's climate change research because she failed science in Year 9."

    Why it's flawed: A person's character or past mistakes don't determine whether their current argument is valid.

    How to counter: "Let's focus on the evidence and reasoning in the argument, not the person presenting it."

    2. Straw Man

    What it is: Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack.

    Example: Person A: "We should have more bike lanes." Person B: "Why do you want to ban all cars?"

    Why it's flawed: It attacks a distorted version of the argument, not the actual position.

    How to counter: "That's not what I said. My actual point is..."

    3. False Dichotomy (False Choice)

    What it is: Presenting only two options when more exist.

    Example: "Either you support this policy 100%, or you're against progress."

    Why it's flawed: Most issues have nuance and multiple possible approaches.

    How to counter: "Actually, there are several other options we should consider..."

    4. Appeal to Authority

    What it is: Claiming something is true just because an authority figure says so.

    Example: "A famous actor says this diet works, so it must be true."

    Why it's flawed: Authorities can be wrong, especially outside their expertise.

    How to counter: "What evidence supports this claim, regardless of who said it?"

    5. Slippery Slope

    What it is: Arguing that one small step will inevitably lead to extreme consequences.

    Example: "If we allow students to redo one test, soon they'll expect to redo everything and never learn responsibility."

    Why it's flawed: It assumes without evidence that one action will cause an unstoppable chain reaction.

    How to counter: "What evidence shows this will actually lead to those outcomes?"
