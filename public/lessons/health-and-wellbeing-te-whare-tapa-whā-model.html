    Introduction to Te Whare Tapa Whā

    Te Whare Tapa Whā is a holistic Māori health model developed by Sir Mason Durie in 1984. It compares health to the four walls of a wharenui (meeting house), where each wall represents a different dimension of wellbeing. All four dimensions are necessary for strength and stability.

    Just as all four walls are essential for a wharenui to stand, all four dimensions of health are necessary for our overall wellbeing. If one wall is weak or damaged, the entire structure is compromised.

    The Four Dimensions of Health

    Taha Wairua

    Spiritual Wellbeing

    Connection to faith, beliefs, values, and life purpose. Our relationship with the environment, heritage, and identity.

    Taha Hinengaro

    Mental & Emotional Wellbeing

    Thoughts, feelings, emotions, and how we communicate. Our capacity for thinking, reasoning, and managing emotions.

    Taha Tinana

    Physical Wellbeing

    Physical body, growth, development, and physical needs. How our body grows, feels, moves, and cares for itself.

    Taha Whānau

    Family & Social Wellbeing

    Relationships, family, friendships, and sense of belonging. Our connections to others and our community.

    Whenua - The Foundation

    Connection to land, roots, and place of belonging. Our relationship with the land, environment, and our ancestral connections.

    Strengthening Your Whare

    Here are some practical activities to strengthen each dimension of your wellbeing:

    Taha Wairua

    • Spend time in nature
    • Practice mindfulness or meditation
    • Explore your cultural heritage
    • Reflect on your values and purpose

    Taha Hinengaro

    • Keep a journal
    • Learn something new
    • Practice positive self-talk
    • Seek help when needed

    Taha Tinana

    • Exercise regularly
    • Eat nutritious foods
    • Get enough sleep
    • Attend regular health check-ups

    Taha Whānau

    • Spend quality time with family
    • Connect with friends
    • Participate in community events
    • Practice active listening

    ## Cultural Context and Safety Notes**Cultural Significance:**- Te Whare Tapa Whā was developed by respected Māori psychiatrist Sir Mason Durie- The model reflects Māori worldview that health is holistic and interconnected- The wharenui (meeting house) metaphor emphasizes community and interdependence**Cultural Safety Considerations:**- Present the model with respect for its Māori origins- Avoid oversimplifying or appropriating the concept- Acknowledge that interpretations may vary among different iwi and hapū- Encourage students to explore their own cultural perspectives on wellbeing- Ensure Māori students feel comfortable sharing or not sharing their perspectives## Learning Objectives (Aligned with NZ Curriculum)**Students will be able to:**1. Explain the four dimensions of Te Whare Tapa Whā and their significance in Māori health perspectives (Health & PE L4)2. Analyze their personal wellbeing using the Te Whare Tapa Whā framework (Health & PE L4)3. Develop strategies to strengthen each dimension of their wellbeing (Health & PE L5)4. Compare holistic and Western medical models of health (Social Sciences L4)5. Demonstrate respect for Māori knowledge and cultural perspectives (Social Sciences L4)## Teacher Implementation Guide**Lesson Duration:** 60
