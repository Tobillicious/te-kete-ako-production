YEAR 9 MATHEMATICS

Statistics for Social Justice: Analyzing Inequity

📊 Mathematics (Statistics) • ⏱️ 75 minutes • 👥 Year 9 • 🌿 Cultural: Data sovereignty

🏫 School Values Connection

⚖️ Justice
Using math to reveal inequity
🎯 Excellence
Advanced data analysis
💪 Integrity
Honest interpretation of data

🎯 WALT (We Are Learning To)

  • Calculate and interpret mean, median, and mode
  • Create and analyze graphs (bar, line, pie charts)
  • Identify trends and patterns in social data
  • Use statistics to understand social justice issues

✅ Success Criteria

  • I can calculate mean/median/mode accurately
  • I can create appropriate graphs for different data types
  • I can interpret what statistics reveal about inequity
  • I can suggest data-informed solutions

📚 Lesson Flow

1️⃣ Introduction (10 minutes)

Hook: Display this statistic: "Māori unemployment rate: 9.8% | Pākehā unemployment rate: 4.2% (Stats NZ 2023)"

Discussion: "What does this data tell us? What questions does it raise? Why might this gap exist?"

Data Sovereignty Concept:

Māori data sovereignty means Māori have the right to control data about Māori communities. Statistics can be used to help OR harm communities - it depends on who controls the data and how it's interpreted.

Key Question: "How can statistics help us understand and address social injustice?"

2️⃣ Activity 1: Calculate Statistics (20 minutes)

Dataset: University enrollment rates by ethnicity (NZ 2023)

Data: Students per 100 who attend university
Ethnicity Enrollment Rate
Asian 62%
Pākehā 48%
Māori 32%
Pasifika 28%
Tasks (pairs):
  1. Calculate mean: What is the average enrollment rate? (62 + 48 + 32 + 28) ÷ 4 = 42.5%
  2. Find median: Order the data, find the middle value(s). (32 + 48) ÷ 2 = 40%
  3. Identify range: Highest - Lowest. 62 - 28 = 34 percentage points
  4. Calculate gaps: What is the gap between Māori and Pākehā enrollment? 48 - 32 = 16 percentage points

Discussion: "What do these numbers tell us about educational equity in NZ?"

3️⃣ Activity 2: Create Visualizations (20 minutes)

Task: Create two different graphs to visualize the university enrollment data.

Graph Options:
📊 Bar Graph

Best for comparing categories (ethnicities). Clear, easy to read. Shows gaps visually.

📈 Line Graph

Best for showing change over time. Could show how enrollment has changed (2010-2023).

Requirements:
  • Title
  • Labeled axes (x-axis: ethnicity, y-axis: percentage)
  • Scale (0-70% in intervals of 10)
  • Accurate data points
  • Color coding for clarity

Extension: Create a second graph showing the same data as a pie chart. Which visualization is most effective? Why?

4️⃣ Activity 3: Data Interpretation & Solutions (20 minutes)

Critical analysis: What barriers might explain lower Māori/Pasifika enrollment?

Possible Barriers (Brainstorm):
  • Financial: Cost of fees, living expenses, student loans
  • Cultural: University cultures not welcoming to Māori
  • Educational: Lower NCEA achievement rates (compounding effect)
  • Geographic: Universities mostly in cities, far from rural Māori communities
  • Systemic: Historical educational inequity
Solutions Task (groups of 3-4):

Propose THREE data-informed solutions to increase Māori university enrollment. Each solution must address a specific barrier identified in the data.

Example: "Free fees for Māori students" (addresses financial barrier) + predict impact using statistics.

5️⃣ Conclusion & Presentation (5 minutes)

Group share: Each group presents ONE solution (30 seconds). Class votes on most impactful solution.

Exit ticket: "What is one new thing you learned about how statistics can reveal social justice issues?"

📊 Assessment

Formative Assessment:
  • Check calculations - Mean, median, range correctly calculated?
  • Review graphs - Accurate data representation? Proper labels?
  • Evaluate interpretations - Do solutions address barriers identified in data?
Success Indicators:
  • ✅ Accurate statistical calculations
  • ✅ Clear, properly labeled graphs
  • ✅ Thoughtful data interpretation
  • ✅ Evidence-based solution proposals

📦 Resources Needed

  • Printed datasets (university enrollment, employment, health, etc.)
  • Graph paper or digital graphing tools (Excel, Google Sheets)
  • Calculators
  • Colored pencils/markers for graphs

💡 Teacher Notes

  • Data sources: Use Stats NZ (stats.govt.nz) for accurate, up-to-date NZ data.
  • Sensitive topic: Some students may be personally affected by inequity statistics. Create safe space for discussion.
  • Extension: Advanced students can calculate standard deviation or create more complex visualizations (stacked bar charts, etc.).
  • Real-world connection: Discuss how government uses statistics to make policy decisions (e.g., targeted funding programs).
  • Follow-up: Next lesson could explore correlation vs causation, or misleading statistics.
