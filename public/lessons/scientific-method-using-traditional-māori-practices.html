    Teacher Note: Cultural Context

    This lesson validates mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge) as a legitimate scientific approach while maintaining respect for its sacred aspects. We'll explore how traditional Māori observation and experimentation methods align with Western scientific processes.

    Learning Objectives

    • Understand and apply the scientific method through a Māori worldview (te ao Māori)
    • Compare and contrast Western scientific methodology with traditional Māori knowledge systems
    • Develop skills in observation, hypothesis formation, and experimentation using both approaches
    • Recognize the validity of indigenous knowledge systems in scientific inquiry
    • Apply learning to a practical investigation about native plants

    Cultural Safety Notes

    • Some mātauranga Māori is tapu (sacred) - we will only use examples appropriate for classroom discussion
    • Always acknowledge the iwi (tribe) associated with specific knowledge
    • Encourage students to approach Māori knowledge with the same respect as Western science
    • Consult with your school's Māori community liaison if unsure about any content

    Lesson Content

    Whakataukī (Māori Proverb)

    "Titiro whakamuri, kōkiri whakamua"

    "Look to the past to inform the future" - This reflects the Māori approach to building knowledge through observation of natural patterns over generations.

    The Scientific Method - Two Worldviews

    Western Science Māori Knowledge System
    1. Observation 1. Titiro (Observation) - Careful watching over time
    2. Question 2. Pātai (Questioning) - Considering relationships
    3. Hypothesis 3. Whakapae (Proposition) - Based on ancestral knowledge
    4. Experiment 4. Whakamātautau (Testing) - Practical trials
    5. Analysis 5. Tātari (Analysis) - Considering all factors
    6. Conclusion 6. Whakatau (Conclusion) - Consensus decision

    Interactive Activity: Rongoā Māori (Traditional Medicine) Investigation

    Scenario: Māori traditionally used kawakawa leaves for medicinal purposes. Let's investigate their antibacterial properties using both scientific approaches.

    Western Scientific Approach:

    1. Observe that kawakawa seems to prevent infection when applied to wounds
    2. Question: Does kawakawa extract inhibit bacterial growth?
    3. Hypothesis: Kawakawa extract will prevent bacterial growth in petri dishes
    4. Experiment: Create agar plates with different concentrations of kawakawa extract
    5. Measure zones of inhibition after incubation

    Māori Knowledge Approach:

    1. Titiro: Observe how ancestors used kawakawa for generations
    2. Pātai: In what situations was it most effective?
    3. Whakapae: The plant is most effective when prepared in specific ways
    4. Whakamātautau: Test different preparation methods (fresh vs dried, different seasons)
    5. Tātari: Consider environmental factors like time of harvest

    Extension Activities

    For Advanced Learners:

    1. Research and compare traditional Polynesian navigation techniques with modern astronomy
    2. Investigate lunar calendars (maramataka) and their scientific basis
    3. Design an experiment that equally values quantitative data and qualitative mātauranga Māori
    4. Interview local kaumātua (elders) about traditional ecological knowledge (with proper protocols)
    5. Create a digital presentation comparing traditional and scientific explanations for a natural phenomenon

    Teacher Implementation Guide

    Before the Lesson:

    1. Consult with Māori community members about appropriate content
    2. Prepare kawakawa leaves (ensure proper harvesting protocols if collecting fresh)
    3. Set up lab equipment for antibacterial testing
    4. Create visual aids comparing the two methodologies

    Lesson Sequence (2-3 sessions):

    1. Introduction (30 min):
      • Discuss what science is - broaden understanding beyond Western paradigm
      • Introduce Māori scientific traditions through examples (navigation, medicine, agriculture)
      • Compare the two methodologies using the table provided
    2. Practical Investigation (60-90 min):
      • Divide class into groups - some use Western method, some use Māori approach
      • Guide through investigation protocols for both methods
      • Emphasize respectful handling of Māori knowledge
    3. Analysis and Reflection (60 min):
      • Groups share findings
      • Discuss strengths of each approach
      • Journal reflection: How can both knowledge systems work together?

    Resources Needed:

    • Kawakawa leaves (fresh or dried)
    • Basic microbiology equipment (petri dishes, agar, etc.)
    • Traditional preparation tools (stone mortar, flax baskets)
    • Digital devices for research
    • Printed comparison tables

    Conclusion

    This lesson demonstrates that science exists in many forms. By valuing both Western and Māori approaches, we gain a more complete understanding of our natural world. As the whakataukī says: "He kōtuinga mahi iti, he hua pai- rawa" - "From small collaborations come great fruits."
