    Reclaiming the Narrative: Ka Whawhai Tonu Mātou

    Unit: Walker - The Challenge to the Narrative | Lesson 1.5 FINALE

    Year 10 | Social Studies, History, English | 75 minutes

    🌟 All Three School Values: Whaimana + Whaiora + Whaiara

    This culminating lesson shows how Dr. Walker's life work embodies all three school values through his commitment to truth (Whaimana), wellbeing of Māori communities (Whaiora), and rising up to challenge injustice (Whaiara).

    Overview: This culminating lesson explores Dr. Ranginui Walker's landmark book Ka Whawhai Tonu Mātou: Struggle Without End (1990), which synthesized the themes from this unit into a comprehensive Māori-centered history that challenged New Zealand's dominant narrative.

    🛡️ Cultural Safety Considerations

    • Approach Māori content with respect and openness
    • Recognize students' diverse cultural backgrounds and experiences
    • Create space for Māori students to share their perspectives
    • Consult with whānau and local iwi for culturally sensitive topics

    🎯 WALT: We are learning to understand how Dr. Ranginui Walker reclaimed Māori history through Ka Whawhai Tonu Mātou

    ✅ Success Criteria:

    • Explain why Ka Whawhai Tonu Mātou was groundbreaking
    • Describe how the book challenged the dominant narrative
    • Connect unit themes (migration, activism, Treaty justice) to the book's message
    • Reflect on why it matters who tells a nation's story
    • Articulate how Dr. Walker exemplifies ALL THREE school values

    📚 Key Vocabulary

    • Ka Whawhai Tonu Mātou: "We will struggle on" or "Struggle without end"
    • Counter-narrative: Story challenging the dominant narrative
    • Historiography: Study of how history is written and interpreted
    • Mātauranga Māori: Māori knowledge and worldview

    🔄 Unit Journey Review

    2. Lesson 1.2: Great Migration (Whaiora)

    ⭐ Dominant vs Counter-Narrative

    Dominant Narrative Walker's Counter-Narrative
    Māori as passive Māori as active agents resisting
    Colonization as progress Colonization as dispossession
    Peaceful settlement Ongoing struggle for justice

    📊 Assessment - Unit Synthesis

    Choose ONE option:

    1. Counter-Narrative Project: Choose a historical event and present both the dominant narrative and alternative perspective (following Walker's model)
    2. Essay: "How did Dr. Ranginui Walker challenge the dominant narrative? Evaluate his contribution" (use evidence from all 5 lessons)
    3. Visual Timeline: "The Struggle Continues" - key events from 1840 to present showing ongoing quest for Treaty justice

    🧑‍🏫 Final Reflection

    Exit Ticket: "What historical story from your own whānau, community, or culture needs to be told?"

    This empowers students to see themselves as storytellers whose voices and experiences are valuable.
