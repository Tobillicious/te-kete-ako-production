YEAR 10 SOCIAL STUDIES - NCEA LEVEL 1

Māori Protest Movements: 1970s to Present

✊ Social Studies (NZ History & Activism) • ⏱️ 90 minutes • 👥 Year 10 (NCEA Level 1) • 🌿 Cultural: Tino rangatiratanga movements

🎓 NCEA Achievement Standards

AS 91037 (1.1): Demonstrate understanding of perspectives on an historical event of significance to New Zealanders

This lesson addresses multiple perspectives on Māori activism (protesters, government, media, iwi, public)

🎯 WALT

  • Analyze major Māori protest movements (1970s-present)
  • Evaluate tactics, goals, and outcomes
  • Examine multiple perspectives on activism
  • Connect protests to Treaty breaches and land rights
  • Assess long-term impacts on policy and society

✅ Success Criteria (NCEA)

  • Achieved: Describe 2 protest movements with detail
  • Merit: Explain perspectives + analyze tactics
  • Excellence: Evaluate outcomes + long-term impact comprehensively

📚 Lesson Flow

1️⃣ Introduction: The Māori Renaissance (15 minutes)

Hook: Show images: 1975 Waitangi Day protest, Bastion Point occupation, 1981 Springbok Tour, 2004 Foreshore & Seabed hikoi. "What do you notice? What were they fighting for?"

Context: Why the 1970s?

By 1970s, Māori had lost 95% of land, te reo nearly extinct, massive urbanization (Māori moved to cities for work), high poverty/poor health. Young Māori generation said: "ENOUGH!" The Māori Renaissance began - protests, activism, cultural revival.

2️⃣ Major Protest Movements - Case Studies (40 minutes)

Jigsaw Groups: Each group becomes experts on ONE protest, then teaches others.

GROUP 1: 1975 Land March (Te Roopu o Te Matakite)

Date: September-October 1975

Leader: Whina Cooper (Te Whaea o te Motu - Mother of the Nation)

Action: Marched from Te Hapua (Far North) to Parliament (1000km, 30 days)

Slogan: "Not one more acre of Māori land"

Goal: Stop ongoing land alienation, raise awareness

Outcome: 60,000 people joined by Wellington. Massive public awareness. Led to Waitangi Tribunal expansion (1985).

Perspectives: Māori: powerful, peaceful, dignified. Government: politically challenging. Media: initially dismissive, then supportive.

GROUP 2: 1977-78 Bastion Point Occupation

Who: Ngāti Whātua ōrākei

Issue: Crown sold Ngāti Whātua land for housing development (broken Treaty promises)

Action: 506-day occupation of Bastion Point (Auckland)

Tactics: Built village, vegetable gardens, lived on ancestral land

End: Police forcibly removed 222 protesters (violent images shocked nation)

Outcome: Government eventually returned land to Ngāti Whātua (1991). Protest became symbol of resistance.

GROUP 3: 1981 Springbok Tour Protests

Issue: South African rugby team touring NZ during apartheid

Māori Position: Strongly opposed (apartheid = racial oppression like colonization)

Actions: Mass protests (Patu Squad), pitch invasions, nationwide demonstrations

Violence: Police vs protesters, NZ divided, "the country tore itself apart"

Impact: NZ ended sporting contact with apartheid South Africa. Protest success energized Māori activism.

GROUP 4: 2004 Foreshore & Seabed Hikoi

Issue: Government passed law taking Māori customary rights to foreshore & seabed

Response: Largest hikoi (march) in NZ history - 15,000+ to Parliament

Leaders: Tariana Turia, Hone Harawira, iwi leaders nationwide

Tactics: Peaceful march, petitions, political party formed (Māori Party)

Outcome: Law partially repealed (2011). Māori Party gained political power. Demonstrated ongoing activism.

Task (20 min): Each group researches their protest, creates timeline, identifies perspectives (Māori activists, government, media, public, iwi affected).

Presentation (20 min): Each group teaches class (5 min each) using timeline + images.

3️⃣ Comparative Analysis (20 minutes)

Class Discussion: Compare and contrast the 4 movements.

Analysis Questions:
  • Tactics: Peaceful vs confrontational? Which worked better?
  • Goals: All sought same thing (land rights, Treaty honor)?
  • Outcomes: Which protests achieved their goals? Why?
  • Government Response: How did authorities react? Changed over time?
  • Public Opinion: NZ society's view of Māori activism - evolved?
  • Legacy: Did these protests create lasting change?

4️⃣ Modern Activism & Ongoing Issues (10 minutes)

Current Māori Activism (2020s):
  • Ihumātao: 2019 land dispute (SOUL prevented development)
  • Three Waters protests: 2021-2023 (co-governance debate)
  • Treaty Principles Bill: 2024 hikoi to Parliament (175,000+!)
  • Climate activism: Māori youth leading climate justice
  • Language revitalization: Te Wiki o te Reo Māori campaigns

Discussion: "Are today's protests different from 1970s? What's changed? What hasn't?"

5️⃣ Assessment Task (5 minutes intro)

NCEA-Style Assessment (Homework/Next Lesson):

Choose ONE Māori protest movement (1975 Land March, Bastion Point, Springbok Tour, or Foreshore Hikoi).

Write 400-600 words explaining:

  • Achieved: What happened? When? Who? Why? (describe with details)
  • Merit: Multiple perspectives (Māori activists, government, media). Analyze tactics used.
  • Excellence: Evaluate long-term impacts. Did it achieve goals? How did it change NZ?

🧠 Critical Analysis Questions

  1. Effectiveness: When is protest justified? When does it work vs fail?
  2. Violence vs Peace: Ethical considerations in protest tactics?
  3. Government Response: Should government negotiate with protesters or enforce law?
  4. Media Role: How did media portrayal affect outcomes?
  5. Your Position: Would YOU have joined these protests? Why/why not?

📦 Resources & Teacher Notes

Resources Needed:
  • Historical photos/videos of protests (Archives NZ, NZ On Screen)
  • Primary sources: newspaper clippings, protest posters, speeches
  • Timeline poster materials
  • NCEA exemplar responses (for assessment guidance)
⚠️ Teacher Notes - VERY SENSITIVE TOPIC:
  • Emotional impact: Māori students may have whānau who participated. Create safe space.
  • Multiple perspectives required: NCEA demands balanced analysis, not just Māori or government view.
  • Current events: Protests still happening! Students may have strong opinions. Manage respectfully.
  • Violence discussion: Some protests involved confrontation. Discuss ethics thoughtfully.
  • Extension: Invite kaumātua or activists to share first-hand experiences if possible.
  • Assessment: Use NCEA marking schedule. Provide exemplars showing Achieved/Merit/Excellence.
