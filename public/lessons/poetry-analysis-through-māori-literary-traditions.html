    Introduction

    Welcome to our exploration of Māori poetry! In this lesson, we'll journey through both traditional and contemporary Māori poetic forms, learning to appreciate the unique cultural perspectives and literary techniques that make this poetry so powerful.

    Cultural Note: Māori poetry is deeply connected to whakapapa (genealogy), whenua (land), and spiritual beliefs. When studying these works, we approach them with manaakitanga (respect and care).

    Learning Objectives

    By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

    • Identify key characteristics of traditional and contemporary Māori poetry
    • Analyze poetic devices within cultural context
    • Understand the significance of whakapapa, whenua, and cultural identity in Māori poetry
    • Compare traditional and contemporary Māori poetic forms
    • Create original poetry inspired by Māori literary traditions

    NZ Curriculum Alignment: English Level 4-5: Listening, Reading, and Viewing; Speaking, Writing, and Presenting

    Cultural Context and Safety Notes

    Cultural Context

    Māori poetry has a rich oral tradition dating back centuries. Traditional forms include:

    • Waiata (songs) - used for storytelling, mourning, celebration
    • Karakia (prayers/invocations) - spiritual poetry
    • Whakataukī (proverbs) - concise wisdom poetry
    • Haka (ceremonial dance) - powerful performance poetry

    Contemporary Māori poets like Hone Tuwhare, Apirana Taylor, and Hinemoana Baker have adapted these traditions into modern literary forms while maintaining cultural connections.

    Safety Considerations

    • Some traditional waiata tangi (laments) address sensitive topics like grief and loss
    • Certain karakia may have spiritual significance - we'll focus on their literary aspects
    • Always acknowledge the source and cultural context of poems
    • Respect that some knowledge may be tapu (sacred/restricted)
    • Create a supportive environment for sharing personal responses

    Traditional Māori Poetry

    Whakataukī (Proverbs)

    Whakataukī are concise poetic statements that convey wisdom, values, and cultural knowledge.

    "He kōtuku rerenga tahi"

    (The white heron flies once)

    Meaning: A rare event or person of distinction

    Analyze This Whakataukī

    "Ka pū te ruha, ka hao te rangatahi"

    (As the old net withers, the new net goes fishing)

    Discussion Questions:

    This speaks to the natural cycle of generations - as the old passes, the young take their place. It acknowledges both the value of elders and the potential of youth.

    Metaphor (comparing generations to fishing nets), parallelism (balanced structure), and imagery (visualizing the old and new nets).

    Waiata (Songs)

    Traditional waiata served many purposes - from recording history to expressing emotion.

    Excerpt from a traditional waiata aroha (love song):

    E hine e, hoki mai ra
    Ka ea te mate i au e hi nei
    Te aroha e, tau here mai

    (Oh girl, return to me
    So my longing may be satisfied
    Love, bind yourself to me)

    Activity: Traditional Poetry Analysis

    Choose one traditional Māori poetic form (whakataukī, waiata, or karakia) and analyze:

    1. The purpose or message
    2. Key poetic devices (metaphor, repetition, imagery)
    3. Cultural values expressed
    4. Connection to whakapapa or whenua

    Contemporary Māori Poetry

    Modern Māori poets blend traditional elements with contemporary forms and concerns.

    Hone Tuwhare - "Rain"

    I can hear you
    making small holes
    in the silence
    rain

    If I were deaf
    the pores of my skin
    would open to you
    and shut

    And I
    should know you
    by the lick of you
    if I were blind

    Compare and Contrast

    How does Tuwhare's poem connect to Māori traditions while using modern poetic techniques?

    Key Poetic Devices in Māori Poetry

    Device Description Example
    Whakapapa Genealogical connections that establish identity and relationships Referencing ancestral lines in formal introductions
    Kōrero pūrākau Storytelling that preserves history and cultural knowledge Narratives explaining natural phenomena or tribal history
    Nature Metaphor Using natural elements to represent human experiences Comparing emotions to weather patterns or landscapes
    Repetition Repeating words or phrases for emphasis and memorability Repeated lines in waiata for emotional impact
    Parallelism Balanced structures that create rhythm and connection Paired phrases in whakataukī
