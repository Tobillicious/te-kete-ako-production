📚 Lesson Flow

1️⃣ Introduction (10 minutes)

Hook: Read aloud a short pūrākau (e.g., "Māui and the Sun" or "How Māui Fished Up the North Island"). "What lesson does this story teach?"

What is Pūrākau?

Pūrākau are traditional Māori narratives passed down through generations. They're not just "stories" - they carry deep wisdom (mātauranga), explain natural phenomena, and teach important life lessons. Every pūrākau has a purpose: to educate, to warn, to inspire, or to explain the world.

Key Features of Pūrākau:
  • Characters: Often gods, ancestors, or animals with human qualities
  • Setting: Te taiao (natural world) - mountains, rivers, sky
  • Problem: A challenge or conflict to overcome
  • Resolution: Problem is solved, often with cleverness or cooperation
  • Teaching: A lesson or value (e.g., respect, courage, wisdom)

2️⃣ Activity 1: Analyzing Pūrākau Structure (15 minutes)

Class discussion: Break down "Māui and the Sun" using story mountain structure.

Example: Māui and the Sun
1. BEGINNING (Setting & Characters):

Māui and his brothers live in Aotearoa. The sun races across the sky too fast - days are short, food can't cook, plants can't grow.

2. PROBLEM (Conflict):

The sun is lazy and selfish. People suffer because of it. Something must be done!

3. ACTION (Rising tension):

Māui convinces his brothers to help. They weave ropes from harakeke (flax), journey to the sun's sleeping place, and set a trap. At dawn, they catch the sun in their ropes!

4. CLIMAX (Turning point):

Māui beats the sun with his grandmother's enchanted jawbone until it agrees to slow down and travel across the sky more slowly.

5. RESOLUTION & TEACHING:

The sun now moves slowly, giving long days for work, cooking, and growing food. Lesson: Courage and cooperation can solve even impossible problems.

Group task: In pairs, identify these 5 parts in another pūrākau (teacher provides 2-3 options).

3️⃣ Activity 2: Planning Your Pūrākau (20 minutes)

Individual planning: Create your own original pūrākau using the story mountain template.

Story Planning Template:
  1. Choose your characters: Who is your protagonist? (Could be an ancestor, animal, or yourself)
  2. Choose your setting: Where does the story take place? (Mountain, forest, ocean, river?)
  3. Create a problem: What challenge do your characters face?
  4. Plan the action: How do they try to solve the problem? (3-4 steps)
  5. Decide the resolution: How is the problem solved?
  6. Choose your teaching: What lesson does your story teach?
Story Prompts (if you're stuck):
  • How the kiwi lost its wings (explain a natural fact)
  • The taniwha who protected the river (environmental message)
  • Two friends who learned to work together (teamwork value)
  • The ancestor who discovered fire/food/shelter (origin story)

4️⃣ Activity 3: Write Your Pūrākau (15 minutes)

Writing time: Transform your plan into a full narrative (300+ words).

Writing Checklist:
  • ✅ Start with "Long ago..." or "In the time of our ancestors..."
  • ✅ Describe your setting using sensory details (what you see, hear, smell)
  • ✅ Show character emotions (scared, brave, determined, etc.)
  • ✅ Include dialogue (what characters say to each other)
  • ✅ End with a clear resolution and teaching
Descriptive Language Examples:
  • Instead of "big mountain" → "towering maunga that scraped the clouds"
  • Instead of "dark forest" → "dense ngahere where sunlight barely touched the earth"
  • Instead of "fast river" → "rushing awa that roared like thunder"

5️⃣ Conclusion & Sharing (5 minutes)

Pair-share: Read your pūrākau to a partner. Partner identifies the teaching/lesson in your story.

Exit ticket: "What is one thing that makes pūrākau different from other stories?"

📊 Assessment

Narrative Writing Rubric:
  • ✅ Clear beginning, middle, and end
  • ✅ Characters with motivations
  • ✅ Problem and resolution
  • ✅ Descriptive language (2+ techniques)
  • ✅ Teaching/lesson included
  • ✅ 300+ words

📦 Resources Needed

  • Printed pūrākau (Māui stories, etc.) - simplified versions
  • Story mountain template (handout)
  • Word banks for descriptive language

💡 Teacher Notes

  • Cultural note: Pūrākau are taonga (treasures). Encourage respect when adapting traditional structures.
  • Differentiation: Struggling writers can work from provided story starters. Advanced writers can include multiple characters or parallel storylines.
  • Extension: Students can illustrate their pūrākau or record audio versions for class podcast.
  • Follow-up: Create class pūrākau collection. Share with younger students or perform at assembly.
