    Evidence Evaluation Frameworks

    In our digital age, we're constantly bombarded with information. Learning to evaluate evidence systematically helps us distinguish reliable information from misinformation. In Te Ao MÄori, this connects to mÄtauranga (validated knowledge) and pÅ«kenga (skill/expertise) - recognizing that not all sources carry the same weight or wisdom.

    ğŸ“Š The CRAAP Test Framework

    Use CRAAP (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) to evaluate any source of information:

    ğŸ’° C - Currency (Timeliness)

    Questions to Ask:

    • When was this information published or last updated?
    • Is the information current enough for your topic?
    • Have newer findings replaced this information?
    • Are the links or references still functional?

    Example: A 2010 article about social media trends might not reflect current platforms like TikTok or BeReal.

    ğŸ¯ R - Relevance (Importance)

    Questions to Ask:

    • Does this information relate to your topic or answer your question?
    • Is it at an appropriate level (not too basic, not too advanced)?
    • Would you be comfortable citing this source?
    • Does it cover the geographic/cultural context you need?

    Example: A US-focused study on education policy may not be relevant to Aotearoa NZ's bicultural context.

    ğŸ‘¤ A - Authority (Source)

    Questions to Ask:

    • Who is the author/publisher/source/sponsor?
    • What are their credentials or organizational affiliations?
    • Are they qualified to write on this topic?
    • Is contact information provided?

    Example: A climate article by climate scientists carries more weight than one by a celebrity influencer.

    âœ“ A - Accuracy (Reliability)

    Questions to Ask:

    • Where does the information come from?
    • Is the information supported by evidence?
    • Can you verify the information in other sources?
    • Does the language or tone seem unbiased and professional?
    • Are there spelling, grammar, or typographical errors?

    Example: Information with citations and links to peer-reviewed research is more reliable than unsupported claims.

    ğŸ­ P - Purpose (Reason)

    Questions to Ask:

    • What is the purpose of this information? (inform, teach, sell, entertain, persuade?)
    • Do the authors/sponsors make their intentions clear?
    • Is the information fact, opinion, or propaganda?
    • Are there political, ideological, cultural, or commercial biases?

    Example: A product review on a company website vs. an independent consumer report - consider who benefits.

    ğŸ¯ Practice Activity: CRAAP Test in Action

    Scenario: You're researching climate change for a school project. Evaluate these three sources using the CRAAP test:

    Source A: Blog post titled "Climate Change is a Hoax"

    • Published last year on personalopinions.com
    • Author: "Steve123" (no credentials listed)
    • No citations or references
    • Full of emotional language and exclamation marks!!!

    Source B: NIWA (National Institute of Water & Atmospheric Research) Report

    • Published 2 months ago on official NIWA website
    • Authors: PhD climate scientists with 20+ years experience
    • Extensive citations to peer-reviewed research
    • Professional tone, clearly states methodology

    Source C: Social Media Post

    • Posted yesterday by @climatefacts2025
    • Shares alarming statistics with no source
    • Links to a petition to "save the planet"
    • 1M followers but account created 2 weeks ago

    Task: Score each source on Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, and Purpose (1-5 scale). Which would you use for your project? Why?
