YEAR 9 SOCIAL STUDIES

Impact of Colonization on Māori Communities

🏛️ Social Studies (NZ History) • ⏱️ 75 minutes • 👥 Year 9 • 🌿 Cultural: Historical impacts

🏫 School Values Connection

💪 Integrity
Facing difficult historical truths
🌿 Respect
Honoring those who suffered
⚖️ Justice
Understanding need for redress

🎯 WALT (We Are Learning To)

  • Identify impacts of colonization on Māori (land, language, culture, health)
  • Understand how Treaty breaches harmed Māori communities
  • Analyze statistical data showing colonial impacts
  • Connect historical events to present-day inequities

✅ Success Criteria

  • I can name 3 specific impacts of colonization
  • I can explain how land loss affected Māori
  • I can interpret data about language loss/revival
  • I can suggest ways to address colonial legacies

📚 Lesson Flow

1️⃣ Introduction (15 minutes)

Hook: "In 1840, Māori owned 66 million acres of land in NZ. By 1975, Māori owned less than 3 million acres. What happened?"

Define Colonization:

Colonization = when one country/people takes control of another's land and resources. In NZ, British colonization began after Te Tiriti (1840). Despite Treaty promises, Māori experienced massive losses.

2️⃣ Activity 1: Four Major Impacts (25 minutes)

Jigsaw groups: Each group researches ONE impact area (15 min), then teaches others (10 min).

Impact Areas:
Group 1: LAND LOSS
  • Confiscations after NZ Wars (Waikato, Taranaki)
  • Unfair land sales & Crown pre-emption
  • Native Land Court broke up communal ownership
  • Result: 95% land loss by 1975
Group 2: LANGUAGE LOSS
  • 1867: English made official language of schools
  • Children punished for speaking te reo
  • By 1980s: <2% Māori children fluent
  • Revival: Kōhanga reo (1982), Māori Language Act (1987)
Group 3: HEALTH IMPACTS
  • Introduced diseases (measles, flu) killed thousands
  • Poverty from land loss → poor health
  • Cultural trauma from assimilation policies
  • Today: Still gaps in health outcomes
Group 4: CULTURAL SUPPRESSION
  • Tohunga Suppression Act (1907) banned traditional healers
  • Assimilation policies ("integration" = forced to be Pākehā)
  • Loss of traditional knowledge & practices
  • Cultural revival movements (1970s+)

3️⃣ Activity 2: Data Analysis (15 minutes)

Examine statistics: What do numbers reveal about colonial impact?

Statistical Evidence:
Impact Area Data
Land ownership 1840: 100% → 1975: <5%
Te reo speakers 1900: ~90% → 1980: <2% (children)
Population 1840: ~100,000 → 1896: 42,000 (lowest)
Life expectancy (1900) Māori: 25 years | Pākehā: 58 years

Discussion: "What do these numbers tell us about the impact of colonization? Which statistic shocks you most?"

4️⃣ Activity 3: Solutions & Redress (15 minutes)

Group task: How can/should NZ address colonial harms?

Current Solutions (Examples):
  • Treaty settlements: Returning land, money, apologies to iwi
  • Te reo revival: Kōhanga reo, Māori Language Week, bilingual schools
  • Waitangi Tribunal: Investigates Treaty breaches
  • Māori health initiatives: Culturally responsive healthcare
  • Education: Teaching true NZ history in schools

Task: In groups, propose ONE additional solution. How would it work? Who would benefit? What would it cost?

5️⃣ Conclusion & Reflection (5 minutes)

Class share: Each group presents their solution (1 minute).

Exit ticket: "What is one thing YOU can do to support decolonization in your own life?"

📊 Assessment

Success Indicators:
  • ✅ Identifies multiple impacts with examples
  • ✅ Interprets statistical data accurately
  • ✅ Connects historical events to present
  • ✅ Proposes thoughtful, realistic solutions

📦 Resources Needed

  • Historical data sheets (land, language, health statistics)
  • Map showing land confiscations
  • Timeline poster (1840-present)
  • Research materials (textbooks, approved websites)

💡 Teacher Notes

  • VERY sensitive topic: May affect Māori students personally. Create safe, respectful space. Have support plan ready.
  • Balance: Acknowledge harm WITHOUT creating guilt/shame. Focus on understanding and solutions.
  • Extension: Research specific iwi experiences (e.g., Ngāi Tahu, Waikato-Tainui settlements).
  • Real-world: Invite kaumātua or iwi historian to share oral histories if possible.
  • Follow-up: Next lessons can explore specific events (NZ Wars, Tohunga Suppression Act, etc.).
