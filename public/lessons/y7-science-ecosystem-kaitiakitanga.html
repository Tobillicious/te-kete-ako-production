YEAR 7 SCIENCE

Ecosystems & Kaitiakitanga (Guardianship)

🔬 Science (Living World) • ⏱️ 60 minutes • 👥 Year 7 • 🌿 Cultural: Kaitiakitanga

🏫 School Values Connection

🌿 Kaitiakitanga
Environmental guardianship in action
🎯 Excellence
Scientific observation skills
🤝 Community
Collective responsibility for te taiao

🎯 WALT (We Are Learning To)

  • Explain what an ecosystem is and identify its components
  • Describe relationships between organisms (food chains)
  • Understand kaitiakitanga as environmental guardianship
  • Apply mātauranga Māori to ecosystem understanding

✅ Success Criteria

  • I can name 5 living and 3 non-living parts of an ecosystem
  • I can draw a simple food chain with 4+ organisms
  • I can explain kaitiakitanga in my own words
  • I can suggest 2 ways to practice kaitiakitanga locally

📚 Lesson Flow

1️⃣ Introduction (10 minutes)

Hook: Show a 2-minute video of a local ngahere (forest) or coastline. Ask: "What living things did you see? What non-living things? How do they all connect?"

Introduce Kaitiakitanga:

"In Māori worldview, we are not separate from nature - we ARE nature. Kaitiakitanga means being guardians of the environment. It's our responsibility to protect and care for ecosystems, not just use them."

Key Question: "What would happen if we removed one animal from this environment?"

2️⃣ Activity 1: Ecosystem Components (15 minutes)

Class brainstorm: List everything you'd find in a New Zealand forest ecosystem (ngahere).

Sort into two categories:
🌱 Biotic (Living)
  • Kererū (wood pigeon)
  • Pōhutukawa trees
  • Wētā insects
  • Fungi, ferns, moss
  • Bacteria
🪨 Abiotic (Non-living)
  • Sunlight
  • Water (wai)
  • Soil (oneone)
  • Air, rocks
  • Temperature

Definition: An ecosystem is all the living organisms (biotic) and non-living things (abiotic) in an area, and how they interact.

3️⃣ Activity 2: Food Chains (15 minutes)

Teacher models: Draw a simple food chain showing energy flow.

Example: NZ Forest Food Chain
☀️ Sun
(Energy source)
→
🌿 Fern
(Producer)
→
🦗 Wētā
(Consumer 1)
→
🦎 Gecko
(Consumer 2)
→
🦅 Kārearea
(Top predator)

Students create: Draw your own NZ food chain with at least 4 organisms. Label each as producer/consumer/decomposer.

4️⃣ Activity 3: Kaitiakitanga in Action (15 minutes)

Group discussion: How can we practice kaitiakitanga to protect local ecosystems?

Examples of Kaitiakitanga:
  • Trap predators (rats, stoats) to protect native birds
  • Plant native trees to restore habitats
  • Remove rubbish from beaches and rivers
  • Use rāhui (temporary bans) to let ecosystems recover
  • Learn from kaumātua about traditional practices
  • Reduce waste and pollution

Task: In groups of 3-4, choose ONE local ecosystem problem (e.g., pollution in local stream, pest animals, litter). Design a kaitiakitanga action plan to address it.

5️⃣ Conclusion & Reflection (5 minutes)

Class share: Each group presents their kaitiakitanga action plan (1 minute each).

Exit ticket: "What is one thing YOU can do this week to be a kaitiaki of the environment?"

📊 Assessment

Formative Assessment:
  • Check food chain diagrams - Correct flow of energy? Correct labels?
  • Listen to group discussions - Understanding of kaitiakitanga?
  • Review action plans - Practical and realistic solutions?
Success Indicators:
  • ✅ Students distinguish biotic from abiotic
  • ✅ Students correctly sequence food chains
  • ✅ Students connect science to cultural responsibility
  • ✅ Students propose actionable environmental solutions

📦 Resources Needed

  • Video of local NZ ecosystem (YouTube or DoC website)
  • Whiteboard for food chain diagrams
  • Paper and colored markers for group work
  • Optional: Photos of native NZ species

💡 Teacher Notes

  • Cultural connection: Invite local iwi member or kaumātua to share traditional kaitiakitanga practices if possible.
  • Extension: Next lesson can explore food webs (more complex than chains) and human impact on ecosystems.
  • Differentiation: Struggling students can work with 3-organism food chains. Advanced students can research specific NZ conservation projects.
  • Real-world link: Connect to local initiatives (e.g., Predator Free 2050, community planting days).
