Traditional Waka Knowledge:

Balanced vs Unbalanced Forces:
  • If THRUST > DRAG ‚Üí waka accelerates (speeds up)
  • If THRUST = DRAG ‚Üí waka maintains constant speed
  • If THRUST < DRAG ‚Üí waka decelerates (slows down)
  • If BUOYANCY = WEIGHT ‚Üí waka floats at surface

Students draw: Sketch a waka and label all 4 forces with arrows (correct direction and relative size).

3Ô∏è‚É£ Activity 2: Drag Investigation (20 minutes)

Hands-on experiment: Test how shape affects water resistance (drag).

Materials:
  • Large tub/sink filled with water
  • Plasticine/clay (3 equal pieces per group)
  • Timer
Method (groups of 3-4):
  1. Shape piece 1 into a flat block
  2. Shape piece 2 into a sphere/ball
  3. Shape piece 3 into a streamlined shape (waka-like)
  4. Drop each from same height, time how long it takes to sink
  5. Record results in table

Discussion: "Which shape sank fastest? Why? How does this relate to waka hull design?"

4Ô∏è‚É£ Activity 3: Waka Design Challenge (10 minutes)

Design task: Sketch a waka hull that minimizes drag while maintaining stability.

Traditional Waka Design Features:
  • Pointed bow (front) - Cuts through water, reduces drag
  • Narrow hull - Less surface area = less resistance
  • Smooth bottom - Reduces friction with water
  • Ama (outrigger) - Adds stability without widening main hull
  • Shallow draft - Less underwater volume = less drag

Task: Draw side view & top view of your waka design. Label features and explain how each reduces drag.
