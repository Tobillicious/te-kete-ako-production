Digital Storytelling with Pūrākau Framework

Year Levels: Years 8-10 | Subject: English

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand and apply traditional Māori storytelling structures (pūrākau) to digital narratives
  • Create a digital story that demonstrates understanding of Māori storytelling conventions
  • Use appropriate digital tools to enhance storytelling while maintaining cultural integrity
  • Reflect on the relationship between traditional and contemporary storytelling forms

Cultural Context: Pūrākau - Māori Storytelling Traditions

Pūrākau are traditional Māori narratives that carry knowledge, values, and history. They often follow specific structures and contain symbolic elements that connect to the natural world.

Tikanga Considerations

  • Respect for sources: Acknowledge the iwi or community where stories originate
  • Appropriate use: Some stories may have tapu (sacred) aspects - check with local kaumātua if unsure
  • Authentic representation: Avoid oversimplifying or altering core meanings of traditional stories
  • Collaboration: Consider working with Māori students or community members to ensure cultural accuracy

Lesson Activities

1. Understanding Pūrākau Structure

Traditional Māori stories often follow this pattern:

  1. Whakapapa (Origins): Establishing connections to land and ancestors
  2. Hīkoi (Journey): The main narrative movement
  3. Tūāpapa (Foundation): Key teachings or lessons
  4. Hokinga (Return): Resolution and connection back to the beginning

Activity: Analyze a traditional pūrākau (like Māui stories) and identify these elements.

2. Digital Story Planning

Create your own digital story using the pūrākau framework:

Traditional Elements

  • Connection to nature/environment
  • Symbolic characters or events
  • Teaching or lesson
  • Cyclical structure

Digital Enhancements

  • Images/symbols
  • Sound effects/music
  • Text narration
  • Simple animations

Tool Options: Book Creator, Adobe Spark, Canva, or PowerPoint

3. Cultural Reflection

Write a 200-word reflection addressing:

  • How your digital choices respected Māori storytelling traditions
  • What you learned about the relationship between traditional and digital storytelling
  • Any challenges you faced in maintaining cultural authenticity

Teacher Implementation Notes

Preparation

  • Familiarize yourself with local pūrākau - consult with school's Māori community liaison if available
  • Test digital tools beforehand and ensure student access
  • Prepare examples of both traditional and digital stories

Differentiation Strategies

  • Support: Provide storyboards or templates for planning
  • Extension: Challenge students to incorporate more complex Māori concepts like whakapapa or mana

Assessment Tips

  • Focus on cultural understanding rather than technical perfection
  • Consider peer-assessment using the rubric criteria
