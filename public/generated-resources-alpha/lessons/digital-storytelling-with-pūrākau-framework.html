Cultural Context: The Art of Pūrākau

Pūrākau are traditional Māori narratives that carry knowledge, values, and history. They follow distinct patterns and serve important cultural functions:

  • Whakapapa - Connections between people, places and events
  • Akoranga - Embedded teachings and lessons
  • Tikanga - Cultural protocols and practices

Tikanga Considerations:

When working with pūrākau, we must respect these cultural protocols:

  1. Some stories have tapu (sacred restrictions) - check with local kaumātua if unsure
  2. Always acknowledge the iwi or source of any traditional stories used
  3. Te reo Māori should be used appropriately and accurately
  4. Consider the mana (prestige) of the stories and characters

Digital Storytelling Activity

Create Your Modern Pūrākau

Steps:

  1. Form small groups (3-4 students)
  2. Brainstorm a contemporary story that follows the pūrākau structure
  3. Develop your story using this framework:
    • Setting (place and time)
    • Main characters (with Māori values)
    • Challenge/conflict
    • Resolution
    • Lesson/teaching
  4. Choose a digital format:
    • Digital comic (using Google Slides or Canva)
    • Podcast episode
    • Short video (live action or animation)
    • Interactive story (using Twine or Google Sites)
  5. Include at least 5 te reo Māori words/phrases used appropriately
  6. Present to class and explain your creative choices

Teacher Notes:

Differentiation: Allow students to choose their digital medium based on skills and interests. Provide templates for less confident students.

Technical Support: Ensure students have access to necessary devices and platforms. Chromebook-friendly options include:

  • Canva (comics, presentations)
  • Online Voice Recorder (podcasts)
  • WeVideo (basic video editing)
  • Google Sites (interactive stories)

Cultural Support: Have Māori vocabulary lists available and encourage consultation with school's Māori advisor if needed.

Extension Activities

  • Research and compare different iwi versions of the same pūrākau
