Digital Storytelling with Pūrākau Framework

He Whakamōhiotanga | Introduction

Pūrākau are traditional Māori narratives that carry cultural knowledge, values, and wisdom. In this lesson, you will learn how to create a digital story using the Pūrākau framework, blending traditional storytelling with modern digital tools.

Ngā Whāinga Ako | Learning Objectives

  • Understand the structure and purpose of Pūrākau in Māori storytelling.
  • Apply the Pūrākau framework to create a digital narrative.
  • Use digital tools (e.g., Google Slides, Canva, or Adobe Spark) to enhance storytelling.
  • Reflect on the cultural significance of storytelling in Te Ao Māori.

Te Horopaki Ahurea | Cultural Context

Pūrākau are more than just stories—they are taonga (treasures) that preserve history, values, and identity. When working with Pūrākau, we must:

  • Respect the mana (prestige) of the stories.
  • Acknowledge the iwi (tribe) or hapū (sub-tribe) from which the story originates.
  • Ensure accuracy when retelling or adapting traditional narratives.

Cultural Safety Note: If using a traditional Pūrākau, consult with local kaumātua (elders) or cultural advisors to ensure appropriate use.

Ngā Mahi | Activities

1. Exploring Pūrākau

Read or listen to a traditional Pūrākau (e.g., Māui and the Sun or Rata and the Waka). Identify:

  • The key characters and their roles.
  • The moral or lesson of the story.
  • How the story connects to the natural world.

2. Planning Your Digital Story

Using the Pūrākau framework, outline your story:

  • Whakapapa (Origins): Where does the story begin?
  • Hītori (Journey): What challenges or events occur?
  • Whakamutunga (Conclusion): What is the outcome or lesson?

3. Creating Your Digital Story

Choose a digital tool (e.g., Google Slides, Canva, or Adobe Spark) and create a multimedia presentation that includes:

  • Text (narration or dialogue).
  • Images or illustrations.
  • Optional: Voice narration or background music.

Aromatawai | Assessment

Your digital story will be assessed based on:

Criteria Excellent (4) Proficient (3) Developing (2) Needs Work (1)
Cultural Authenticity Story respects and accurately reflects Māori storytelling traditions. Story mostly aligns with Māori traditions. Some cultural elements are present but may be unclear. Little to no cultural connection.
Story Structure Clear beginning, journey, and conclusion. Most elements are present. Some parts are unclear. No clear structure.
Creativity & Digital Skills Highly engaging, uses multimedia effectively. Good use of digital tools. Basic digital elements. Minimal effort in presentation.

Ngā Mahi Whakawhānui | Extension Activities

  • Collaborative Storytelling: Work in groups to create an interactive digital story with branching narratives.
  • Whakapapa Connections: Research and include your own family or local whakapapa (genealogy) in a modern Pūrākau.
  • Augmented Reality (AR) Storytelling: Use tools like HP Reveal to add AR elements to your story.

Ngā Whakaritenga mō ngā Ākonga Rerekē | Adaptations

  • For Struggling Learners: Provide a storyboard template or allow oral storytelling with minimal text.
  • For Advanced Learners: Challenge them to include waiata (songs) or whakataukī (proverbs) in their digital story.
  • For Visual Learners: Encourage the use of illustrations, animations, or video clips.

Ngā Tohutohu mā ngā Kaiako | Teacher Notes

Preparation: Familiarize yourself with local Pūrākau and consult with kaumātua if needed.

Implementation Steps:

  1. Introduce the concept of Pūrākau and its cultural significance.
  2. Guide students through analyzing a traditional story.
  3. Support students in planning their digital narratives.
  4. Provide time for creation and peer feedback.
  5. Host a class showcase where students present their digital stories.

Digital Tools Suggestions:

  • Google Slides (simple, collaborative).
  • Canva (for visually rich presentations).
  • Adobe Spark (for video storytelling).
