Digital Storytelling with Pūrākau Framework

Year Levels: Years 8-10 | Subject: English

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand the structure and purpose of traditional Māori pūrākau (stories)
  • Apply pūrākau framework to create original digital narratives
  • Develop digital storytelling skills using appropriate technologies
  • Demonstrate cultural awareness in storytelling practices
  • Critically reflect on the storytelling process

Cultural Context: Pūrākau in Te Ao Māori

Pūrākau are traditional Māori narratives that convey knowledge, values, and worldviews. They serve as:

  • Historical records of iwi and hapū
  • Teaching tools for important lessons
  • Connections between the spiritual and physical worlds
  • Expressions of cultural identity

Tikanga Considerations

When working with pūrākau or creating new stories in this tradition:

  • Respect the sacred nature of some stories - check with local iwi if using specific tribal narratives
  • Understand that pūrākau often have multiple versions - there may not be one "correct" version
  • Consider the relationship between people and the natural world (whakapapa connections)
  • Use te reo Māori appropriately - consult Māori dictionaries or speakers for accuracy
  • Acknowledge sources and origins of any traditional elements used

Pūrākau Story Structure

Traditional pūrākau often follow this pattern:

  1. Whakapapa (Origins): Establishes connections between characters and environment
  2. Hekenga (Challenge): Introduces a problem or disruption
  3. Raruraru (Conflict): Develops the central struggle
  4. Ohooho (Awakening): Moment of realization or transformation
  5. Whakatau (Resolution): Brings closure and often a lesson

Modern adaptations might include:

  • Digital elements (images, sound, animation)
  • Contemporary settings with traditional themes
  • Bilingual text (English and te reo Māori)

Activity: Create Your Digital Pūrākau

Part 1: Planning

Use this framework to plan your story:

  1. Choose a theme (e.g., friendship, courage, environmental care)
  2. Develop characters with connections to nature
  3. Map your story using the pūrākau structure
  4. Identify cultural elements you'll include
  5. Plan your digital format (slideshow, video, comic, etc.)

Part 2: Creation

Create your digital story using tools like:

  • Google Slides or PowerPoint
  • Canva or Storyboard That
  • Simple video editors (WeVideo, iMovie)
  • Digital comic creators (Pixton, MakeBeliefsComix)

Remember to:

  • Include appropriate visuals that respect Māori culture
  • Use sound/music thoughtfully (consider traditional instruments)
  • Credit all sources and influences

Extension Activities

Create a bilingual version of your story (English/te reo Māori)
Develop an audio version with sound effects and music
Collaborate with a buddy to create a "chain story" where each adds a section
Research a traditional pūrākau from your local area and create a modern retelling
Present your story to a younger audience and gather their feedback

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge: Students should have basic digital skills and some exposure to Māori stories.

Duration: 4-6 lessons (adjust based on digital tools used).

Differentiation:

  • Provide story starters for students needing support
  • Offer choice of digital tools based on student capability
  • Create mixed-ability groups for collaborative projects

Cultural Support:

  • Invite local kaumatua or Māori storytellers to share pūrākau
  • Use school cultural advisors to review student work for appropriateness
  • Provide access to quality Māori language resources

Assessment Tips:

  • Use the rubric for formative feedback during the process
  • Include peer assessment using the same criteria
  • Have students complete a brief written reflection with their submission
