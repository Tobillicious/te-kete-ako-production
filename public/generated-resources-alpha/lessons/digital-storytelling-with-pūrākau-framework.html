Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori

Pūrākau are traditional Māori narratives that carry knowledge, values, and history. They serve multiple purposes:

  • Preserving tribal knowledge (mātauranga Māori)
  • Teaching moral lessons and values
  • Explaining natural phenomena and origins
  • Maintaining connections to the land (whenua) and ancestors (tīpuna)

Tikanga Considerations:

  • Respect: Treat all stories with reverence - they are taonga (treasures)
  • Authenticity: Ensure proper attribution if using traditional stories
  • Context: Understand the cultural significance before adapting
  • Permission: Seek guidance when using stories from specific iwi

Digital Storytelling Activity

Creating Your Modern Pūrākau

Using the pūrākau framework, create a digital story that addresses a contemporary issue while maintaining traditional storytelling elements.

Steps:

  1. Choose your kaupapa (theme): Select a meaningful topic (environment, identity, community)
  2. Develop characters: Create characters with connections to land and ancestors
  3. Structure your story: Follow the pūrākau framework (see above)
  4. Select digital tools: Choose from:
    • Digital comics (Pixton, Canva)
    • Audio storytelling (Anchor, Soundtrap)
    • Short video (iMovie, Adobe Spark)
    • Interactive stories (Twine, Scratch)
  5. Incorporate cultural elements: Consider adding:
    • Māori proverbs (whakataukī)
    • Traditional motifs
    • Te reo Māori phrases
    • References to local landmarks

Extension Activities

1. Whakapapa Connections

Research your own whakapapa (genealogy) or that of a local landmark. Create a digital timeline showing connections between people, places and events.

2. Bilingual Storytelling

Create a version of your story that incorporates te reo Māori phrases or develops parallel English/Māori narratives.

3. Community Stories

Interview community elders about local stories and create digital versions to preserve and share them.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Preparation:

  • Familiarize yourself with local pūrākau (check with school's Māori advisor)
  • Test digital tools to ensure accessibility on school devices
  • Prepare examples of both traditional and modern pūrākau

Differentiation Strategies:

  • Scaffolding: Provide story templates or partially completed examples
  • Extension: Challenge advanced students to incorporate more te reo Māori
  • Collaboration: Allow group work for students needing support

Cultural Safety:

  • Create a respectful learning environment
  • Invite Māori community members to share stories if possible
  • Ensure students understand the significance of what they're working with

Timeframe:

This unit works well as a 4-6 week project with 2-3 lessons per week.
