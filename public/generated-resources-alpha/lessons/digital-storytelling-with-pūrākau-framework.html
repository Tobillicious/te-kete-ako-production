Digital Storytelling with Pūrākau Framework

Year Levels: Years 8-10 | Subject: English

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand the structure and purpose of traditional Māori pūrākau (stories)
  • Apply digital tools to create contemporary versions of traditional narratives
  • Incorporate appropriate tikanga (protocols) in digital storytelling
  • Develop skills in narrative structure, character development, and digital presentation
  • Reflect on the relationship between traditional and contemporary storytelling forms

Cultural Context: Pūrākau in Te Ao Māori

Pūrākau are traditional Māori narratives that convey history, values, and worldviews. Unlike Western storytelling that often focuses on entertainment, pūrākau serve multiple purposes:

  • Preserving tribal history and genealogy (whakapapa)
  • Teaching moral lessons and cultural values
  • Explaining natural phenomena and the origins of things
  • Connecting people to place (whenua)

Tikanga Considerations for Digital Pūrākau:

  • Respect for sources: Acknowledge the iwi or hapū associated with any traditional stories used
  • Appropriate representation: Avoid altering core elements of traditional stories without consultation
  • Cultural safety: Be mindful of sacred (tapu) elements in stories
  • Whanaungatanga: Consider how your digital story builds relationships and connections

Lesson Activities

1. Understanding Pūrākau Structure

Traditional pūrākau often follow this pattern:

  1. Kupu whakataki (Introduction) - Establishes context and characters
  2. Huarahi (Journey) - The main events and challenges
  3. Taumata (Climax) - The turning point or most significant moment
  4. Whakamutunga (Conclusion) - Resolution and lessons learned

2. Creating Your Digital Pūrākau

Choose one of these approaches:

  • Modern retelling: Update a traditional pūrākau with contemporary settings/characters while keeping the core message
  • Original story: Create a new story following pūrākau structure that addresses a modern issue
  • Personal pūrākau: Tell a significant story from your own life using the pūrākau framework

Digital Tools (choose one):

  • Comic strip creator (e.g., Pixton, Canva)
  • Digital slideshow with narration
  • Simple animation tools (e.g., Animaker, Powtoon)
  • Interactive story (e.g., Twine for branching narratives)

Extension Activities

1. Pūrākau Podcast

Create an audio recording of your story with sound effects and music. Research traditional Māori instruments (taonga pūoro) to include.

2. Augmented Reality Storytelling

Use tools like HP Reveal to create AR markers that reveal different parts of your story when scanned.

3. Community Connection

Interview kaumātua (elders) about local stories and create digital versions to share with younger students.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge:

Students should have basic digital literacy skills appropriate to the tools being used. Some prior exposure to Māori stories would be beneficial but isn't essential.

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide story starters or partially completed templates. Allow partnership work.
  • Extension: Challenge students to create bilingual versions or explore more complex digital tools.

Cultural Considerations:

  • Consult with your school's Māori community liaison if students want to work with specific iwi stories
  • Be aware that some stories may be considered tapu (sacred) to particular iwi or hapū
  • Encourage students to make connections to their own cultural storytelling traditions

Timeframe:

This unit works well as a 4-6 week project with 2-3 lessons per week. Allow time for:

  • Introduction to pūrākau (1-2 lessons)
  • Planning and drafting (2-3 lessons)
  • Digital creation (3-4 lessons)
  • Sharing and reflection (1-2 lessons)
