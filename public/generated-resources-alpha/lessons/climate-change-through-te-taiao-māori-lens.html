Climate Change Through Te Taiao Māori Lens

Year Levels: 10-12 | Subject: Science | Duration: 3-4 lessons

Learning Objectives

By the end of this unit, ākonga (students) will be able to:

  • Explain key climate change concepts using both scientific and mātauranga Māori frameworks
  • Analyze climate change impacts through a Te Taiao (Māori environmental) perspective
  • Compare and contrast Western scientific and Māori knowledge systems regarding environmental change
  • Develop strategies for climate action grounded in kaitiakitanga (guardianship)
  • Apply systems thinking to understand interconnected environmental relationships

Cultural Context and Tikanga Considerations

Whakapapa Connections

In te ao Māori, all elements of the natural world are connected through whakapapa (genealogy). Climate change is understood as a disruption to these sacred relationships between tangata (people) and the natural world.

Key Māori Concepts

  • Te Taiao: The Māori worldview of the environment as a living system
  • Kaitiakitanga: The responsibility to care for and protect the environment
  • Mauri: The life force present in all things
  • Whanaungatanga: The interconnected relationships between all living things
  • Tino rangatiratanga: The right to self-determination over one's environment

Teacher Guidance

Cultural Safety: Ensure the classroom environment respects Māori perspectives as equally valid to Western science. Invite local iwi representatives if possible.

Karakia: Consider beginning the lesson with a simple karakia (prayer) to acknowledge the mauri of the environment you'll be discussing.

Language Support: Provide pronunciation guides for Māori terms and encourage their correct use.

Understanding Climate Change Through Dual Lenses

Climate Change Indicators

Click the buttons to explore different climate change indicators and their Māori interpretations:

Pāmahana Piki (Rising Temperatures)

Scientific View: Global average temperature increase due to greenhouse gas emissions

Māori Interpretation: Disruption of Tāne Mahuta's realm (forests) and the balance between Rangi (sky) and Papa (earth)

Impact: Changes in traditional food gathering seasons (maramataka), stress on native species

Tai Pari (Sea Level Rise)

Scientific View: Thermal expansion and ice melt causing ocean levels to rise

Māori Interpretation: Tangaroa's response to human disrespect, loss of ancestral coastal lands

Impact: Threat to marae and urupā (burial grounds) in coastal areas, changes in mahinga kai (food gathering places)

Ngaro Koiora (Biodiversity Loss)

Scientific View: Species extinction due to habitat loss and climate change

Māori Interpretation: Breaking of whakapapa connections, loss of kin relationships

Impact: Decline in traditional medicines (rongoā), disruption of ecological knowledge systems

Āhuarangi Taikaha (Extreme Weather)

Scientific View: Increased frequency/intensity of storms, droughts, floods

Māori Interpretation: Atua (deities) expressing displeasure at environmental harm

Impact: Damage to cultural infrastructure, challenges for traditional forecasting methods

Activity: Whakapapa of Climate Change

Task: Create a visual representation showing the whakapapa (genealogical connections) of climate change impacts in your local area.

Steps:

  1. Identify a local climate change impact (e.g., coastal erosion, changing bird migrations)
  2. Research both scientific causes and Māori understandings of this phenomenon
  3. Create a diagram showing:
    • Western scientific causal relationships
    • Māori whakapapa connections
    • Points of alignment and difference between the two knowledge systems
  4. Include at least three mātauranga Māori sources in your research

Format Options: Digital infographic, poster, or interactive web page

Extension Activities

Option 1: Kaitiakitanga Action Plan

Develop a practical kaitiakitanga plan for your school or community that addresses climate change. Include:

  • Scientific rationale for your chosen actions
  • Māori principles guiding your approach
  • Implementation timeline
  • Methods for measuring impact

Option 2: Future Scenarios

Create two future scenarios for Aotearoa in 2050:

  1. Business-as-usual climate trajectory
  2. Regenerative future based on kaitiakitanga principles

For each scenario, describe impacts on:

  • Cultural practices
  • Biodiversity
  • Community wellbeing
  • Economic systems
