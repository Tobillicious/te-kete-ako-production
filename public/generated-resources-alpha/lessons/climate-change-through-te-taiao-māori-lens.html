Climate Change Through Te Taiao Māori Lens

Science | Years 10-12

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand climate change through both Western science and Mātauranga Māori perspectives
  • Explore the concept of kaitiakitanga (guardianship) in environmental management
  • Analyze traditional Māori environmental indicators of climate change
  • Compare and contrast indigenous and scientific knowledge systems
  • Develop strategies for climate action informed by Te Ao Māori principles

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori Considerations

This lesson approaches climate change through Te Taiao (the Māori worldview of the environment), recognizing the deep connection between tangata whenua (people of the land) and their environment.

Key Concepts:

  • Whakapapa: The interconnectedness of all living things
  • Mauri: The life force present in all things
  • Kaitiakitanga: Guardianship and sustainable management of resources
  • Manaakitanga: Care for people and environment

Protocols to Observe:

  • Begin with a karakia (prayer) to acknowledge the mauri of the space and topic
  • Respect tapu (sacredness) when discussing environmental elements
  • Use correct pronunciation of Māori terms (provide phonetic guides)
  • Acknowledge local iwi knowledge specific to your region

Understanding Climate Change Through Dual Lenses

Compare Perspectives Activity

Click the buttons to explore different understandings of climate phenomena:

Western Science Perspective

  • Quantitative data (temperature records, CO2 levels)
  • Global climate models and predictions
  • Focus on human-induced factors (fossil fuels, deforestation)
  • Technological solutions (renewable energy, carbon capture)

Mātauranga Māori Perspective

  • Observation of natural indicators (plant flowering times, bird migrations)
  • Intergenerational knowledge passed through whakapapa
  • Holistic view connecting environmental health to human wellbeing
  • Solutions based on kaitiakitanga and restoring balance

Activity: Traditional Environmental Indicators

Māori have long used environmental signs to predict weather and seasonal changes. Research and discuss how these indicators reflect climate change:

Indicator (Tohu) Traditional Meaning Climate Change Impact
Pohutukawa flowering Signaled the arrival of summer Now flowering earlier due to warmer temperatures
Tītī (muttonbird) arrival Marked seasonal fishing times Changing migration patterns affecting traditional harvest
Maramataka (Māori lunar calendar) Guided planting and harvesting Shifting seasons making traditional timing less reliable

Discussion Questions:

  1. How might these changes impact Māori cultural practices?
  2. What traditional knowledge could help address climate challenges?
  3. How can science and mātauranga Māori work together?

Extension Activities

1. Local Iwi Knowledge Research

Investigate how your local iwi or hapū have observed and responded to climate change:

  • Interview local kaumatua (elders) about environmental changes
  • Research traditional place names that reflect past environments
  • Map how climate change might affect culturally significant sites

2. Maramataka and Climate

Create a modern adaptation of the Māori lunar calendar that accounts for climate change:

  • Compare traditional seasonal indicators with current observations
  • Propose adjustments to planting/harvesting times
  • Design a visual representation of your updated maramataka

Teacher Implementation Notes

Preparation:

  • Consult with local iwi or Māori advisors to ensure cultural appropriateness
  • Prepare pronunciation guides for Māori terms
  • Identify local environmental changes that students can observe

Delivery Tips:

  • Begin with a karakia (sample provided in resources)
  • Emphasize that both knowledge systems have value - this isn't about proving one "right"
  • Use local examples wherever possible to make concepts relevant
  • Allow time for reflection on personal connections to the environment

Differentiation:

  • For less confident students: Provide template for climate action plan
  • For advanced students: Challenge to identify potential conflicts between knowledge systems and propose resolutions
