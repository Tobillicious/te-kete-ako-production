Climate Change Through Te Taiao Māori Lens

Year Levels: 10-12 | Subject: Science | Duration: 3-4 lessons

Learning Objectives

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

  • Explain key climate change concepts using both scientific and Māori frameworks
  • Describe how kaitiakitanga (guardianship) relates to environmental management
  • Analyze climate impacts through te taiao (Māori worldview of environment)
  • Compare Western scientific and Māori knowledge systems regarding climate
  • Develop strategies for climate action grounded in tikanga Māori

Cultural Context: Tikanga Considerations

This unit approaches climate science through te ao Māori (Māori worldview), recognizing:

  • The interconnectedness of all living things (whanaungatanga)
  • The responsibility of humans as guardians (kaitiaki)
  • The importance of mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge) in environmental understanding
  • The concept of mauri (life force) in ecosystems

Protocols to observe:

  • Begin with karakia (prayer) to acknowledge the significance of the topic
  • Respect local iwi knowledge and variations in environmental perspectives
  • Emphasize reciprocal relationships rather than human dominance over nature

Case Study: Ngā Waihotanga Iho (Environmental Legacy)

Research a local environmental change (e.g., coastal erosion, changing bird migrations) through:

  1. Scientific data (temperature records, sea level measurements)
  2. Māori observations (mātauranga passed through generations)
  3. Interview an elder or kaumatua about changes they've witnessed

Assessment: Kaitiakitanga Action Plan

Students will develop a climate action plan for their school/community that integrates:

  • Scientific understanding of climate change
  • Kaitiakitanga principles
  • Practical steps grounded in tikanga

Extension Activities

1. Taonga Species Investigation

Research how climate change affects a taonga (treasured) species important to local iwi (e.g., tītī muttonbird, kūmara). Create an infographic showing:

  • Scientific data on climate impacts
  • Cultural significance of the species
  • Traditional and modern conservation strategies

2. Whakataukī (Proverb) Analysis

Analyze how these whakataukī relate to climate change:

  • "Toitū te marae a Tāne, toitū te marae a Tangaroa, toitū te iwi" (If the domains of Tāne and Tangaroa are healthy, the people will be healthy)
  • "Ka ora te whenua, ka ora te tangata" (When the land is well, the people are well)

Create modern interpretations applying these to current climate challenges.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Preparation:

  • Consult with local iwi environmental representatives if possible
  • Prepare Māori language pronunciation guides for key terms
  • Gather both scientific data and Māori environmental narratives

Differentiation:

  • For less confident students: Provide sentence starters for comparison activities
  • For advanced students: Add complexity by examining tensions between knowledge systems

Sensitive Topics:

  • Be aware that climate impacts may cause eco-anxiety - balance with empowerment through kaitiakitanga
  • Some iwi may have specific protocols around discussing certain environmental knowledge
