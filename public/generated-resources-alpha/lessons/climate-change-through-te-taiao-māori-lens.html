Climate Change Through Te Taiao Māori Lens

Years 10-12 | Science | Indigenous Environmental Knowledge

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Explain key climate change concepts using both scientific and Māori worldviews
  • Describe how traditional Māori environmental knowledge (mātauranga Māori) informs our understanding of climate change
  • Analyze climate change impacts through the lens of kaitiakitanga (guardianship)
  • Compare and contrast Western scientific and Māori perspectives on environmental change
  • Develop strategies for climate action grounded in both scientific and indigenous knowledge

NZ Curriculum Links: Science (Living World, Planet Earth and Beyond), Social Sciences, Health and PE

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori Considerations

This lesson approaches climate change through Te Taiao (the Māori worldview of the natural environment), which sees humans as part of an interconnected web of life rather than separate from nature.

Key Concepts:

  • Whakapapa: The genealogical connections between all living things
  • Kaitiakitanga: Guardianship and sustainable management of resources
  • Mauri: The life force present in all things
  • Tapu and noa: The sacred and the ordinary

Tikanga for the Classroom:

  • Begin with a karakia (prayer/invocation) to acknowledge the interconnectedness of all things
  • Respect mana whenua (local indigenous authority) by acknowledging local iwi perspectives
  • Use correct pronunciation of Māori terms (provide audio support if possible)
  • Create space for both scientific and indigenous ways of knowing

Understanding Climate Change Through Dual Lenses

Compare Perspectives Activity

Click the buttons to explore different perspectives on climate change:

Western Scientific View

  • Climate change as measured through temperature records, ice cores, and atmospheric CO₂ levels
  • Primary causes: Greenhouse gas emissions from human activities
  • Impacts: Rising sea levels, extreme weather, biodiversity loss
  • Solutions: Technological innovations, policy changes, carbon reduction

Māori Worldview

  • Climate change as disruption to whakapapa (genealogical connections)
  • Primary causes: Breakdown of kaitiakitanga (guardianship) relationships
  • Impacts: Damage to mauri (life force) of land, water, and ecosystems
  • Solutions: Restoration of balance through traditional practices and values

Integrated Understanding

  • Both perspectives recognize human responsibility for environmental changes
  • Science provides measurement tools while Māori knowledge offers holistic frameworks
  • Combined approaches can create more effective, culturally grounded solutions
  • Examples: Combining traditional planting calendars with climate data

Case Study: Coastal Erosion

Examine how both knowledge systems understand and respond to coastal erosion:

Aspect Scientific Approach Māori Approach
Understanding Cause Sea level rise, storm frequency, human development Disruption of taniwha (spiritual guardians), loss of mana (prestige) of place
Monitoring GPS measurements, aerial photography Observation of tohu (natural signs), ancestral knowledge
Solutions Sea walls, managed retreat Rāhui (temporary restrictions), restoration of mauri

Discussion Questions:

  1. What strengths does each perspective bring to understanding coastal erosion?
  2. How might these approaches complement each other in developing solutions?
  3. What cultural considerations would be important when implementing solutions?

Assessment: Climate Action Plan

Students will create a climate action plan that integrates both scientific and Māori perspectives.

Extension Activities

1. Local Climate Impacts Research

Investigate how climate change is affecting your local area through both scientific data and Māori knowledge:

  • Interview local scientists about climate data for your region
  • Connect with local iwi representatives to understand traditional observations
  • Compare and contrast these perspectives in a report or presentation

2. Traditional Ecological Knowledge Project

Research and document one example of traditional Māori environmental knowledge:

  • Lunar calendars (maramataka) for planting and fishing
  • Traditional weather indicators (tohu)
  • Resource management practices (rāhui)

Teacher Implementation Notes

Preparation:

  • Consult with local iwi or Māori advisors to ensure cultural appropriateness
  • Prepare pronunciation guides for Māori terms (consider audio recordings)
  • Gather both scientific and indigenous resources about local climate impacts

Lesson Sequence:

  1. Begin with karakia and introduction to dual knowledge systems
  2. Interactive comparison activity (15 mins)
  3. Case study analysis in small groups (20 mins)
  4. Whole class discussion of findings (15 mins)
  5. Introduction of assessment task (10 mins)

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide sentence starters for discussion questions
  • Extension: Have students research specific iwi responses to climate change

Sensitive Issues:

  • Be aware that climate change may cause eco-anxiety - provide hope through action-oriented solutions
  • Ensure Māori perspectives are presented with respect and accuracy
