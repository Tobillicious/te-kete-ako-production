Game Development with Cultural Themes

Cultural Context

This unit explores how digital games can celebrate and share aspects of Te Ao Māori (the Māori world view). We'll focus on respectful representation of Māori culture in game design, drawing inspiration from pūrākau (traditional stories), taonga (treasures), and Māori concepts.

Cultural Safety: Always consult with local iwi or Māori advisors when using specific cultural elements. Avoid sacred (tapu) elements in games. Focus on educational and celebratory aspects rather than competitive or violent contexts.

Learning Objectives

  • Develop a digital game that incorporates authentic Māori cultural elements
  • Apply computational thinking to solve game design challenges
  • Demonstrate understanding of cultural protocols in digital representation
  • Use appropriate game development tools to create interactive experiences
  • Evaluate the effectiveness of cultural representation in digital media

NZ Curriculum Alignment: Digital Technologies Progress Outcomes 4-6, Social Sciences, Te Ao Māori

Quick Cultural Concepts Quiz

Test your knowledge before we begin:

1. What does pūrākau mean?

2. Which of these would NOT be appropriate to include in a game without expert consultation?

Lesson Activities

Activity 1: Exploring Cultural Representation

Research existing games that incorporate indigenous cultures (e.g., Never Alone, which features Iñupiat culture).

In small groups, analyze:

  • How cultural elements are represented
  • What makes the representation respectful
  • How gameplay mechanics reflect cultural values

Share findings with the class.

Activity 2: Brainstorming Game Concepts

Using these Māori concepts as inspiration, brainstorm game ideas:

  • Whakapapa (genealogy) - Could this be a puzzle or connection game?
  • Kaitiakitanga (guardianship) - How about an environmental simulation?
  • Manaakitanga (hospitality) - Could this be a social interaction game?

Sketch your top 3 ideas and share with a partner for feedback.

Activity 3: Prototype Development

Choose one concept to develop further. Using Scratch, Unity, or another platform:

  1. Create a storyboard for your game
  2. Develop core gameplay mechanics
  3. Design culturally appropriate characters/environments
  4. Implement basic functionality

Cultural Check: Have you avoided sacred elements? Is your representation accurate?

Implementation Guide

Preparation:

  1. Consult with Māori advisors or cultural experts in your community
  2. Prepare example games showing good cultural representation
  3. Set up development environments (Scratch accounts, Unity installs, etc.)

Lesson Sequence:

  1. Introduction to cultural representation in games (1-2 lessons)
  2. Concept development and cultural consultation (2 lessons)
  3. Prototype development (4-6 lessons)
  4. Playtesting and refinement (2 lessons)
  5. Final presentations and reflection (1-2 lessons)

Technical Options:

  • Beginner: Scratch or Twine
  • Intermediate: Construct 3 or GameMaker
  • Advanced: Unity or Godot

Assessment Rubric

Extension Activities

For Advanced Learners

  • Develop a multiplayer version of your game that incorporates manaakitanga (hospitality) concepts
  • Create a companion website explaining the cultural elements in your game
  • Implement more sophisticated AI for NPCs based on Māori values
  • Develop your game for mobile platforms
  • Create a marketing plan that respects cultural protocols while promoting your game

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

Support Strategies

For students needing more support:

  • Provide pre-made game templates to modify
  • Use visual programming tools like Scratch
  • Pair students with more experienced peers
  • Focus on one cultural concept rather than multiple

For English Language Learners:

  • Provide bilingual vocabulary lists (Māori/English)
  • Use more visual planning tools
  • Allow verbal explanations of concepts

For students with limited technology access:

  • Focus on paper prototyping and design
  • Use browser-based tools that work on Chromebooks
  • Provide extended time for computer access
