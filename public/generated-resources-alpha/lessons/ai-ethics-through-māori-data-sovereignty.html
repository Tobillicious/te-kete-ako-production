AI Ethics Through Māori Data Sovereignty

Digital Technology | Years 11-13

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Explain the concept of Māori Data Sovereignty (MDS) and its principles
  • Analyze how AI systems can impact indigenous data rights
  • Apply MDS principles to evaluate ethical considerations in AI development
  • Develop strategies for incorporating indigenous perspectives in technology design
  • Reflect on personal responsibility in digital citizenship from a Te Ao Māori perspective

NZ Curriculum Alignment: Digital Technologies Progress Outcomes 6-8, Social Sciences, Tikanga ā-Iwi

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori and Technology

This lesson is grounded in Māori Data Sovereignty (MDS) principles which assert that Māori have inherent rights to control Māori data and information. Key concepts include:

Tapu and Noa in Digital Spaces

In Māori worldview, data can have tapu (sacredness) and requires appropriate handling:

Tapu: The sacred, restricted, or set apart nature of people, places, or things in Māori culture.

Consider how these principles apply to AI systems:

  • Who has access to Māori data?
  • How is Māori data being used?
  • What protections exist for sensitive information?

Key Principles of Māori Data Sovereignty

  1. Rangatiratanga: Self-determination and control over Māori data
  2. Whakapapa: Understanding the relationships and connections in data
  3. Kaitiakitanga: Guardianship and ethical stewardship of data
  4. Manaakitanga: Respectful and reciprocal use of data
  5. Kotahitanga: Collective benefit and unity in data use

"A tech company is developing an AI health app for New Zealanders. They want to use historical health data from Māori communities to train their algorithms to predict health outcomes. The data includes sensitive whakapapa information."

Extension Activities

Option 1: Comparative Analysis

Research how other indigenous communities (e.g., Native American, Aboriginal Australian) approach data sovereignty. Compare their principles with Māori Data Sovereignty.

Option 2: Policy Proposal

Draft a policy for your school's use of student data that incorporates Māori Data Sovereignty principles.

Option 3: Digital Product Review

Select a popular app or website and evaluate its data practices against MDS principles. Present your findings.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge

Students should have basic understanding of:

  • How AI systems work (machine learning basics)
  • Key concepts of digital citizenship
  • Fundamental Māori concepts (tapu, noa, mana)

Differentiation Strategies

Support: Provide pre-filled examples for the design challenge table. Use mixed-ability grouping.

Extension: Challenge advanced students to develop implementation plans for their ethical frameworks.

Timing

This lesson is designed for 2-3 class periods (100-150 minutes):

  • Introduction and cultural context: 30 min
  • Case study analysis: 40 min
  • Design challenge: 50 min
  • Reflection and assessment: 30 min

Cultural Safety

Ensure:

  • Māori students feel comfortable sharing perspectives
  • Non-Māori students approach topics with respect
  • Consult with local iwi or Māori advisors if discussing sensitive topics

Algorithmic Bias: When computer systems reflect the implicit values of the humans involved in their creation.
