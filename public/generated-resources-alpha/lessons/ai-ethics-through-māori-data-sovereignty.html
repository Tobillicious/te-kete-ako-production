AI Ethics Through Māori Data Sovereignty

Digital Technology | Years 11-13

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Explain the concept of Māori Data Sovereignty (Tino Rangatiratanga over data)
  • Analyze how AI systems might impact Māori data rights
  • Apply ethical frameworks to evaluate AI data collection practices
  • Develop guidelines for culturally responsive AI development
  • Compare Western and Māori perspectives on data ownership

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori and Technology

Māori Data Sovereignty (Raraunga Māori) is based on the principle that Māori have inherent rights to control data about Māori people, resources, and knowledge. This stems from:

  • Te Tiriti o Waitangi (Article 2 guarantees tino rangatiratanga)
  • The concept of kaitiakitanga (guardianship and responsibility)
  • The importance of whakapapa (genealogical connections)
  • The value of manaakitanga (respect and care for people)

Whakataukī Reflection

"He kai kei aku ringa" - There is food at the end of my hands (the value of self-determination)

Discuss in pairs: How might this whakataukī apply to control over data and AI systems?

Understanding Māori Data Sovereignty

Key principles from the Te Mana Raraunga Māori Data Sovereignty Network:

  1. Rights: Māori have rights and interests in Māori data
  2. Authority: Māori have the right to exercise authority over Māori data
  3. Relationships: Data must be managed to protect Māori relationships
  4. Wellbeing: Data governance must advance Māori wellbeing

Case Study: Facial Recognition Technology

A company is developing AI facial recognition using images collected from social media. The dataset includes many Māori faces without explicit consent.

Consider these questions:

  1. What tikanga principles might be violated?
  2. How could this impact Māori communities?
  3. What alternative approaches would respect Māori Data Sovereignty?

Ethical Framework Comparison

Aspect Western Approach Māori Approach
Data Ownership Individual ownership Collective rights (hapū/iwi)
Consent Individual consent forms Community consultation (hui process)
Purpose Commercial benefit Community wellbeing (ora)

Developing Ethical Guidelines

Create 3-5 guidelines for AI developers to respect Māori Data Sovereignty. Consider:

  • Data collection methods
  • Storage and access
  • Commercial use
  • Cultural impact assessments

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge: Students should have basic understanding of AI systems and data collection practices.

Cultural Considerations:

  • Consult with your school's Māori community liaison if available
  • Be prepared to discuss Treaty of Waitangi implications
  • Create a respectful space for diverse perspectives

Differentiation:

  • Provide sentence starters for discussion responses
  • Offer Māori language support through Te Aka Māori Dictionary
  • Pair students for collaborative activities

Assessment: Use the rubric to evaluate student responses in their digital portfolios.
