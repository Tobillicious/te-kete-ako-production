AI Ethics Through Māori Data Sovereignty

Year Levels: 11-13 | Subject: Digital Technology

Learning Objectives

  • Understand key principles of Māori Data Sovereignty (MDS)
  • Analyze how AI systems can impact indigenous data rights
  • Apply tikanga Māori to ethical decision-making in technology
  • Develop protocols for culturally responsive AI development
  • Evaluate case studies through both technical and cultural lenses

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori and Technology

This lesson is grounded in Māori Data Sovereignty principles that assert Māori rights to control Māori data. Key concepts include:

  • Rangatiratanga: Self-determination over Māori data
  • Whanaungatanga: Relationships and responsibilities in data collection
  • Manaakitanga: Ethical care of data and its subjects
  • Kaitiakitanga: Guardianship and protection of data

Cultural safety note: When discussing indigenous data, we acknowledge data is not just information but represents people, ancestors, and future generations.

Understanding Māori Data Sovereignty

Māori Data Sovereignty (MDS) refers to the inherent rights and interests that Māori have in relation to the collection, ownership, and application of Māori data.

Case: Predictive Policing Algorithms

A city police department implemented an AI system to predict crime hotspots. The algorithm disproportionately flagged Māori neighborhoods, leading to increased surveillance.

Analysis Questions:

  1. What training data might have caused this bias?
  2. How does this impact mana (prestige/authority) of Māori communities?
  3. What MDS principles were violated?
  4. How could the development process have incorporated tikanga?

Extension Activities

2. Comparative Analysis

Research how other indigenous communities (Native American, Aboriginal Australian) approach data sovereignty. Identify common themes and unique aspects.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Timing: This lesson is designed for 3-4 class periods (60 minutes each).

Preparation:

  • Consult with your school's Māori community liaison if available
  • Preview recommended resources (below)
  • Prepare printouts of case studies for group work

Differentiation Strategies:

  • Support: Provide sentence starters for analysis questions
  • Extension: Have students research local iwi data policies

Assessment Notes:

The cultural audit can be submitted as written report, presentation, or multimedia format. Emphasize process over perfection when evaluating cultural analysis.
