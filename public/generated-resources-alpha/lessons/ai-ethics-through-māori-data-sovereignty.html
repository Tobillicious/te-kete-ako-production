AI Ethics Through Māori Data Sovereignty

Digital Technology | Years 11-13

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Explain the concept of tino rangatiratanga (self-determination) in relation to data
  • Analyze AI systems through the lens of Māori data sovereignty principles
  • Evaluate ethical considerations in data collection and use from multiple perspectives
  • Develop guidelines for ethical AI development that honor Te Tiriti o Waitangi
  • Apply tikanga (Māori protocols) to digital technology contexts

Cultural Context: Te Ao Māori Perspectives on Data

In Te Ao Māori (the Māori world), data is not just information - it carries mauri (life force) and represents whakapapa (genealogical connections). Māori data sovereignty asserts that Māori have inherent rights to control data about their people, resources, and culture.

Key Principles:

  • Tino rangatiratanga: The right to self-determination over Māori data
  • Whakapapa: Understanding the relationships and connections data represents
  • Kaitiakitanga: Guardianship and ethical stewardship of data
  • Manaakitanga: Respectful and reciprocal use of data
  • Tapu and noa: Sacred and ordinary aspects of data

Understanding Māori Data Sovereignty

Māori data sovereignty challenges conventional Western approaches to data collection and AI development by asserting that:

  • Data about Māori should be governed by Māori
  • Data has cultural and spiritual dimensions beyond its information value
  • Current AI systems often perpetuate colonial biases and power imbalances
  • Ethical technology must honor Te Tiriti o Waitangi relationships

Case Study Analysis

Read the following scenario:

"A government agency is developing an AI system to predict educational outcomes. The system will use historical student data including ethnicity, address, and family income. Māori education leaders express concerns about how this data will be used and who will benefit from the insights."
  1. How might this AI system impact Māori tino rangatiratanga?
  2. What tikanga principles should guide the collection and use of this data?
  3. How could the developers ensure Māori benefit from this technology?
  4. What alternative approaches might better honor Māori data sovereignty?

Activity: Developing Ethical AI Guidelines

Working in small groups, develop a set of guidelines for ethical AI development that incorporates Māori data sovereignty principles. Consider:

  • Data collection protocols
  • Governance and decision-making
  • Benefit sharing
  • Cultural safety
  • Accountability measures

Extension Activities

Option 1: Comparative Analysis

Research how Indigenous peoples in other countries (e.g., First Nations in Canada, Aboriginal Australians) approach data sovereignty. Create a comparison chart highlighting similarities and differences with Māori approaches.

Option 2: AI Audit

Select a commonly used AI system (e.g., facial recognition, recommendation algorithms) and evaluate it against Māori data sovereignty principles. Prepare a report with recommendations for improvement.

Option 3: Digital Whakapapa Project

Design a concept for a digital platform that respects whakapapa data sovereignty. Consider:

  • Who controls the data?
  • How is access managed?
  • What protocols protect cultural knowledge?

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge

Students should have basic understanding of:

  • AI and machine learning concepts
  • Data collection and privacy issues
  • Te Tiriti o Waitangi principles

Differentiation Strategies

Support: Provide pre-made templates for the guideline builder with starter ideas. Pair students strategically.

Extension: Challenge advanced students to develop implementation plans for their guidelines or create prototype systems.

Cultural Safety

  • Consult with local iwi or Māori advisors if available
  • Create a respectful learning environment for discussing sensitive topics
  • Encourage use of te reo Māori terms with proper pronunciation
  • Acknowledge that some students may have personal connections to these issues

Assessment Tips

  • Focus on depth of cultural understanding rather than quantity of content
  • Allow for diverse expressions of learning (oral, visual, written)
  • Consider peer feedback as part of the assessment process
