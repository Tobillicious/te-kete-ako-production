Whaik≈çrero and Contemporary Debate: A Comparison

Structural Comparison

Whaik≈çrero Structure

  1. Tauparapara - Opening incantation
  2. Whakapapa - Genealogical connections
  3. Reference to the occasion/purpose
  4. Main message/argument
  5. References to landmarks and ancestors
  6. Closing remarks and connection to future

Contemporary Debate Structure

  1. Introduction and thesis statement
  2. Point 1 with evidence
  3. Point 2 with evidence
  4. Point 3 with evidence
  5. Rebuttal of opposing arguments
  6. Conclusion and call to action

Activity: Structural Analysis

Watch examples of both whaik≈çrero and contemporary debates. Identify similarities and differences in their structures.

Persuasive Techniques

Shared Techniques

  • Metaphor and imagery - Creating vivid mental pictures
  • Repetition - Emphasizing key points
  • Rhetorical questions - Engaging the audience
  • Appeals to values - Connecting to shared beliefs

  • Whakataukƒ´ (proverbs) - Traditional wisdom
  • Kƒ´waha (idiomatic expressions) - Cultural metaphors
  • Genealogical references - Establishing authority
  • Spiritual acknowledgments - Connecting to the unseen

Speaking Protocols

Whaik≈çrero Protocols

  • Speaker stands to deliver speech
  • Formal opening and closing greetings
  • Use of te reo MƒÅori
  • Respectful body language and delivery
  • Acknowledgement of ancestors and spiritual elements
  • Connection to the land and people

Contemporary Debate Protocols

  • Time limits for speeches
  • Structured rebuttal periods
  • Formal address ("Madam Chair, opposing team...")
  • Evidence-based arguments
  • Respect for procedural rules

Practical Application: Blended Debate Format

This activity combines elements of whaik≈çrero with contemporary debate structures.

Blended Debate Structure

  1. Opening (1 minute) - Formal greeting and acknowledgement of context/occasion
  2. Position Statement (2 minutes) - Clear thesis using cultural references if appropriate
  3. Arguments (4 minutes) - 2-3 main points with evidence, using persuasive techniques from both traditions
  4. Rebuttal (2 minutes) - Response to opposing arguments with respectful language
  5. Conclusion (1 minute) - Summary and closing remarks that connect to broader values

Activity: Practice Debate

Topic: "That New Zealand schools should incorporate more te reo MƒÅori in daily activities."

Divide into teams and prepare arguments using the blended structure. Consider how you might incorporate:

  • Appropriate cultural references
  • Values-based arguments
  • Respectful language and protocols
  • Evidence and logical reasoning

For the affirmative: Focus on cultural preservation, educational benefits, and national identity.

For the negative: Consider practical implementation challenges, resource allocation, and alternative approaches.

For both: Use respectful language, acknowledge valid points from the other side, and ground arguments in shared values.
