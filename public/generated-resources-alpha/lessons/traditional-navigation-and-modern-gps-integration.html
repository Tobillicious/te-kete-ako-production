Learning Objectives

  • Understand traditional Māori (Mātauranga Māori) navigation techniques
  • Explore how modern GPS technology works

  • Analyze the strengths and limitations of each approach
  • Develop appreciation for indigenous knowledge systems in science

Cultural Context: Te Whakatere Waka (Canoe Navigation)

Traditional Māori navigation (whakatere waka) is a sophisticated knowledge system developed over centuries of Pacific voyaging. Key elements include:

  • Te kapehu whetū - Star compass and celestial navigation
  • Ngā tohu o te moana - Ocean signs (currents, swells, wildlife)
  • Ngā hau me ngā āwhā - Wind and weather patterns
  • Te kōrero tuku iho - Oral transmission of knowledge

Tikanga Considerations

When studying Mātauranga Māori:

  • Recognize this as living knowledge, not just historical information
  • Approach with whakaute (respect) and manaaki (care)
  • Understand that some knowledge may be tapu (sacred/restricted)
  • Where possible, connect with local iwi knowledge holders

Traditional Māori Navigation Systems

Māori navigators used multiple environmental cues for wayfinding across vast ocean distances:

Star Compass Activity

Traditional Polynesian navigators divided the horizon into 32 directional points based on star paths:

North: Te Toka o Autahi (Southern Cross)

East: Te Māhutonga (Rising sun)

South: Te Punga o te Waka (Anchor stone)

West: Te Tohorā (Whale constellation)

Task: Research how these star paths change with seasons and how navigators compensated.

Ocean Wayfinding Signs

Sign (Tohu) Meaning Scientific Explanation
Te au (Currents) Direction and strength of ocean flow Thermohaline circulation and wind-driven currents
Ngā ngaru (Swell patterns) Land proximity and direction Wave refraction and diffraction patterns
Ngā manu (Seabirds) Distance from land and fishing grounds Species-specific foraging ranges
Te kōpūpūtanga (Cloud formations) Indication of land over horizon Orographic cloud formation

Modern GPS Technology

Global Positioning System (GPS) uses satellite networks to determine precise locations:

GPS Receiver

Key Concepts:

  • 24+ satellites in medium Earth orbit
  • Triangulation using timing signals
  • Requires at least 4 satellites for 3D positioning
  • Accuracy affected by atmospheric conditions

Comparative Analysis Activity

Compare traditional Māori navigation with modern GPS systems:

Feature Traditional Navigation Modern GPS
Energy Requirements Human observation only Requires satellite network and battery power
Failure Points Cloud cover affects stars Signal blockage, solar flares
Cultural Knowledge Embedded in oral traditions Technical/scientific knowledge
Environmental Awareness Deep connection to natural systems Minimal environmental interaction

Discussion Questions

  1. What advantages does traditional navigation have in certain situations?
  2. How might GPS technology benefit from incorporating traditional knowledge?
  3. What cultural knowledge might be lost with over-reliance on GPS?
  4. How could these systems complement each other?

Extension Activities

1. Star Path Calculator

Research how star positions change throughout the year and create a simple calculator showing which stars would be visible for navigation at different times.

2. GPS Error Investigation

Experiment with GPS receivers in different locations (urban canyons, under trees, indoors) to document accuracy variations.

3. Wayfinding Challenge

Design an orienteering course where students use both modern tools (compass, GPS) and traditional techniques (sun position, landmarks).

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge

Students should have basic understanding of:

  • Earth's rotation and celestial movements
  • Basic geography concepts (latitude/longitude)
  • Cultural respect protocols

Suggested Sequence

  1. Introduce through Polynesian migration stories
  2. Explore traditional navigation techniques
  3. Investigate modern GPS technology
  4. Comparative analysis activity
  5. Reflection on knowledge integration

Differentiation

Support: Provide structured comparison tables with prompts
Extension: Research contemporary Polynesian voyaging societies (e.g., Te Aurere waka)

Safety Considerations

  • Sun safety for outdoor observations
  • Digital safety if using GPS apps
  • Cultural safety when discussing Mātauranga Māori
