Cultural Context: Te Whakatere Waka (Māori Wayfinding)

Traditional Māori navigation is a sophisticated knowledge system developed over centuries of Pacific voyaging. Key concepts include:

  • Te kapehu whetū - Star compass and celestial navigation
  • Ngā tohu o te moana - Ocean signs (currents, swell patterns, wildlife)
  • Ngā hau me ngā āwhā - Wind and weather patterns
  • Te kōrero o ngā tīpuna - Oral traditions and ancestral knowledge

Tikanga Considerations

When studying traditional navigation, we must respect:

  • This knowledge is tapu (sacred) to Māori
  • Some knowledge may be tūtohu (restricted) - check with local iwi
  • Always acknowledge sources of traditional knowledge
  • Understand this as a living knowledge system, not just historical

Traditional Navigation Methods

Star Compass Activity

Traditional navigators used a star compass with 32 directional points. Match these celestial navigation elements:

Māori Name Description
Autahi (Canopus) Southern zenith star for latitude
Te Ikaroa (Milky Way) Cloud formation indicator
Puanga (Rigel) Used for timing voyages (Matariki season)
Tāwhirimātea Wind patterns and weather signs

Ocean Wayfinding Signs

Traditional navigators observed these ocean indicators:

  • Wave patterns reflecting distant land
  • Cloud formations over islands
  • Bird flight paths (especially toroa - albatross)
  • Changes in water color and temperature
  • Marine life distributions

Activity: In pairs, create a mindmap showing how 3 of these indicators could help locate land.

Modern GPS Technology

Global Positioning Systems work through:

  • Network of 24+ satellites orbiting Earth
  • Triangulation of signals from multiple satellites
  • Precision timing measurements
  • Ground station corrections

Comparison Activity

Feature Traditional Navigation Modern GPS
Energy Requirements Human knowledge only Requires electricity/batteries

Integration of Knowledge Systems

Wayfinding Challenge

Scenario: You're navigating from Auckland to Rarotonga (approx. 3,300km). Create a hybrid navigation plan using:

  1. 3 traditional Māori navigation techniques
  2. 3 modern GPS/navigation technologies
  3. Explain how they complement each other

Present as an illustrated journey log.

Extension Activities

1. Star Compass Construction

Research and build a physical star compass model showing key navigation stars used in the Pacific.

2. Modern Voyaging

Investigate how modern waka hourua (voyaging canoes) like Hōkūleʻa combine traditional and modern navigation.

3. Error Analysis

Compare potential error margins in traditional vs GPS navigation over a 1000km journey.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Timing: This unit works well as a 4-6 lesson sequence:

  1. Introduction to traditional navigation (1 lesson)
  2. Modern GPS technology (1 lesson)
  3. Comparison and integration (1-2 lessons)
  4. Assessment task completion (1-2 lessons)

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide star charts and simplified GPS diagrams
  • Extension: Research Polynesian navigation beyond Māori traditions

Safety: If doing outdoor observations, follow school safety policies.

Cultural Protocol: Consider inviting a local kaumatua or navigator to speak if appropriate.
