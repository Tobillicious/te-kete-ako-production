Traditional Navigation & Modern GPS Integration

Exploring Māori wayfinding (whakatere waka) and contemporary navigation technologies

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Explain key principles of traditional Māori navigation (whakatere waka)
  • Describe how modern GPS technology works
  • Compare and contrast traditional and modern navigation methods
  • Analyze how mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge) can inform contemporary science
  • Create a navigation plan using both traditional and modern techniques

NZ Curriculum Links: Science Level 5 - Physical World (PW), Nature of Science (NOS), Planet Earth and Beyond (PEB)

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori

Traditional Māori navigation is part of a rich knowledge system called mātauranga Māori. When studying this topic:

  • Recognize that this knowledge was developed over centuries of observation and experience
  • Understand that wayfinding was (and is) both a practical skill and spiritual practice
  • Respect that some knowledge may be tapu (sacred) to certain iwi (tribes)
  • Pronounce Māori terms correctly - practice with students (e.g., whakatere = fah-kah-teh-reh)
  • Acknowledge the iwi whose rohe (territory) you're in when discussing local navigation knowledge

Key concept: Kaitiakitanga - the guardianship of knowledge and environment that guided traditional navigation.

Discussion: How might this compare to modern compass directions? What advantages might this system have?

Other Navigation Methods

  • Te kapehu whetū (star compass) - using rising and setting points of stars
  • Ngā tohu moana (ocean signs) - wave patterns, currents, and swells
  • Ngā manu (birds) - observing migratory patterns and bird behavior
  • Te hau (winds) - understanding seasonal wind patterns
  • Ngā kapua (clouds) - cloud formations over land

Modern GPS Technology

Global Positioning System (GPS) uses satellites to determine precise locations:

  • 24+ satellites orbiting Earth
  • Triangulation from multiple satellites
  • Accuracy within meters (sometimes centimeters)
  • Works globally in all weather conditions

Integration of Knowledge Systems

Modern navigators are combining traditional knowledge with technology:

  • Using star knowledge when electronics fail
  • Teaching traditional wayfinding alongside GPS in maritime training
  • Applying traditional environmental observations to climate change research
  • Developing navigation apps that incorporate Māori place names and knowledge

Extension Activities

1. Navigation Challenge

Plan a journey from your school to a nearby landmark using:

  • Traditional methods only (create a star chart, note environmental signs)
  • Modern methods only (GPS coordinates, digital maps)
  • A combination of both

Present your plans and compare their strengths/weaknesses.

2. Star Observation Journal

Over two weeks, observe and record:

  • Positions of 3 bright stars at the same time each night
  • Moon phases and position
  • Any visible planets
  • Weather patterns and their effect on visibility

3. Technology Debate

Prepare arguments for and against: "Traditional navigation methods are obsolete in the age of GPS."

Teacher Implementation Notes

Timing: This unit can be taught over 4-6 lessons depending on depth and extensions.

Preparation:

  • Consult with local iwi or Māori knowledge holders if possible
  • Test any digital tools beforehand (Google Earth, star map apps)
  • Prepare printed star charts for cloudy nights
  • Identify safe outdoor observation areas

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide partially completed comparison tables
  • Extension: Research specific iwi navigation traditions
  • Practical: Create simple quadrant instruments for angle measurement

Safety: For night observations, ensure proper supervision and parent notifications.
