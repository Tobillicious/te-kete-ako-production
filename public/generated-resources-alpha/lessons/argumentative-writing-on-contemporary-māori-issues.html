Argumentative Writing on Contemporary Māori Issues

Learning Objectives

  • Analyse and construct persuasive arguments on contemporary Māori issues.
  • Apply whakapapa (connections) and mana (respect) when discussing Māori perspectives.
  • Develop critical thinking by evaluating different viewpoints.
  • Write a structured argumentative essay using evidence from credible sources.

🌿 Cultural Context | Horopaki Ahurea

Whakataukī

"Whāia te iti kahurangi"

"Seek the treasure you value most dearly"

🏛️ Connection to Mangakōtukutuku College Values

Whaimana: Integrity - Standing firm in truth and principle

This learning connects to the value of whaimana by encouraging students to develop persuasive arguments grounded in truth-telling and social justice.

🛡️ Cultural Safety Considerations

  • Approach Māori content with respect and openness
  • Recognize students' diverse cultural backgrounds and experiences
  • Create space for Māori students to share their perspectives
  • Consult with whānau and local iwi for culturally sensitive topics

Cultural Context & Safety Notes

He Whakataukī (Māori Proverb): "Kia whakatōmuri te haere whakamua" – "I walk backwards into the future with my eyes fixed on the past."

Cultural Considerations:

  • Ensure discussions remain respectful of Māori perspectives.
  • Encourage students to consider Te Tiriti o Waitangi (Treaty of Waitangi) principles.
  • Use appropriate tikanga (protocols) when discussing sensitive topics.
  • Provide trigger warnings if discussing historical injustices.

Lesson Activities

1. Exploring Contemporary Māori Issues

Task: In small groups, research one of the following topics:

  • Māori representation in media
  • Land rights and whenua (land) disputes
  • The impact of colonisation on Māori health
  • Te reo Māori revitalisation in schools

Discussion: Share findings and debate different perspectives.

2. Crafting an Argument

Task: Choose a stance on your issue and draft an argument using:

  • A clear thesis statement
  • Evidence from credible sources (e.g., news articles, academic papers)
  • Counterarguments and rebuttals

3. Peer Review & Reflection

Task: Exchange essays with a partner and provide constructive feedback.

Reflection: How did your perspective evolve during this process?

Assessment Rubric

Criteria Excellent (5) Proficient (3-4) Developing (1-2)
Argument Structure Clear thesis, strong evidence, logical flow Some structure, adequate evidence Weak argument, lacks coherence
Cultural Sensitivity Respectful, integrates Māori perspectives Some awareness, minor missteps Lacks cultural consideration
Critical Thinking Deep analysis, strong rebuttals Basic analysis, some counterarguments Limited analysis, weak rebuttals

Extension Activities

  • Debate: Hold a formal debate on one of the issues.
  • Multimedia Presentation: Create a video or podcast advocating for a Māori issue.
  • Guest Speaker: Invite a Māori advocate or academic to discuss their perspective.

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

  • Struggling Writers: Provide sentence starters or graphic organisers.
  • ESOL Students: Offer vocabulary lists and simplified texts.
  • Advanced Learners: Challenge them to include whakataukī (proverbs) in their arguments.
