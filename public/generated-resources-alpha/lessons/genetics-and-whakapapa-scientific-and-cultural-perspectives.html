Genetics and Whakapapa: Scientific and Cultural Perspectives

Cultural Context

This lesson explores the connections between Western scientific understandings of genetics and MƒÅori concepts of whakapapa (genealogy). We acknowledge that these are different but complementary knowledge systems, each with their own validity and importance.

Cultural Safety Note: Discussions about ancestry and genetics can be sensitive. Always approach these topics with respect. Some students may have complex family situations or may not know their biological ancestry. Emphasize that all forms of family and ancestry are valid.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain basic principles of genetic inheritance (AS91157)
  • Describe how whakapapa represents relationships and connections in Te Ao MƒÅori
  • Compare and contrast scientific and cultural understandings of ancestry
  • Analyze how DNA evidence can support or challenge traditional knowledge systems
  • Reflect on the importance of both scientific and cultural perspectives in understanding identity

Engage: Personal Connections

Whakawhanaungatanga (Building Relationships): Begin by asking students to think about how they define their own identity and ancestry. Provide options for response (written, drawn, or digital).

Questions to prompt thinking:

  • What stories have you been told about your ancestors?
  • How does your family track relationships and connections?
  • What physical or personality traits do you think you've inherited?

Elaborate: Applying Knowledge

Creative Synthesis Task

Choose one of the following options to demonstrate your understanding:

  1. Create a digital presentation comparing genetic inheritance and whakapapa
  2. Write a short story about a character who discovers something unexpected through both DNA testing and whakapapa research
  3. Design an infographic showing how scientific and cultural knowledge can complement each other
  4. Record a podcast interview with an imagined scientist and kaumatua discussing ancestry

Assessment

Students will be assessed on their ability to:

  • Accurately explain genetic inheritance patterns
  • Demonstrate understanding of whakapapa as a cultural concept
  • Compare and contrast the two knowledge systems with sensitivity
  • Apply knowledge creatively in their synthesis task

Teacher Implementation Guide

Before the Lesson:

  • Consult with your school's MƒÅori community liaison about appropriate approaches
  • Prepare alternative activities for students who may find ancestry discussions difficult
  • Test all digital resources to ensure they work on school devices

Lesson Sequence (3-4 periods):

  1. Period 1: Engage activity and introduction to genetics
  2. Period 2: Whakapapa exploration and comparison activity
  3. Period 3: Case study discussion and begin synthesis task
  4. Period 4: Complete and present synthesis tasks

Adaptations:

  • Visual learners: Provide graphic organizers for comparisons
  • ESOL students: Pre-teach key vocabulary, allow use of first language for initial discussions
  • Students with anxiety: Offer private alternatives to sharing personal family information
  • Advanced students: Provide extension research questions
