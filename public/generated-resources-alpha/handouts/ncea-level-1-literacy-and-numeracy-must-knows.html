Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori in Assessment

In preparing for assessments, we acknowledge the principles of ako (reciprocal learning) and whanaungatanga (relationships). Consider these cultural dimensions:

  • Manaakitanga: Support your peers in their learning journey
  • Wairua: Bring your whole self to learning - mind, body and spirit
  • Kaitiakitanga: Take responsibility for your own learning progress
  • Mātauranga Māori: Look for opportunities to connect learning to Māori knowledge systems

Remember that assessments are not just about individual achievement, but about contributing to the collective knowledge of your whānau, hapū and iwi.

Reading Comprehension Practice

Read this excerpt from a Māori legend:

"In the beginning, Ranginui (sky father) and Papatūānuku (earth mother) were locked in a tight embrace, leaving their children in darkness. The children debated how to separate their parents to allow light into the world..."

Question: What does this creation story explain about the Māori worldview?

This story explains the Māori understanding of the origin of light and space in the world, showing the interconnectedness of family relationships (whanaungatanga) and the natural world. It demonstrates how challenges can be overcome through collective decision-making.

Writing Activity: Whakataukī (Proverb) Reflection

Choose one of these whakataukī and write a 150-word reflection on what it means for your learning journey:

  • "Ehara taku toa i te toa takitahi, engari he toa takitini" - My strength is not as an individual, but as a collective
  • "Whāia te iti kahurangi ki te tūohu koe me he maunga teitei" - Seek the treasure you value most dearly, if you bow your head, let it be to a lofty mountain

Tips: Connect the proverb to your NCEA preparation, consider both literal and metaphorical meanings, and reflect on personal experiences.

Māori Land Calculations

A whenua (land) block is being divided among 6 whānau members. The total area is 7.2 hectares. Calculate how much land each whānau member would receive.

Solution: 7.2 hectares ÷ 6 = 1.2 hectares per whānau member

Assessment Readiness Checklist

  • I understand the requirements for each standard
  • I can identify which standards I've already completed
  • I know where to find help if I need it (teachers, peers, whānau)
  • I have practiced both literacy and numeracy skills
  • I can explain how my learning connects to my cultural identity
  • I have a study plan for upcoming assessments
  • I know the assessment dates and locations
  • I understand the appeal process if needed

Extension Activities

1. Whakapapa Research

Research your own whakapapa (genealogy) and create a family tree that includes:

  • At least three generations
  • Significant places connected to your whānau
  • Numerical data (years, ages, distances between locations)
  • A written explanation of what you discovered

2. Community Statistics Project

Investigate a statistic about your local community (e.g., sports participation, kapa haka involvement, employment rates) and:

  1. Collect data through surveys or research
  2. Present the data in at least two different formats (table, graph, etc.)
  3. Write a 200-word analysis of what the data shows
  4. Suggest ways the community could respond to the findings
