Visual Arts Analysis with Māori Aesthetic Principles

Learning Objectives

  • Understand key Māori aesthetic principles (whakapapa, mana, mauri)
  • Analyze artworks using both Western and Māori frameworks
  • Compare and contrast different cultural perspectives in art
  • Develop respectful engagement with Māori art traditions

Cultural Context & Safety Notes

He whakamārama (Explanation): Māori art is deeply connected to whakapapa (genealogy), whenua (land), and spiritual values. When analyzing Māori art, we must approach it with manaakitanga (respect) and tikanga (correct protocols).

Key Māori Aesthetic Principles

  • Whakapapa: The ancestral connections and stories behind the artwork
  • Mana: The spiritual power and prestige embodied in the art
  • Mauri: The life force present in the materials and design
  • Kōrero: The narrative or message conveyed

Art Analysis Framework

Use this framework to examine artworks through both Māori and Western lenses:

1. Formal Elements (Western Analysis)

Describe the artwork using:

  • Line, shape, color
  • Composition
  • Technique

2. Māori Aesthetic Analysis

Consider these questions:

  • What whakapapa connections might this artwork have?
  • How does it express mana or mauri?
  • What cultural stories or values does it communicate?

Assessment Rubric

Extension Activities

  • Research a local Māori artist and present their work using this framework
  • Create an artwork that blends Māori and Western aesthetic principles
  • Interview a Māori artist or kaumātua about art traditions (with guidance)

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

  • Visual Learners: Provide image-based comparison charts
  • ESOL Students: Pre-teach key vocabulary with visual aids
  • Kinesthetic Learners: Create a physical representation of an art principle

Teacher Implementation Guide

  1. Preparation: Research Māori artworks (e.g., from Te Papa collections)
  2. Introduction: Discuss the importance of cultural perspectives in art
  3. Activity: Students analyze an artwork using both frameworks
  4. Discussion: Compare findings in small groups
  5. Reflection: Journal about how culture influences interpretation

Note: Always consult with local iwi or cultural advisors when engaging with Māori content.
