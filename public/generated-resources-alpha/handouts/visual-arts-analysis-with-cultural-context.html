Visual Arts Analysis with Māori Aesthetic Principles

Learning Objectives

  • Analyse artworks using Māori aesthetic principles (Ngā Toi Māori)
  • Understand the cultural significance of whakapapa (genealogy) in Māori art
  • Compare and contrast Western and Māori approaches to visual analysis
  • Create informed interpretations of artworks with cultural respect

Cultural Context & Safety Notes

Key Concepts:

  • Mana (prestige) – Respect the spiritual and cultural significance of Māori art
  • Taonga (treasure) – Recognise that Māori artworks are often sacred
  • Kaitiakitanga (guardianship) – Approach analysis with care and responsibility

Safety Note: Always seek permission before reproducing images of Māori artworks, especially those with cultural significance (e.g., whakairo carvings).

Māori Aesthetic Principles

Māori art follows key principles that differ from Western analysis:

Principle (Kaupapa) Description
Whakapapa Connection to ancestors and stories
Mana Spiritual power and prestige
Kōrero Narrative and oral tradition
Whenua Connection to land

Step-by-Step Art Analysis

Look at this artwork: (Insert image of a Māori artwork, e.g., a kōwhaiwhai pattern or contemporary Māori painting)

  1. First Impressions: What do you notice first? How does it make you feel?
  2. Cultural Context: Research the artist’s iwi (tribe) and the artwork’s purpose.
  3. Elements & Principles: Analyse using Māori aesthetics (see table above).
  4. Compare: How would a Western analysis differ?

Assessment Suggestions

Criteria Achieved Merit Excellence
Cultural Understanding Identifies basic Māori elements Explains cultural significance Compares Māori & Western perspectives
Analysis Depth Describes visual features Links to whakapapa or mana Critically evaluates cultural meaning

Extension Activities

  • Artist Interview: Research a Māori artist (e.g., Ralph Hotere, Robyn Kahukiwa) and present their influences.
  • Create Your Own: Design an artwork using Māori principles and explain your choices.
  • Debate: "Should all art analysis include cultural context?"

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

  • Visual Learners: Use graphic organisers to compare Māori and Western analysis.
  • ESOL Students: Provide key terms in English and te reo Māori.
  • Kinesthetic Learners: Create a physical representation of an artwork’s meaning.
