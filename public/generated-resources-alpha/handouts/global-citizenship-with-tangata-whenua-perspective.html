Global Citizenship with Tangata Whenua Perspective

"He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tangata, he tangata, he tangata."
"What is the most important thing in the world? It is people, it is people, it is people."

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the concept of global citizenship from both Western and Māori perspectives
  • Explore how kaitiakitanga (guardianship) relates to global citizenship
  • Analyze contemporary global issues through a Te Ao Māori lens
  • Develop strategies for responsible global citizenship that honor indigenous worldviews
  • Connect local indigenous knowledge to global challenges

Understanding Global Citizenship

Global citizenship typically refers to the idea that all people have rights and responsibilities that extend beyond national boundaries. However, from a Tangata Whenua (people of the land) perspective, this concept has deeper dimensions.

Whakawhiti Kōrero (Discussion Starter)

In pairs, discuss: How might indigenous concepts like whanaungatanga (relationships), manaakitanga (hospitality), and kaitiakitanga (guardianship) expand our understanding of global citizenship?

Māori Perspectives on Global Belonging

Traditional Māori worldview sees all things as interconnected through whakapapa (genealogy). This creates a different foundation for global citizenship:

Western Concept Māori Concept Global Implications
Individual rights Whanaungatanga (collective responsibility) Emphasizes our obligations to others worldwide
Environmental management Kaitiakitanga (guardianship) Sees humans as caretakers rather than owners of nature
Cultural diversity Mana motuhake (self-determination) Supports indigenous rights globally while maintaining unity

Case Study: Climate Change

Read this excerpt from a Māori elder about climate change:

"Our ancestors taught us that when the land is sick, the people are sick. Climate change isn't just about carbon levels - it's about broken relationships with Papatūānuku (Earth Mother)."

Discussion: How does this perspective differ from mainstream climate change discussions? What solutions might emerge from this worldview?

Becoming a Global Citizen with Māori Values

Practical ways to integrate Māori values into global citizenship:

  1. Think intergenerationally: Consider how your actions today will affect seven generations ahead
  2. Practice manaakitanga: Extend hospitality and care beyond your immediate community
  3. Honor indigenous knowledge: Seek out and respect indigenous perspectives on global issues
  4. Act locally, think globally: Apply Māori environmental principles to global challenges

Action Project

Design a global citizenship initiative that incorporates at least two Māori values. Your project should:

  • Address a real-world issue
  • Show how Māori values provide solutions
  • Include practical action steps
  • Consider impacts on indigenous communities

Reflection Questions

  • How has your understanding of global citizenship changed after considering Māori perspectives?
  • What responsibilities do we have toward indigenous peoples in global citizenship discussions?
  • How can we ensure indigenous voices are heard in global decision-making?
