Global Citizenship with Tangata Whenua Perspective

Whakataukī: "He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tangata, he tangata, he tangata."

"What is the most important thing in the world? It is people, it is people, it is people."

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the concept of global citizenship from both Western and Māori perspectives
  • Explore how Te Ao Māori concepts like whanaungatanga and kaitiakitanga relate to global citizenship
  • Analyze contemporary global issues through a Tangata Whenua lens
  • Develop strategies for responsible global citizenship that honor Te Tiriti o Waitangi
  • Create action plans for local-global connections that reflect Māori values

Cultural Context and Safety Notes

Cultural Safety: This resource explores sensitive topics including colonization, indigenous rights, and environmental justice. Ensure:

  • Create a safe space for sharing diverse perspectives
  • Respect all cultural viewpoints while centering Māori perspectives
  • Be aware of students' personal connections to these issues
  • Follow school protocols for discussing sensitive topics
  • Invite Māori students to share only if they choose to

Understanding Global Citizenship

Global citizenship typically refers to recognizing our interconnectedness with people and environments worldwide. From a Tangata Whenua perspective, this connects deeply with concepts like:

  • Whakapapa - Our genealogical connections to all living things
  • Kaitiakitanga - Guardianship of our environment
  • Manaakitanga - Hospitality and care for others
  • Tino rangatiratanga - Self-determination

Whakapapa Connections Activity

Create a visual representation showing how you're connected to:

  1. Your immediate whānau
  2. Your local community
  3. New Zealand as a whole
  4. The Pacific region
  5. The global community

Consider: How do these connections create responsibilities?

Case Studies: Global Issues Through a Māori Lens

1. Climate Change and Kaitiakitanga

Compare Western environmentalism with Māori concepts of guardianship. How might solutions differ?

2. Indigenous Rights and Tino Rangatiratanga

Research how Māori perspectives on sovereignty compare with other indigenous movements worldwide.

Group Discussion: Global Challenges, Local Solutions

In small groups, select one global issue (e.g., plastic pollution, inequality, cultural preservation).

Develop solutions that:

  1. Use Western scientific approaches
  2. Incorporate Māori knowledge systems
  3. Combine both perspectives

Present your ideas to the class.

Assessment

Option 1: Research Project (Years 11-13)

Investigate a global issue through both mainstream and Māori perspectives. Present findings in a format of your choice (essay, digital presentation, video documentary).

Option 2: Action Plan (All Year Levels)

Create a practical plan for addressing a global issue locally, incorporating Māori values. Include:

  • Issue identification
  • Māori values connection
  • Proposed actions
  • Potential partners (e.g., local iwi, community groups)
  • Evaluation methods

Teacher Implementation Guide

Lesson Sequence (Suggested 4-6 sessions)

Session 1: Introduce concepts of global citizenship and Māori perspectives

Session 2: Case study analysis in small groups

Session 3: Fieldwork or guest speaker (e.g., local iwi representative)

Session 4: Action plan development

Session 5-6: Presentations and reflection
