Te Reo Maths Glossary

Key Mathematical Terms in Te Reo Māori and English

Years 7-13 | Mathematics

Learning Objectives

By using this resource, ākonga (students) will:

  • Develop mathematical literacy in both Te Reo Māori and English
  • Understand key mathematical concepts through bilingual terminology
  • Make connections between mathematical ideas and Te Ao Māori perspectives
  • Build confidence in using Te Reo Māori in academic contexts
  • Recognize the value of indigenous knowledge in mathematics

Cultural Context

This resource acknowledges that mathematics has always existed in Te Ao Māori, with sophisticated mathematical concepts embedded in traditional practices like whakairo (carving), raranga (weaving), and celestial navigation.

Tikanga Considerations

  • Whanaungatanga: Encourage students to work collaboratively when learning terms
  • Ako: Create reciprocal learning opportunities where students can teach each other
  • Manaakitanga: Foster a supportive environment for language learners
  • Wairua: Connect mathematical concepts to Māori worldviews where appropriate

Note: Some terms are transliterations (e.g., "parabola") while others are conceptual translations (e.g., "ture" for formula). Both approaches are valid in developing academic Māori.

Tatau
Number
The abstract concept used to count, measure, and label. In Māori tradition, counting systems were used for practical purposes like tracking lunar cycles.
Ture
Formula
A mathematical relationship or rule expressed in symbols. The word "ture" also means law, reflecting the unchanging nature of mathematical truths.
Whakarea
Multiplication
Repeated addition. The prefix "whaka-" indicates causation, turning "rea" (spread) into "make spread" - conceptually accurate for multiplication.
Wehe
Division
Splitting into equal parts. The term reflects the action of separating or partitioning quantities.
Tāpiri
Addition
Combining quantities. The term comes from "piri" meaning to join or adhere.
Tangohanga
Subtraction
Removing a quantity from another. From "tango" meaning to take or remove.
Āhua
Shape
The form of an object. In Māori worldview, shapes carry cultural significance (e.g., koru pattern).
Paerangi
Graph
A visual representation of data. "Pae" means horizon and "rangi" means sky, suggesting the broad view a graph provides.
Pāngarau
Mathematics
The study of numbers, patterns, and relationships. "Pānga" means to relate or connect, while "rau" means many.
Taurangi
Variable
A symbol representing a changing quantity. "Tau" means number and "rangi" means to arrange, reflecting its changing nature.
Rōnaki
Equation
A statement that two expressions are equal. "Rō" means to extend and "naki" suggests balance.
Pārōnaki
Inequality
A statement that two expressions are not equal. "Pā" can mean to touch or affect, modifying the equality concept.
Pūrua
Fraction
A part of a whole. "Pū" means base and "rua" means two, originally describing halves.
Taurite
Ratio
A comparison of two quantities. "Tau" means number and "rite" means alike or similar.
Paenga
Perimeter
The distance around a shape. "Pae" means boundary and "nga" is a plural marker.
Horahanga
Area
The space inside a shape. From "hora" meaning to spread out.
Pou
Axis
A reference line in a coordinate system. The term relates to the posts in a wharenui that provide structure.
Tikanga tau
Number pattern
A sequence that follows a rule. "Tikanga" means method or protocol, showing the ordered nature.
Kokona
Angle
The space between intersecting lines. The term relates to corners or bends.
Ira
Digit
A single symbol used in numerals. Also means "gene," showing the fundamental nature.

Self-Checklist for Students

  • I can match at least 10 Māori maths terms with their English equivalents
  • I can use 5 Māori terms correctly in my maths work this week
  • I can explain one way Māori culture connects to mathematical concepts
  • I have helped a classmate learn at least one new term

Extension Activities

1. Term Investigation

Choose 3 terms from the glossary and research:

  • Their etymology (word origins)
  • How the Māori term conceptually relates to the mathematical idea
  • Any traditional Māori knowledge that connects to the concept

2. Create Your Own Glossary Entry

Select a mathematical term not in this glossary and:

  1. Research if there's an existing Māori translation
  2. If none exists, work with your teacher to develop a culturally appropriate term
  3. Create a glossary card with definition and cultural connections

3. Bilingual Problem Solving

Write and solve 3 word problems that:

  • Use Māori mathematical terms appropriately
  • Incorporate Māori cultural contexts (e.g., weaving patterns, star navigation)
  • Include both languages in the problem statement

Teacher Implementation Notes

Suggested Approaches

  • Gradual Integration: Introduce 3-5 terms per week rather than all at once
  • Word Walls: Create a classroom display with terms grouped by topic
  • Peer Teaching: Have students teach terms to each other in pairs
  • Contextual Use: Model using terms naturally during lessons

Differentiation Strategies

  • Support: Provide visual cues and bilingual word banks
  • Extension: Challenge students to find terms beyond this glossary
  • Engagement: Incorporate games like bilingual maths bingo

Assessment Ideas

  • Include bilingual terms in regular vocabulary quizzes
  • Have students maintain a maths vocabulary journal
  • Observe natural use of terms during problem-solving activities
  • Create matching or translation activities
