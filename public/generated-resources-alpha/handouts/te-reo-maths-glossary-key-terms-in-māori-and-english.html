Ngā Whāinga Ako | Learning Objectives

By using this resource, ākonga (students) will:

  • Develop bilingual mathematical vocabulary in Te Reo Māori and English
  • Understand key mathematical concepts through both cultural lenses
  • Apply mathematical terms correctly in problem-solving contexts
  • Appreciate the connections between mātauranga Māori and mathematics
  • Build confidence using Te Reo Māori in academic contexts

Horopaki Ahurea | Cultural Context

Mathematics has always been present in Māori culture through:

  • Whakapapa (genealogical patterns and counting systems)
  • Tātai (Māori astronomy and navigation calculations)
  • Whakairo (carving geometric patterns and symmetry)
  • Raranga (weaving mathematical patterns)

Tikanga Considerations for Teachers

  • Pronounce Māori words correctly - practice with students or use online pronunciation guides
  • Encourage students to use terms in context, not just memorization
  • Highlight how mathematical concepts appear in te ao Māori (Māori world)
  • Invite local kaumātua or Māori mathematicians to share their perspectives
  • Create a supportive environment where students feel safe to practice Te Reo

Ngā Ngohe Anō | Extension Activities

Te Reo Maths Bingo

Create bingo cards with mathematical terms in Te Reo Māori. Call out definitions in English or show mathematical symbols. Students mark the corresponding Te Reo term.

Whakapapa of Numbers

Research traditional Māori counting systems and create a presentation comparing them to modern mathematical concepts. Include how different iwi counted objects.

Mathematical Whakataukī

Analyze Māori proverbs that contain mathematical concepts (e.g., "Ka pu te ruha, ka hao te rangatahi" - as the old net is cast aside, the new net goes fishing). Discuss the mathematical metaphors.

Bilingual Problem Solving

Create word problems that incorporate both languages. Students must solve the problems while correctly using terminology from both languages in their solutions.

Ngā Tohutohu mā te Kaiako | Teacher Implementation Notes

Implementation Strategies

  • Gradual Introduction: Add 2-3 new Te Reo terms each week rather than overwhelming students
  • Word Walls: Create classroom displays with terms and visual representations
  • Peer Teaching: Have students teach terms to each other in pairs
  • Contextual Learning: Always teach terms within mathematical problems, not in isolation
  • Cultural Experts: Invite Māori elders or mathematicians to discuss traditional knowledge systems

Differentiation

For beginners: Focus on basic number and operation terms first

For intermediate: Add geometry and measurement terms

For advanced: Include algebra and statistics terms

Cross-Curricular Links

  • Te Reo Māori: Coordinate with language teachers to reinforce vocabulary
  • Social Studies: Explore traditional Māori knowledge systems
  • Art: Study geometric patterns in Māori art forms
