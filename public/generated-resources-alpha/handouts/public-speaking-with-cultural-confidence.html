    Public Speaking with Cultural Confidence

    Kaupapa (Purpose): Develop public speaking skills while grounding your voice in cultural identity.

    🌿 Cultural Context | Horopaki Ahurea

    Whakataukī

    "Ko te akoranga te pūtake o te ora"

    "Education is the foundation of wellbeing"

    🏛️ Connection to Mangakōtukutuku College Values

    Whaiora: Wellbeing - Caring for collective health and prosperity

    This learning connects to the value of whaiora by encouraging students to [engage with content in ways that embody this value].

    🛡️ Cultural Safety Considerations

    • Approach Māori content with respect and openness
    • Recognize students' diverse cultural backgrounds and experiences
    • Create space for Māori students to share their perspectives
    • Consult with whānau and local iwi for culturally sensitive topics

    Learning Objectives

    • Deliver a structured speech with confidence (NZC English: Speaking, Writing, Presenting)
    • Incorporate elements of whakapapa (identity) and pepeha (introduction) into speeches
    • Use vocal techniques to engage an audience
    • Respect cultural protocols in public speaking

    Cultural Context & Safety Notes

    He Whakamārama (Explanation):

    In te ao Māori, speaking confidently (kōrero ā-manawa) connects to identity and community. We acknowledge:

    • Not all students will share the same cultural background—allow choice in how they present themselves.
    • Always ask permission before sharing someone else's pepeha or story.
    • Provide alternatives for students uncomfortable with public speaking (e.g., pre-recorded speeches).

    Step-by-Step Activity

    1. Whakatau (Introduction)

    Watch: "How to Deliver a Pepeha" (3 min). Discuss what makes the speaker engaging.

    2. Voice & Body Workshop

    Interactive Exercise: In pairs, practice:

    • Projecting your voice (stand 2m apart, speak clearly)
    • Using hand gestures naturally
    • Eye contact with 3 different audience members

    3. Speech Structure

    Use this framework (adapt as needed):

    1. Opening: Greeting in your heritage language OR mihi
    2. Story: Share a personal/cultural experience (2 min)
    3. Message: What did you learn? Why does it matter?
    4. Closing: Thank audience in a meaningful way

    4. Practice & Feedback

    In small groups, give "2 stars and 1 wish" feedback (2 positives, 1 improvement).

    Assessment Suggestions

    Criteria Achieved (✓) Notes
    Clear structure (opening, body, closing)
    Authentic cultural connection
    Voice projection & engagement

    Extension Activities

    • Debate Challenge: Organize a wānanga (discussion) on a local issue using formal speech structures.
    • Digital Storytelling: Create a video speech combining visuals and voice.
    • Community Speakers: Interview a kaumatua (elder) about traditional oratory and present findings.

    Adaptations

    • ESOL/EAL: Allow use of first language with English subtitles/keyword glossary.
    • Anxiety Support: Present to teacher only or use Flipgrid for recorded submissions.
    • Visual Aids: Encourage slides or props to supplement speaking.

    Teacher Notes

    Timing: 3–4 lessons (prep, practice, present).

    Resources Needed: Chromebooks for research, printed feedback sheets, open speaking space.

    Tikanga (Protocols): Begin with a karakia (prayer) or moment of reflection to set the tone.
