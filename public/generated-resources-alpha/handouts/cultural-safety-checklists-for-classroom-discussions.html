Cultural Safety Checklists for Classroom Discussions

Creating safe learning environments for Māori students

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Understand the concept of cultural safety in educational settings
  • Identify key elements that contribute to culturally safe classrooms
  • Apply cultural safety principles to classroom discussions
  • Reflect on personal and collective responsibility in maintaining cultural safety
  • Develop strategies to address cultural safety concerns

Cultural Context

Tikanga Māori Considerations

Cultural safety (ahurea haumaru) in the classroom aligns with these Māori concepts:

  • Whanaungatanga - Building relationships and connections
  • Manaakitanga - Showing respect, hospitality and care
  • Ako - Reciprocal teaching and learning
  • Wairuatanga - Spiritual dimension of learning
  • Kotahitanga - Unity and collective responsibility

When discussing cultural safety, we must remember that for Māori, the classroom is not separate from the wider world view - it is part of a holistic approach to education that values identity, language and culture.

Cultural Safety Checklist

Use this checklist before, during and after sensitive classroom discussions to ensure cultural safety for Māori students:

Before the Discussion

I have considered the cultural backgrounds of all students in the class
I have prepared resources that represent diverse perspectives
I have established classroom norms for respectful discussion
I have identified potential cultural sensitivities in the topic
I have considered how to make the space physically and emotionally safe

During the Discussion

I am using inclusive language that respects all cultures
I am monitoring student reactions for signs of discomfort
I am ensuring all voices have equal opportunity to participate
I am addressing any culturally insensitive comments immediately
I am making space for Māori perspectives without tokenism

After the Discussion

I have provided opportunities for private feedback
I have reflected on what worked well and what could improve
I have followed up with any students who seemed affected
I have considered how to apply learnings to future discussions
I have acknowledged the contributions of all participants

Scenario Practice

Read each scenario and consider how you would respond to maintain cultural safety:

Scenario 1

During a discussion about New Zealand history, a student says "Māori get too many special privileges these days." Several Māori students look uncomfortable.

Suggested Response: Acknowledge the comment without agreeing: "That's an interesting perspective. Let's examine the historical context of Treaty settlements and why they exist." Then invite a Māori student to share their perspective only if they volunteer.

Scenario 2

A group is working on a project about family traditions. Some Māori students seem reluctant to share details about their whānau.

Suggested Response: Respect their privacy: "It's okay to share only what you're comfortable with. Some aspects of whānau may be tapu (sacred). You could talk generally about traditions if you prefer."

Extension Activities

1. Cultural Safety Audit

In small groups, audit your classroom environment considering:

  • Physical space (displays, seating arrangements)
  • Resources (books, posters, digital content)
  • Classroom routines and rituals
  • Communication styles

Present your findings with recommendations for improvement.

2. Whakataukī (Proverb) Analysis

Research these whakataukī and discuss how they relate to cultural safety:

  • "He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tangata, he tangata, he tangata" (What is the most important thing in the world? It is people, it is people, it is people)
  • "Ehara taku toa i te toa takitahi, engari he toa takitini" (My strength is not as an individual, but as a collective)

Teacher Implementation Notes

Preparation: Familiarize yourself with the cultural backgrounds of your students. Consult with Māori colleagues or community members if unsure about cultural protocols.

Delivery: Introduce the concept of cultural safety by relating it to students' own experiences of feeling safe/unsafe. Use the checklists as living documents that evolve with your class.

Differentiation: For younger students (Years 7-8), focus on concrete examples and role-play. For senior students, explore more complex scenarios and systemic issues.

Sensitive Topics: Always provide opt-out options for activities that might involve sharing personal cultural information. Never put Māori students on the spot as "representatives" of their culture.
