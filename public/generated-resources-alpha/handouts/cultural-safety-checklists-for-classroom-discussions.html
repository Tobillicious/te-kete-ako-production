Creating Safe Learning Environments for Māori Students

Learning Objectives

By using this resource, students will:

  • Understand the concept of cultural safety in learning environments
  • Identify behaviors that create culturally safe spaces for Māori students
  • Develop skills for respectful cross-cultural communication
  • Apply tikanga Māori principles in classroom discussions
  • Reflect on their own cultural awareness and responsiveness

NZ Curriculum Links: Social Sciences L4-8, Key Competencies (Relating to Others, Participating and Contributing)

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori Considerations

When creating culturally safe spaces, these tikanga (Māori cultural practices) are particularly important:

  • Manaakitanga - Showing respect, hospitality and care for others
  • Whanaungatanga - Building and maintaining relationships
  • Wairuatanga - Recognizing spiritual dimensions of learning
  • Kotahitanga - Unity and collective responsibility
  • Ako - Reciprocal teaching and learning relationships

Remember that cultural safety is determined by the recipient, not the giver. What feels safe for one student may not for another.

Cultural Safety Checklist for Discussions

Use this checklist before, during and after classroom discussions to ensure cultural safety for Māori students:

Before the Discussion

Have I created opportunities for Māori students to contribute in ways that align with their cultural preferences?
Have I considered the cultural backgrounds of all participants in planning this discussion?
Are the discussion topics presented in ways that value Māori knowledge systems?
Have I established clear protocols for respectful discussion?

During the Discussion

Am I pronouncing Māori words and names correctly?
Am I allowing adequate wait time for responses (especially important in Māori communication styles)?
Am I validating Māori perspectives as equally valuable to other viewpoints?
Am I monitoring for and addressing any microaggressions immediately?
Am I encouraging diverse participation without putting individuals on the spot?

After the Discussion

Have I provided opportunities for private feedback about the cultural safety of the discussion?
Have I reflected on my own cultural biases that may have emerged during the discussion?
Have I followed up appropriately if any cultural safety concerns were raised?

Reflection Activity

Think about a recent classroom discussion. How many of these cultural safety elements were present?

Reflection questions:

  • What aspects of cultural safety do you feel most confident about?
  • Which areas need more attention in your classroom?
  • How might you adapt future discussions based on this reflection?

Extension Activities

Create a student version of this checklist with your class
Interview Māori community members about their experiences of cultural safety in education
Research and compare cultural safety approaches from other indigenous cultures
Develop a class treaty for culturally safe discussions
Create a "cultural safety moments" journal to record and reflect on observations

Implementation Notes for Teachers

Preparation: Familiarize yourself with the school's Māori Achievement Plan and any relevant policies. Consult with your school's Māori liaison or cultural advisor.

Introducing the Resource: Begin by discussing why cultural safety matters. Share the concept that "cultural safety is determined by the recipient."

Ongoing Use: Laminate checklists for regular reference. Build reflection time into your planning routine.

Student Voice: Regularly seek feedback from Māori students about their experience of cultural safety (ensure anonymous options).

Professional Learning: Use this resource for self-assessment and goal-setting in your teaching practice.
