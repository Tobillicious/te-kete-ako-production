Cultural Safety Checklists for Classroom Discussions

Subject: Social Sciences | Year Levels: 7–13

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the importance of cultural safety in classroom discussions.
  • Apply culturally responsive practices when engaging in group discussions.
  • Reflect on personal and collective responsibility in maintaining a safe learning environment.
  • Develop strategies to support Māori and other diverse learners in discussions.

Cultural Context & Safety Notes

This resource is designed to foster a mana-enhancing environment where all students, particularly Māori learners, feel valued and respected. Key considerations:

  • Whanaungatanga (relationships) – Build trust before discussing sensitive topics.
  • Manaakitanga (care and respect) – Ensure discussions uplift, not diminish, others.
  • Wairuatanga (spiritual well-being) – Be mindful of students' emotional and cultural connections.

Note: Always check with local iwi or kaumātua if discussing topics related to specific Māori histories or practices.

Cultural Safety Checklist for Students

Before the Discussion:

  • ✅ Have we established group norms (tikanga) for respectful dialogue?
  • ✅ Do I understand the cultural significance of the topic?
  • ✅ Am I prepared to listen actively (whakarongo) before speaking?

During the Discussion:

  • ✅ Am I speaking in a way that respects others' perspectives (mana)?
  • ✅ Am I avoiding stereotypes or assumptions about cultures?
  • ✅ If unsure, do I ask respectful questions (pātai) instead of making statements?

After the Discussion:

  • ✅ Did I reflect on how my words may have impacted others?
  • ✅ Did I acknowledge others' contributions (mihi)?
  • ✅ Do I need to follow up with anyone to ensure they feel safe?

Reflection Activity: Think-Pair-Share

Prompt: "Describe a time when you felt culturally safe (or unsafe) in a discussion. What made it that way?"

  1. Think individually (1 min).
  2. Share with a partner (2 min).
  3. Volunteers share with the class.

Teacher Implementation Guide

Step 1: Introduce Cultural Safety (10 min)

Discuss why cultural safety matters, using local examples (e.g., school values, Treaty of Waitangi principles).

Step 2: Co-Create Group Norms (15 min)

Have students suggest and agree on discussion guidelines (e.g., "We listen without interrupting").

Step 3: Practice with Case Studies (20 min)

Use scenarios (e.g., debating a Māori land issue) to apply the checklist.

Step 4: Reflect & Debrief (10 min)

Ask: "What worked well? What could we improve next time?"

Extension Activities

  • Research Project: Compare cultural safety protocols in NZ schools vs. indigenous education models overseas.
  • Student-Led Workshop: Have advanced learners design and facilitate a cultural safety session for younger students.
  • Creative Response: Create a poster, podcast, or video advocating for cultural safety in schools.

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

  • ESOL/EAL: Provide translated checklists or visual aids (e.g., emoji ratings for comfort levels).
  • Neurodiverse: Offer written reflection options instead of verbal sharing.
  • Reluctant Participants: Allow anonymous feedback via digital tools (e.g., Google Forms).
