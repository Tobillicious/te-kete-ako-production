Creating Safe Learning Environments for Māori Students

Learning Objectives

By engaging with this resource, students will:

  • Understand the concept of cultural safety in learning environments
  • Develop skills to create and maintain culturally safe spaces
  • Recognize the importance of tikanga Māori in classroom discussions
  • Apply cultural safety principles in group work and discussions
  • Reflect on personal and collective responsibility for cultural safety

Cultural Context: Tikanga Considerations

Cultural safety (ahurea haumaru) in the classroom requires understanding and respecting Māori ways of being, knowing and doing. Key concepts include:

  • Manaakitanga: Showing respect, hospitality and care for others
  • Whanaungatanga: Building and maintaining relationships
  • Wairuatanga: Recognizing spiritual dimensions of learning
  • Kotahitanga: Working together in unity
  • Ako: Reciprocal teaching and learning

When facilitating discussions about Māori culture or with Māori participants, consider:

  • Appropriate karakia (prayers) to open and close discussions
  • Ensuring Māori voices are centered in discussions about Māori matters
  • Providing time for whakawhanaungatanga (relationship building)
  • Being aware of tapu (sacred restrictions) and noa (ordinary) spaces

Cultural Safety Checklists

Before Discussion Checklist

I have considered the cultural backgrounds of all participants
I have prepared appropriate protocols (e.g., karakia, mihi)
The physical space is welcoming and inclusive
I have consulted with Māori colleagues/community if discussing sensitive topics
Learning materials represent diverse Māori perspectives

During Discussion Checklist

Māori participants feel comfortable to share or pass
Non-Māori participants listen more than they speak
The discussion maintains respect for Māori knowledge systems
Participants use correct te reo Māori pronunciation
Challenging views are expressed respectfully

After Discussion Checklist

Participants have opportunity for private reflection
Feedback is gathered about cultural safety
Next steps acknowledge Māori perspectives
Appropriate closing protocols are observed
Follow-up support is offered if sensitive topics arose

Extension Activities

1. Cultural Safety Scenario Analysis

In small groups, analyze these scenarios and develop culturally safe responses:

  • A non-Māori student dismisses a Māori perspective as "not scientific"
  • A class discussion about colonization becomes emotionally charged
  • A student mispronounces Māori words repeatedly despite corrections

2. Whakawhanaungatanga Practice

Practice relationship-building by:

  • Learning and sharing pepeha (personal introductions)
  • Interviewing classmates about their cultural backgrounds
  • Creating class norms for cultural safety together

3. Cultural Safety Audit

Assess another classroom or school space for cultural safety indicators:

  • Visual representation of Māori culture
  • Accessibility of te reo Māori resources
  • Evidence of tikanga in daily practice

Teacher Implementation Notes

Preparation: Before using these checklists, ensure you have basic knowledge of tikanga Māori relevant to your school's local iwi. Consult with your school's Māori community liaison if available.

Differentiation: Adapt checklists for different year levels by simplifying language for junior students or adding complexity for senior students.

Sensitive Topics: When discussing potentially sensitive cultural topics, always provide opt-out options and follow-up support.

Ongoing Practice: Cultural safety is developed over time - revisit these checklists regularly rather than treating as one-off activities.

Student Leadership: Empower senior students to facilitate cultural safety check-ins with younger students.
