Creating safe learning environments for Māori students (Years 7-13)

Learning Objectives

By engaging with this resource, students and teachers will:

  • Understand the concept of manaakitanga (care and respect) in learning environments
  • Develop skills to create culturally safe spaces for discussions
  • Recognize and apply tikanga Māori (Māori protocols) in classroom settings
  • Reflect on personal and collective responsibilities in maintaining cultural safety
  • Evaluate discussions using cultural safety criteria

Cultural Context: Tikanga Considerations

Cultural safety in education is grounded in these key Māori concepts:

  • Whanaungatanga - Building relationships and connections
  • Manaakitanga - Showing care, respect and hospitality
  • Wairuatanga - Spiritual dimension of learning
  • Kotahitanga - Unity and collective responsibility
  • Ako - Reciprocal teaching and learning

These principles should guide all classroom discussions involving Māori content or Māori students.

Extension Activities

1. Cultural Safety Scenario Analysis

In small groups, analyze these scenarios and develop culturally safe responses:

  • A Pākehā student dismisses a Māori perspective as "not relevant today"
  • A discussion about colonization becomes emotionally charged
  • A Māori student is consistently asked to "represent the Māori view"

2. Create Your Own Protocols

Develop a set of class discussion protocols that incorporate tikanga Māori principles.

3. Reflection Journal

Keep a journal documenting:

  • Times you felt culturally safe/unsafe in discussions
  • How you contributed to others' cultural safety
  • Ideas for improving classroom cultural safety

Teacher Implementation Notes

Before Use

  • Consult with your school's Māori community or kaumatua (elders) for local protocols
  • Consider your own cultural position and biases
  • Prepare students with foundational knowledge of Māori concepts

During Use

  • Model vulnerability by sharing your own learning journey
  • Be prepared to pause discussions if cultural safety is compromised
  • Use the checklists as teaching tools, not just evaluation

After Use

  • Seek feedback from students and colleagues
  • Reflect on your facilitation and adjust approaches
  • Celebrate successes in creating safer spaces
