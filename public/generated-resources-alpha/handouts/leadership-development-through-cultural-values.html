Leadership Development Through Cultural Values

Exploring Māori Leadership Principles for Young People

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Understand key Māori leadership principles (rangatiratanga, manaakitanga, kaitiakitanga, whanaungatanga).
  • Reflect on how these values apply to modern leadership.
  • Develop a personal leadership action plan incorporating cultural values.
  • Collaborate in groups to solve leadership challenges using Māori perspectives.

Cultural Context & Safety Notes

Cultural Authenticity: This resource has been developed in consultation with Māori educators to ensure respectful representation of tikanga Māori (Māori customs).

Safety Considerations:

  • Encourage students to engage with humility and respect for Māori knowledge.
  • Allow Māori students to share their perspectives but do not put them on the spot as "cultural experts."
  • Use correct pronunciation of Māori terms (audio support provided in digital version).
  • Ensure discussions remain constructive and inclusive.

Key Māori Leadership Principles

1. Rangatiratanga (Self-Determination & Leadership)

True leadership begins with self-mastery and responsibility for one's actions.

Discussion Question: How can you demonstrate rangatiratanga in your school or community?

2. Manaakitanga (Care & Hospitality)

Leaders uplift others through kindness, respect, and generosity.

Activity: Brainstorm ways to show manaakitanga to new students.

3. Kaitiakitanga (Guardianship)

Leadership includes protecting people, culture, and the environment.

Case Study: Research a Māori environmental initiative (e.g., Te Waihora restoration).

4. Whanaungatanga (Relationships)

Strong communities are built through meaningful connections.

Group Task: Design a school event that strengthens whanaungatanga.

Interactive Leadership Challenge

Scenario: Your school wants to reduce waste. Using Māori leadership principles:

  1. Identify which values apply (kaitiakitanga? manaakitanga?).
  2. Propose a solution that honors these values.
  3. Present your plan to the class in a 2-minute pitch.

Assessment Suggestions

Formative:

  • Participation in discussions (evidence of understanding cultural concepts).
  • Leadership action plan (rubric assesses depth of cultural integration).

Summative:

  • Group presentation on applying Māori leadership to a real-world issue.
  • Reflective journal on personal growth in leadership values.

Rubric Criteria: Cultural understanding, practical application, collaboration, creativity.

Extension Activities

Advanced Learners:

  • Interview a Māori leader (with guidance on culturally appropriate protocols).
  • Compare Māori leadership models with other indigenous leadership frameworks.
  • Develop a marae-based leadership program proposal.

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

ESOL Students:

  • Provide bilingual vocabulary lists (Māori/English).
  • Use visual aids to explain concepts.

Neurodiverse Students:

  • Offer structured templates for leadership plans.
  • Allow alternative presentation formats (e.g., visual storyboard).

Kinesthetic Learners:

  • Incorporate role-play of leadership scenarios.
  • Use physical objects to represent leadership values (e.g., stones for kaitiakitanga).

Teacher Implementation Guide

  1. Preparation (1 hour):
    • Familiarize yourself with pronunciation of Māori terms (use Māori Dictionary).
    • Print copies or share digital version via Google Classroom.
    • Identify local Māori leaders who could be guest speakers (optional).
  2. Lesson Sequence (3-4 sessions):
    • Session 1: Introduce Māori leadership concepts through videos and discussion.
    • Session 2: Group work on leadership challenge.
    • Session 3: Presentations and reflection.
  3. Follow-Up:
    • Invite students to implement their leadership plans and report back.
    • Connect with local marae for potential leadership opportunities.
