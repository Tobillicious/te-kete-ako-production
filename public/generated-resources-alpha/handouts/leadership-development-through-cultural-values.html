Leadership Development Through Cultural Values

Exploring MƒÅori Leadership Principles for Young People

Years 10-13 | Cross-curricular Resource

Introduction

This resource explores leadership through the lens of MƒÅori cultural values. Traditional MƒÅori leadership was not about individual power but about collective wellbeing, service, and maintaining balance within the community. By understanding these principles, we can develop leadership approaches that are inclusive, culturally responsive, and focused on the common good.

Teacher Notes

Cultural Context: MƒÅori leadership traditionally emphasizes collective responsibility, service, and maintaining balance (equilibrium) within the community. Leaders were expected to demonstrate humility, wisdom, and a deep connection to their people and environment.

Cultural Safety: Ensure students understand that these concepts are living traditions, not historical artifacts. Invite students to share their own cultural perspectives on leadership. Be mindful that some students may have personal connections to these concepts.

Learning Objectives

By engaging with this resource, students will be able to:

  • Identify and explain key MƒÅori leadership values and principles
  • Compare and contrast traditional MƒÅori leadership with contemporary leadership models
  • Apply MƒÅori leadership principles to real-world scenarios
  • Reflect on their own leadership strengths and areas for development
  • Develop a personal leadership philosophy incorporating cultural values

NZ Curriculum Links: Social Sciences, Health and Physical Education, English, The Arts, and Key Competencies (especially Relating to Others, Participating and Contributing)

Core MƒÅori Leadership Values

Traditional MƒÅori leadership is guided by several interconnected values that emphasize collective wellbeing and responsibility:

Whanaungatanga (Relationships)

Building and maintaining strong relationships, kinship, and connections. Leadership is about nurturing these bonds and creating a sense of belonging.

Leadership application: Prioritizing team cohesion, active listening, and creating inclusive environments.

Manaakitanga (Hospitality, Care)

Showing respect, generosity, and care for others. A leader demonstrates manaakitanga by uplifting and supporting their people.

Leadership application: Ensuring all team members feel valued, supported, and able to contribute.

Kaitiakitanga (Guardianship)

The responsibility of stewardship and protection of people, resources, and environment for future generations.

Leadership application: Making sustainable decisions, considering long-term impacts, and protecting team wellbeing.

Rangatiratanga (Self-determination)

Exercising authority with wisdom, integrity, and in the best interests of the collective.

Leadership application: Making principled decisions, empowering others, and taking responsibility for outcomes.

Kotahitanga (Unity)

Working collaboratively toward common goals, emphasizing collective action over individual achievement.

Leadership application: Building consensus, resolving conflicts, and fostering team spirit.

Wairuatanga (Spirituality)

Recognizing the spiritual dimension of leadership and decision-making, connecting to deeper purpose.

Leadership application: Leading with authenticity, integrity, and connection to values.

Interactive Activity: Leadership Self-Assessment

Reflect on your own leadership strengths and areas for growth in relation to MƒÅori leadership values:

Whanaungatanga (Relationships)

How well do you build and maintain positive relationships?




Manaakitanga (Care)

How do you show care and support for others?




Group Discussion: Leadership Scenarios

In small groups, discuss how you would apply MƒÅori leadership values to these scenarios:

  1. Your school group needs to plan a major event, but there are conflicting ideas about how to proceed.
  2. A member of your team is consistently not contributing their fair share of work.
  3. Your class has the opportunity to improve an area of the school environment.
  4. There's disagreement about how to allocate limited resources for a project.

For each scenario, consider: Which leadership values are most relevant? How would you apply them? What would be the outcome?

Implementation Guide for Teachers

Step 1: Introduction (30 minutes)

Begin with a karakia (if appropriate for your context) and introduce the concept of leadership through cultural lenses. Use the introduction section to frame the learning.

Step 2: Exploring Values (45 minutes)

Guide students through the Core MƒÅori Leadership Values section. Consider using a "jigsaw" approach where groups become experts on one value then teach others.

Step 3: Self-Assessment (30 minutes)

Students complete the interactive self-assessment and reflect on their leadership strengths and areas for development.

Step 4: Scenario Application (45 minutes)

Facilitate the group discussion activity with leadership scenarios. Encourage students to explicitly reference the MƒÅori values in their responses.

Step 5: Personal Leadership Philosophy (Homework/Extension)

Students develop their own leadership philosophy statement incorporating at least three MƒÅori values and how they will apply them.

Differentiation Strategies

For students needing support: Provide sentence starters for discussions, create value cards with visual cues, allow verbal responses instead of written.

For advanced students: Research contemporary MƒÅori leaders, analyze how these values appear in different leadership contexts, interview community leaders about their leadership philosophy.

For diverse learners: Offer multiple ways to engage with content (visual, auditory, kinesthetic), provide glossaries of key terms, use collaborative grouping strategically.
