Leadership Development Through Māori Cultural Values

"He aha te mea nui o te ao? He tāngata, he tāngata, he tāngata"

"What is the most important thing in the world? It is people, it is people, it is people."

Learning Objectives

  • Understand key Māori leadership principles and values
  • Apply cultural leadership concepts to contemporary situations
  • Develop personal leadership skills grounded in cultural values
  • Analyze how traditional leadership models can inform modern practice
  • Create a personal leadership development plan

Cultural Context

Traditional Māori leadership is based on collective wellbeing rather than individual power. Leaders were expected to demonstrate:

  • Manaakitanga - hospitality, care for others
  • Whanaungatanga - relationship building
  • Kaitiakitanga - guardianship of people and resources
  • Rangatiratanga - self-determination with responsibility
  • Wairuatanga - spiritual awareness

Reflection Activity: Leadership in Your Life

Think of someone you consider a good leader (could be a family member, teacher, coach, or community leader).

  1. What qualities make them a good leader?
  2. How do they show care for others?
  3. What responsibilities do they have?
  4. How do they build relationships?

Key Leadership Concepts

1. Manaakitanga - Leading with Care

True leadership begins with caring for others. In traditional Māori society, a leader's status was measured by their generosity.

Activity: Acts of Manaakitanga

In small groups, brainstorm ways students can show manaakitanga in:

  • The classroom
  • Sports teams
  • School leadership roles
  • Community groups

Create a poster or digital presentation showing your ideas.

2. Whanaungatanga - Relationship Building

Effective leaders build strong relationships and create connections between people.

Activity: Mapping Your Whakapapa of Influence

Create a diagram showing:

  1. People who have influenced your development
  2. People you currently influence
  3. Potential relationships you could develop

Write a short reflection on how you can strengthen these relationships.

Assessment

Leadership Development Plan

Create a personal leadership development plan that:

  • Identifies 3-5 Māori leadership values you want to develop
  • Explains why these are important to you
  • Describes specific actions you will take
  • Includes how you will measure progress
  • Reflects on potential challenges

Extension Activities

1. Comparative Leadership Study

Research and compare Māori leadership models with another indigenous leadership tradition (e.g., Pasifika, Native American, Aboriginal Australian). Create a presentation highlighting similarities and differences.

2. Community Leadership Project

Identify a need in your school or local community. Develop and implement a small project that applies Māori leadership principles. Document the process and outcomes.

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

For Students Needing Additional Support

  • Provide templates for leadership plans
  • Use visual organizers for relationship mapping
  • Offer sentence starters for reflections
  • Allow verbal rather than written responses where appropriate

For English Language Learners

  • Provide bilingual vocabulary lists
  • Use visual representations of concepts
  • Allow use of first language in initial brainstorming
  • Provide additional time for tasks

For Gifted and Talented Students

  • Encourage deeper research into specific Māori leadership models
  • Challenge to apply concepts to complex real-world scenarios
  • Opportunity to mentor younger students in leadership
  • Develop a leadership portfolio with multiple examples

Teacher Implementation Guide

Before Teaching

  1. Familiarize yourself with basic Māori leadership concepts
  2. Connect with local iwi or cultural advisors if possible
  3. Prepare all necessary materials (paper, digital tools, etc.)
  4. Consider your own positionality and cultural perspective

Lesson Sequence (Suggested 4-6 sessions)

  1. Session 1: Introduction to Māori leadership concepts through discussion and examples
  2. Session 2: Deep dive into specific values with activities
  3. Session 3: Application activities and planning
  4. Session 4: Work on leadership development plans
  5. Session 5-6: Presentations and reflections

Cultural Safety Considerations

  • Ensure all te reo Māori is used correctly
  • Create an environment where diverse perspectives are valued
  • Be aware that some students may have personal connections to these concepts
  • Allow students to engage at their own comfort level
  • Avoid oversimplifying complex cultural concepts
