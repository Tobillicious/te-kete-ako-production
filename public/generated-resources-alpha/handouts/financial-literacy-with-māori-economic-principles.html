Financial Literacy with Māori Economic Principles

Years 11-13 | Social Sciences | NZ Curriculum

Learning Objectives

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

  • Explain key Māori economic concepts and values (tikanga ōhanga)
  • Compare and contrast Māori and Western economic systems
  • Apply principles of manaakitanga (hospitality) and kaitiakitanga (guardianship) to financial decision-making
  • Analyze contemporary economic issues through a Māori worldview lens
  • Develop a personal financial plan incorporating Māori values

Cultural Context: Tikanga Ōhanga (Māori Economic Principles)

Traditional Māori economic systems were based on collective wellbeing rather than individual profit. Key concepts include:

  • Utu - Reciprocity and balance in exchanges
  • Manaakitanga - Generosity and care for others
  • Kaitiakitanga - Sustainable resource management
  • Whanaungatanga - Relationships and collective responsibility
  • Taonga tuku iho - Intergenerational wealth transfer

Tikanga Considerations: When discussing Māori economic concepts, remember these are living traditions connected to whakapapa (genealogy) and whenua (land). Approach with respect for Māori knowledge systems.

Applying Māori Economic Principles

Case Study: Kohanga Reo Financial Model

The Kohanga Reo (Māori language nests) movement combines traditional values with modern financial management:

  • Collective ownership (mana motuhake)
  • Voluntary labor (mahi aroha)
  • Intergenerational investment
  • Cultural capital as economic asset

Activity: In small groups, design a business plan for a community initiative that incorporates at least three Māori economic principles.

Extension Activities

1. Whenua Investment Analysis

Research a Māori land trust or incorporation (e.g., Wakatu Incorporation, Tainui Group Holdings). Analyze how they balance economic development with cultural values.

2. Intergenerational Wealth Project

Interview kaumātua (elders) about traditional Māori wealth concepts. Create a presentation comparing these with modern financial literacy principles.

3. Social Enterprise Design

Design a social enterprise business model that uses Māori economic principles to address a contemporary issue (e.g., housing, food security).

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge: Students should have basic understanding of financial literacy concepts (budgeting, saving, investing) before this unit.

Cultural Safety:

  • Consult with local Māori community members or kaumatua when teaching this content
  • Use correct Māori pronunciation (audio resources provided below)
  • Acknowledge the diversity of Māori perspectives on economic matters

Differentiation:

  • For less confident students: Provide sentence starters for comparison activities
  • For advanced students: Include analysis of Treaty of Waitangi economic redress

Assessment Options:

  • Written report comparing economic systems
  • Presentation on applying Māori principles to personal finance
  • Group project designing a values-based financial plan
