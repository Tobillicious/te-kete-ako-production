Financial Literacy with Māori Economic Principles

Year Levels: 11-13 | Subject: Social Sciences

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand key Māori economic concepts and values
  • Compare traditional Māori and contemporary economic systems
  • Apply principles of kaitiakitanga to financial decision-making
  • Develop culturally responsive financial literacy skills
  • Analyze case studies of Māori economic enterprises

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori in Economics

Traditional Māori economic systems were based on principles that differ significantly from Western capitalist models. Key concepts include:

  • Utu: The principle of reciprocity and balance in exchanges
  • Manaakitanga: Hospitality and care for others in economic transactions
  • Kaitiakitanga: Guardianship and sustainable management of resources
  • Whanaungatanga: Economic relationships based on kinship and community
  • Tapu and Noa: Sacred and secular aspects of resources and transactions

Cultural Considerations: When discussing Māori economic principles, it's important to:

  • Recognize the diversity of iwi perspectives
  • Respect the spiritual dimensions of economic concepts
  • Acknowledge the impacts of colonization on Māori economic systems
  • Use te reo Māori terms appropriately

Activity: Case Study Analysis

Read the following case study and answer the questions:

Case Study: A Māori fishing company allocates 10% of its profits to community development projects, even though this reduces shareholder returns. They justify this as part of their tikanga-based business model.

  1. Which Māori economic principles are demonstrated in this case?
  2. How does this approach differ from conventional business practices?
  3. What might be the long-term benefits of this approach?
  4. How could these principles be applied in other industries?

Applying Māori Economic Principles Today

Financial Decision-Making Framework

Use this framework to evaluate financial decisions through a Māori economic lens:

  1. Whakapapa: How does this decision connect to past and future generations?
  2. Kaitiakitanga: What are the environmental impacts?
  3. Manaakitanga: How does this benefit the community?
  4. Utu: Is there balance in the exchange?
  5. Tikanga: Does this align with cultural values?

Scenario Analysis

Apply the framework to these scenarios:

  • Investing in a company with strong environmental policies but lower returns
  • Choosing between a lower-paying job with cultural alignment or higher-paying corporate job
  • Deciding whether to sell ancestral land for development

Extension Activities

Research Project

Investigate a contemporary Māori business or enterprise (e.g., Wakatu Incorporation, Ngāi Tahu Holdings) and analyze:

  • How they incorporate Māori economic principles
  • Challenges in balancing cultural values and profitability
  • Their impact on Māori communities

Community Engagement

Interview a local Māori business owner or economic leader about:

  • How cultural values influence their financial decisions
  • Advice for young people about financial literacy
  • Their vision for Māori economic development

Creative Response

Create a resource (poster, video, presentation) that teaches financial literacy concepts using Māori economic principles.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Suggested Approach

  • Begin with a mihi/karakia to set the cultural context
  • Use cooperative learning strategies (mahi tahi)
  • Invite Māori community members to share perspectives where possible
  • Connect to NCEA standards in Economics, Business Studies, or Social Studies

Differentiation Strategies

  • Provide te reo Māori glossary for key terms
  • Offer case studies at varying complexity levels
  • Allow multiple formats for assessment responses
  • Provide templates for financial analysis frameworks

Assessment Opportunities

  • Case study responses
  • Financial decision analyses
  • Research project on Māori enterprises
  • Participation in discussions and activities

Glossary of Key Terms

Kaitiakitanga
Guardianship, stewardship, the obligation to care for resources
Manaakitanga
Hospitality, kindness, generosity - showing respect and care for others
Utu
The principle of reciprocity, balance, and restoration
Whanaungatanga
Relationship, kinship, sense of family connection
Tapu
Sacred, restricted, with spiritual limitations
Noa
Safe, unrestricted, free from tapu
