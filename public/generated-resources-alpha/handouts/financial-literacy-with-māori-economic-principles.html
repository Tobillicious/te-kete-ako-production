Financial Literacy with Māori Economic Principles

He Whakamōhiotanga | Introduction

This resource explores financial literacy through the lens of tikanga Māori (Māori customs) and traditional Māori economic systems. We'll examine how kaitiakitanga (guardianship), manaakitanga (hospitality), and whanaungatanga (relationships) shape economic decision-making in Te Ao Māori (the Māori world).

Quick Reflection

Before we begin, consider:

  • What does "wealth" mean to you?
  • How do your cultural values influence your financial decisions?
  • What traditional economic practices exist in your culture?

Ngā Whāinga Ako | Learning Objectives

By the end of this unit, you will be able to:

  • Explain key principles of traditional Māori economic systems
  • Compare and contrast Western and Māori approaches to wealth and resource management
  • Apply tikanga Māori to contemporary financial decision-making
  • Analyze how Māori economic values can inform sustainable business practices
  • Develop a personal financial plan incorporating Māori economic principles

Ngā Mātāpono Ōhanga Māori | Māori Economic Principles

1. Kaitiakitanga - Guardianship

The responsibility to care for resources for future generations. In financial terms, this means sustainable investing and long-term thinking.

2. Manaakitanga - Hospitality/Generosity

The obligation to share wealth with the community. This challenges individualistic notions of wealth accumulation.

3. Whanaungatanga - Relationships

Economic decisions are made considering their impact on relationships and community wellbeing.

4. Utu - Reciprocity

The concept of balance and exchange - all economic transactions should maintain equilibrium.

Case Study: Traditional Hākari (Feast)

Analyze how these economic principles are demonstrated in traditional Māori feasting practices:

  1. How does manaakitanga manifest in food distribution?
  2. What role does utu play in reciprocal feasting between hapū?
  3. How is kaitiakitanga demonstrated in food gathering/preparation?

Ngā Whakamahinga Hou | Modern Applications

Māori Business Models

Many successful Māori businesses incorporate traditional values:

  • Whānau-based enterprises: Profit supports extended family networks
  • Environmental focus: Sustainable practices reflecting kaitiakitanga
  • Community reinvestment: Profits fund marae, education, and social services

Ōhanga Whaiaro | Personal Finance

Applying Māori Principles to Your Finances

  1. Budgeting with manaakitanga: Allocate funds for community support
  2. Investing with kaitiakitanga: Choose sustainable/ethical investments
  3. Saving with whanaungatanga: Consider whānau needs in financial planning
  4. Spending with utu: Support Māori businesses and fair trade

Create Your Mahere Pūtea (Financial Plan)

Develop a personal budget that incorporates Māori economic principles:

  1. Calculate your income and essential expenses
  2. Allocate 10% for whānau/community support (manaakitanga)
  3. Identify one sustainable investment opportunity (kaitiakitanga)
  4. Plan one way to support Māori businesses (utu)

Aromātai | Assessment

Option 1: Research Report

Investigate a Māori business or organization and analyze how it incorporates traditional economic principles in its operations (800-1000 words).

Option 2: Financial Plan Presentation

Present your mahere pūtea (financial plan) to the class, explaining how Māori values inform your decisions (5-7 minute presentation).

Option 3: Debate

Participate in a structured debate: "Māori economic principles are more sustainable than Western capitalist models."

Ngā Mahi Whakawhānui | Extension Activities

1. Rūnanga Simulation

Role-play a hapū meeting making economic decisions about resource allocation, balancing modern needs with traditional values.

2. Comparative Analysis

Research economic principles from another indigenous culture and compare with Māori approaches.

3. Social Enterprise Proposal

Design a business plan for a social enterprise that embodies Māori economic values.
