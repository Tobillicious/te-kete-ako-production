Financial Literacy with Māori Economic Principles

Year Levels: 11-13 | Subject: Social Sciences

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand key Māori economic concepts and values
  • Compare traditional Māori and contemporary economic systems
  • Apply Māori economic principles to personal financial decisions
  • Analyze how Māori values influence business practices
  • Develop culturally responsive financial literacy skills

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori in Economics

Traditional Māori economic systems were based on principles that differ significantly from Western capitalism. Key concepts include:

  • Manaakitanga - Hospitality and care for others, influencing economic exchanges
  • Kaitiakitanga - Guardianship and sustainable resource management
  • Whanaungatanga - Relationship-based economic networks
  • Utu - Concept of reciprocity and balance in exchanges
  • Tapu and Noa - Sacred and ordinary aspects affecting resource use

Tikanga Considerations: When discussing Māori economic systems, remember that these are living traditions still practiced today. Approach with respect for Māori knowledge systems and recognize regional variations in practices.

Māori Economic Principles in Practice

Case Study: Traditional vs Modern

Compare these two scenarios and identify which Māori economic principles are present:

Traditional Hākari (Feast)

  • Community gathers food resources
  • Food distributed according to status and need
  • Reciprocal obligations created
  • Spiritual aspects observed

Modern Business Meeting

  • Catered lunch provided
  • Contracts negotiated
  • Profit margins discussed
  • Individual responsibilities assigned

Discussion Questions:

  1. Which Māori economic principles can you identify in each scenario?
  2. How might manaakitanga be expressed differently in each context?
  3. What aspects of utu (reciprocity) might be present in the modern business context?

Extension Activities

Research Project

Investigate a contemporary Māori business or enterprise (e.g., Tainui Group Holdings, Ngāi Tahu Holdings). Analyze how traditional economic principles are incorporated into their modern operations.

Community Connection

Interview a kaumātua or local Māori business owner about how Māori values influence their economic decisions. Prepare questions about balancing cultural values with financial realities.

Creative Response

Design a financial literacy pamphlet for Māori youth that incorporates traditional economic principles. Consider visual elements that reflect Māori culture alongside practical financial advice.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge: Students should have basic understanding of financial literacy concepts (budgeting, saving, investing) and introductory knowledge of Māori culture.

Differentiation Strategies:

  • Support: Provide vocabulary lists with Māori terms and definitions
  • Extension: Have students research regional variations in Māori economic practices
  • Engagement: Invite local Māori business owners to speak about their experiences

Assessment Ideas:

  • Reflective journal on personal financial values vs Māori economic principles
  • Case study analysis of Māori business practices
  • Creation of a financial plan incorporating Māori values

Cultural Safety: Ensure Māori students feel comfortable sharing their perspectives without being tokenized. Partner with local iwi or Māori advisors if possible.
