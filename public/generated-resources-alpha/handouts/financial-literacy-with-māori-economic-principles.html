Financial Literacy with Māori Economic Principles

He Whakataukī (Māori Proverb)

"He kai kei aku ringa" - There is food at the end of my hands (with hard work comes reward)

This whakataukī reflects the Māori value of self-reliance and productivity. As we explore financial literacy, we'll consider how traditional Māori economic principles can inform modern financial decision-making.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand key Māori economic concepts and values
  • Compare and contrast Māori and Western economic systems
  • Apply Māori economic principles to modern financial scenarios
  • Develop a personal financial plan incorporating Māori values
  • Analyze the sustainability of different economic approaches

Key Māori Economic Concepts

Term Definition Modern Application
Utu Reciprocity, balance and repayment Considering the social and environmental impacts of financial decisions
Manaakitanga Hospitality, generosity and care for others Budgeting for charitable giving and community support
Kaitiakitanga Guardianship and sustainable resource management Ethical investing and sustainable business practices
Whanaungatanga Relationships, kinship and connection Networking for career opportunities and financial advice

Case Study: The Marae Economy

Traditional marae operate on principles of collective responsibility and reciprocity. Consider how these principles apply to modern financial situations:

  1. How might utu influence wage negotiations in a Māori business?
  2. What would a budget look like that prioritizes manaakitanga?
  3. How can kaitiakitanga guide investment decisions?

Discussion: In small groups, develop a financial plan for a community project that incorporates at least three Māori economic principles.

Assessment Activity

Personal Financial Plan with Māori Values

Create a 12-month financial plan for yourself that:

  • Includes income and expenses
  • Incorporates at least two Māori economic principles
  • Explains how these principles influence your financial decisions
  • Considers both short-term and long-term financial wellbeing

Present your plan as either a written report (500-700 words), infographic, or short video presentation.

Extension Activities

For Advanced Learners

  1. Research a contemporary Māori business and analyze how traditional economic principles are applied in modern contexts.
  2. Develop a business plan for a social enterprise that embodies Māori economic values.
  3. Compare the Māori concept of wealth (taonga) with Western economic measures of wealth.
  4. Investigate how kaitiakitanga principles are being applied in sustainable finance initiatives.

Adaptations for Diverse Learners

For Students Needing Additional Support

  • Provide a template for the financial plan with guided sections
  • Offer a glossary of key terms in both English and te reo Māori
  • Allow verbal explanations instead of written responses where appropriate
  • Use concrete examples from students' own experiences

For English Language Learners

  • Provide bilingual vocabulary lists
  • Use visual aids to explain concepts
  • Allow use of first language in initial brainstorming
  • Provide sentence starters for written responses
