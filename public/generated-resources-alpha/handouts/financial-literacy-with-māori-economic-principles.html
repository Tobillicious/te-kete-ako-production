By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

  • Explain key Māori economic concepts and their contemporary relevance

  • Analyze how traditional Māori economic practices can inform modern financial literacy
  • Develop a personal financial plan incorporating Māori economic values

Cultural Context: Māori Economic Systems

Traditional Māori economics was based on principles of reciprocity, sustainability, and collective wellbeing rather than individual profit maximization. Key concepts include:

Key Māori Economic Concepts

  • Utu - The principle of balance and reciprocity
  • Koha - The practice of reciprocal gift-giving
  • Manaakitanga - Hospitality and care for others
  • Kaitiakitanga - Guardianship of resources
  • Whanaungatanga - Relationship-based economics
  • Taonga tuku iho - Intergenerational wealth transfer

Teacher Note: Before teaching this content, consult with local Māori experts or kaumātua to ensure appropriate cultural protocols are followed. Consider the diversity of iwi economic practices across Aotearoa.

Comparing Economic Systems

Interactive Comparison

Māori Economic Principles

  • Collective ownership and decision-making
  • Wealth measured in relationships and sustainability
  • Reciprocal exchange systems (koha, utu)
  • Long-term intergenerational focus
  • Resources viewed as taonga (treasures) to be protected
  • Economic activity embedded in cultural practices

Western Economic Principles

  • Individual ownership and property rights
  • Wealth measured in monetary terms
  • Market-based exchange systems
  • Short to medium-term profit focus
  • Resources viewed as factors of production
  • Economic activity often separated from cultural context

Applying Māori Economic Principles Today

Case Study: Modern Māori Business

Read this scenario and answer the questions that follow:

"Te Rūnanga o Ngāi Tahu manages significant assets on behalf of their iwi. Their investment strategy balances financial returns with cultural and environmental responsibilities. They prioritize investments that align with their values of kaitiakitanga and whanaungatanga."

  1. How might Ngāi Tahu's approach differ from a conventional investment fund?
  2. What advantages might this approach have in the long term?
  3. How could these principles be applied to your personal finances?

Assessment: Personal Financial Plan

Create a personal financial plan that incorporates both conventional financial literacy principles and Māori economic values. Your plan should cover:

Component Conventional Approach Māori Principles Approach
Income Salary, wages, investments Koha, collective resources, skills exchange
Saving Bank accounts, term deposits Taonga tuku iho, intergenerational wealth
Spending Budgeting, needs vs wants Utu, reciprocal relationships
Investing Stocks, bonds, property Kaitiakitanga, sustainable investments
