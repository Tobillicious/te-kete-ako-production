Financial Literacy with Māori Economic Principles

Years 11-13 | Social Sciences | NZ Curriculum Levels 6-8

Learning Objectives

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

  • Explain key Māori economic concepts and values
  • Compare traditional Māori and contemporary economic systems
  • Apply principles of manaakitanga (hospitality) and kaitiakitanga (guardianship) to financial decision-making
  • Analyze how Māori economic principles can inform sustainable financial practices
  • Develop a personal financial plan incorporating Māori values

Match each Māori economic principle with its correct definition:

Term Definition
Utu Reciprocity - maintaining balance in exchanges
Manaakitanga Generosity and hospitality - enhancing others' mana
Kaitiakitanga Guardianship - sustainable resource management
Whanaungatanga Relationship building - economic connections
Tikanga Correct procedures - economic protocols

Tikanga Considerations: When discussing Māori economic systems, remember these protocols:

  • Respect the mana (prestige) of traditional knowledge
  • Understand economic decisions were made collectively by hapū (sub-tribe)
  • Recognize that resources were viewed as taonga (treasures) to be protected

Applying Māori Economic Principles

Case Study: Sustainable Investment

Read this scenario and answer the questions:

"A Māori land trust must decide between leasing land to a dairy corporation (high financial return but environmental impacts) or developing an organic community garden (lower financial return but sustainable and benefits community)."

Decision-Making Framework:

  1. How would kaitiakitanga influence this decision?
  2. What utu (balance) needs to be considered?
  3. How might manaakitanga apply to workers' conditions?
  4. What intergenerational impacts (whakapapa) should be considered?

Extension Activities

Personal Financial Plan

Create a personal financial plan incorporating Māori economic principles:

Include kaitiakitanga in spending choices (eco-friendly products)
Apply manaakitanga in budgeting (charitable giving)
Consider whanaungatanga in income (supporting Māori businesses)
Implement utu in savings (balance between present/future needs)

Research Project

Investigate a contemporary Māori business or organization and analyze how it blends traditional economic principles with modern practices.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge

Students should have basic understanding of:

  • Fundamental economic concepts (supply/demand, profit, etc.)
  • Key aspects of Māori culture (concepts of mana, tapu, noa)
  • Basic financial literacy (budgeting, saving, investing)

Differentiation Strategies

  • Support: Provide vocabulary lists with definitions, use graphic organizers for comparisons
  • Extension: Have students research specific iwi economic models (e.g., Tainui's economic development)

Assessment Options

  • Written report comparing economic systems
  • Presentation on applying Māori principles to a financial case study
  • Creation of a financial plan with Māori values integration

Cultural Safety

  • Consult with local Māori experts or kaumatua where possible
  • Use correct pronunciation of Māori terms (provide audio if needed)
  • Acknowledge regional variations in Māori economic practices
