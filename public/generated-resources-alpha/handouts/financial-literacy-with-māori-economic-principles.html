Financial Literacy with Māori Economic Principles

Year Levels: 11-13 | Subject: Social Sciences

Learning Objectives:

  • Understand key Māori economic concepts and values
  • Compare traditional Māori and contemporary economic systems
  • Apply Māori economic principles to personal financial decisions
  • Analyze how Māori values influence economic behavior
  • Develop culturally responsive financial strategies

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori in Economics

Traditional Māori economic systems were based on principles that differ significantly from Western capitalist models. Key concepts include:

  • Utu: The concept of reciprocity and balance in exchanges
  • Manaakitanga: Hospitality and generosity as economic practices
  • Kaitiakitanga: Guardianship and sustainable resource management
  • Whanaungatanga: Economic activities strengthening relationships
  • Tapu and Noa: Spiritual dimensions affecting economic transactions

Tikanga Considerations: When discussing Māori economic systems, remember that these concepts are living practices, not just historical artifacts. Approach with respect for contemporary Māori economic initiatives.

Traditional Māori Economic Features:

  • Resource allocation based on whakapapa connections
  • Wealth measured in taonga (treasures) and relationships
  • Reciprocal gift exchange (koha) rather than fixed prices
  • Collective ownership and decision-making
  • Production focused on immediate needs and ceremonial purposes

Modern Market Economic Features:

  • Resource allocation through price mechanisms
  • Wealth measured in monetary terms
  • Fixed prices determined by supply and demand
  • Private ownership and individual decision-making
  • Production focused on profit and accumulation

Case Study: Applying Māori Economic Values

Scenario: Your whānau has come into ownership of a small piece of whenua (land). Consider how different economic approaches might guide its use:

Economic Principle Traditional Māori Approach Modern Market Approach
Ownership Collective ownership by whānau/hapū Individual/family ownership
Land Use Mixed use for food, resources, and cultural purposes Specialized use for maximum profit
Decision-making Consensus through hui Owner decides independently
Profit Distribution Shared according to need and contribution Kept by owner or shareholders

Discussion Questions:

  1. What are the advantages and disadvantages of each approach?
  2. How might a blended approach work in contemporary Aotearoa?
  3. What cultural values are preserved or lost in each model?

Extension Activities

1. Research Contemporary Māori Businesses

Investigate how modern Māori businesses (e.g., Miraka, Kono, Ngāi Tahu Holdings) blend traditional values with contemporary business practices.

2. Design a Social Enterprise

Create a business plan for a social enterprise that uses Māori economic principles to address a community need.

3. Interview Community Members

Speak with kaumātua or local Māori business owners about how they incorporate Māori values in their economic activities.

Teacher Implementation Notes

Prior Knowledge: Students should have basic understanding of financial literacy concepts and some familiarity with Māori culture.

Suggested Timeframe: 4-6 weeks (adjust based on depth of study)

Differentiation Strategies:

  • Provide vocabulary lists with Māori economic terms
  • Offer case studies at varying complexity levels
  • Allow choice in assessment format (written, oral, multimedia)

Cultural Safety: Consult with local Māori community members or cultural advisors when possible. Be prepared to address potential conflicts between cultural values and mainstream economic practices.
