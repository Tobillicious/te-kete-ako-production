Digital Equity and Accessibility

Chromebook-Optimized Learning Guide for Years 7-10

Learning Objectives

By the end of this unit, students will be able to:

  • Define digital equity and explain its importance in Aotearoa
  • Identify barriers to digital access in different communities
  • Apply accessibility features of Chromebooks to support diverse learners
  • Analyze how digital technologies can both support and challenge whanaungatanga (relationships)
  • Create solutions to improve digital accessibility in their community

Cultural Context: Tikanga Māori and Digital Equity

The concept of manaakitanga (care for others) is central to digital equity. When we ensure all people have equal access to technology, we uphold their mana (dignity) and support kotahitanga (unity).

Consider these perspectives:

  • Many kura kaupapa Māori (Māori immersion schools) face greater challenges accessing digital resources
  • Traditional marae protocols may need adapting for digital spaces while maintaining cultural integrity
  • The digital divide often affects whānau in rural areas more severely

Whakaaro: Cultural Reflection

How might digital technologies support or challenge these Māori concepts?

  • Whakapapa (genealogy) - Can digital tools help preserve and share whakapapa?
  • Kaitiakitanga (guardianship) - How do we care for digital spaces as we do for the natural world?
  • Wānanga (learning spaces) - What makes an online space effective for Māori ways of learning?

Understanding Digital Equity

Digital equity means all individuals and communities have the information technology capacity needed for full participation in our society.

Activity: Barriers to Access

In small groups, research and list different barriers to digital access in Aotearoa. Consider:

  • Geographic location
  • Socioeconomic factors
  • Language and cultural needs
  • Physical and learning disabilities

Click for Barrier Examples

  • Rural broadband access
  • Cost of devices and data
  • English-language dominated content
  • Lack of te reo Māori interfaces
  • Small text sizes for visually impaired

Chromebook Accessibility Features

Chromebooks have built-in tools to support diverse learners. Explore these features:

Feature How to Access Who It Helps
Screen Reader (ChromeVox) Ctrl + Alt + Z Visually impaired users
High Contrast Mode Settings > Accessibility Users with low vision
On-Screen Keyboard Settings > Accessibility Users with mobility challenges
Dictation Press Search + D Users with writing difficulties

Activity: Accessibility Challenge

Complete these tasks using only accessibility features:

  1. Write a sentence using dictation
  2. Navigate a webpage using only keyboard shortcuts
  3. Read a paragraph with ChromeVox
  4. Change your display to high contrast mode

Extension Activities

Choose one to explore further:

  • Research the Hapori Matihiko (Digital Communities) initiative and its impact
  • Interview a community member about their digital access challenges
  • Design an app interface that incorporates both English and te reo Māori
  • Create a poster teaching others about Chromebook accessibility features
  • Develop a proposal to improve digital access at your school

Teacher Implementation Notes

Curriculum Links: This resource addresses the Digital Technologies learning area (Computational Thinking for Digital Technologies and Designing and Developing Digital Outcomes), as well as key competencies like Participating and Contributing.

Duration: 3-5 lessons depending on depth of exploration.

Differentiation:

  • Support: Provide pre-selected resources and structured templates
  • Extension: Challenge students to test accessibility features on other devices
  • Cultural: Invite local kaumatua or digital experts to share perspectives

Assessment: Use the rubric to evaluate student understanding. Consider peer assessment for the accessibility challenge activities.
